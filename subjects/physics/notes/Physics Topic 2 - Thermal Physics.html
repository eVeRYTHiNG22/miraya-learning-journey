Particles, Heat & Temperature

Thermal physics, built around one mental model: everything is tiny particles, and heat is their hidden motion. Get that, and expansion, heat capacity, latent heat and every transfer method fall out of it. Tap the panels — the particles move.

01 / 10 · 2.1.1–2.1.2 Kinetic Particle Model

The one idea behind everything: matter is particles

Every solid, liquid and gas is made of enormous numbers of tiny particles in constant motion. You can't see them, but their behaviour explains the entire topic. The difference between ice, water and steam is not the particles — they're the same H₂O — it's how much energy those particles have and how freely they can move.

💡 The whole topic in one sentence
Heat is just particle movement you can't see. Add energy → particles move faster and push further apart. That single idea explains why things expand when heated, why gases push on their containers, and why a kettle stays at 100 °C while it boils. Hold onto it.

🕺 The dance-floor analogy
A solid is people standing in a tight grid, swaying on the spot — no one moves seats. A liquid is a packed dance floor: everyone's touching, but you can squeeze past to the bar. A gas is a few people sprinting around an empty hall, bouncing off the walls. Same people, three energies.
⚠️ Common wrong intuition
"Gas particles repel each other, that's why they spread out." No — in the IGCSE model, gas particles barely interact at all; they spread out simply because nothing holds them back and they're moving fast in random directions. Forces between gas particles are treated as negligible.
02 / 10 · 2.1.2 Temperature & Particle Energy

What temperature actually is

Temperature is not "amount of heat". Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles. Hotter means the particles are jiggling, sliding or flying around faster, on average.

Internal energy — the total energy of all the particles in an object: the kinetic energy of their motion plus the potential energy stored in the forces (bonds) between them. Heating an object increases its internal energy.
🔥 Temperature vs. energy — the bathtub trick
A sparkler at 1000 °C and a bathtub of warm water at 40 °C: the sparkler is far hotter (its particles are faster), but the bath holds far more thermal energy overall, because it has vastly more particles. High temperature ≠ lots of energy. This is exactly why a tiny spark doesn't burn you but a bath can warm a room.
⚠️ Mark-winning one-liner
"Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles." When you heat something and its temperature rises, you are saying its particles are moving faster on average.
03 / 10 · 2.1.3 Gases & the Absolute Scale

Gas pressure, and why physicists count from −273 °C

A gas pushes outward on its container. Why? Its particles are constantly colliding with the walls, and every collision is a tiny push. Billions per second add up to a steady pressure.

🏓 Pressure = a hail of tiny impacts
Picture popcorn popping inside a sealed pan, hammering the lid from below. More pops per second, or harder pops, means more force on the lid. Gas pressure is exactly that: the combined drumming of particles on the walls. Anything that makes them hit more often or harder raises the pressure.

The two ways to change gas pressure

Heat it (volume fixed)
More energy → faster particles → they hit the walls harder and more often → pressure rises. So pressure increases with temperature: p ∝ T (T in kelvin).
Squeeze it (temperature fixed)
Smaller volume → particles reach the walls sooner → more collisions per second → pressure rises. So pV = constant (Boyle's law).
Boyle's Law (Extended) — at constant temperature, pV = constant, so p₁V₁ = p₂V₂. Halve the volume and you double the pressure. The graph of p against V is a curve; p against 1/V is a straight line.

The Kelvin (absolute) temperature scale

Cool a gas and its particles slow down. Keep cooling and there must be a point where motion is at its absolute minimum. That point is −273 °C = absolute zero = 0 K. You cannot get colder, because particles can't have less than zero motion.

Kelvin conversion — T (in K) = θ (in °C) + 273. A change of 1 °C equals a change of 1 K — the scale is just shifted, not stretched. Always use kelvin in gas-law calculations.
⚠️ The trap that costs marks
In any pressure–temperature gas calculation, temperature must be in kelvin. Doubling from 20 °C to 40 °C does not double the pressure — in kelvin that's 293 K → 313 K, a rise of only ~7%. Convert first, every time.
04 / 10 · 2.2.1 Thermal Expansion

Why things grow when you heat them

Heat almost anything and it expands; cool it and it contracts. The particle model explains it instantly: hotter particles vibrate (or move) more vigorously, so on average they push each other slightly further apart — the object takes up more space.

↔️ Same particles, bigger gaps
Expansion is not the particles themselves getting bigger. It's the average spacing between them increasing because they're swinging through a wider arc. The number of particles never changes — only the room they need.

Gases expand most, liquids less, solids least — because the freer the particles, the more the extra energy spreads them out:

Gases — most
Particles already free and fast; a little heat sends them much further apart.
Liquids — medium
Used in thermometers: the liquid rises up a thin tube as it expands.
Solids — least
Strong forces resist; expansion is small but powerful enough to matter.
✅ Real-world applications to quote
Expansion gaps in bridges and railway tracks stop them buckling in summer. Overhead power cables are hung with slack so they don't snap when they contract in winter. Liquid-in-glass thermometers work because liquids expand more than the glass around them.
⚠️ The famous exception
Water is unusual: between 0 °C and 4 °C it expands as it cools, and ice is less dense than liquid water — which is why ice floats and pipes burst in winter. (You won't be asked to explain this in detail, but don't claim "everything contracts when cooled".)
05 / 10 · 2.2.2 Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity — why some things heat up slowly

Pour the same energy into a kilogram of water and a kilogram of iron, and the iron gets far hotter. Water needs a lot of energy to change its temperature. That "stubbornness" is measured by the specific heat capacity.

Specific heat capacity, c — the energy needed to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 °C: E = m·c·Δθ, units J/(kg·°C). Water's is huge: 4200 J/(kg·°C).

✅ Why this matters in the real world
Water's enormous c is why it's used in central-heating radiators and car coolant (it carries lots of energy), and why coastal towns have milder weather than inland ones — the sea soaks up summer heat and releases it slowly in winter. Method note: measure c by heating a known mass with an electrical heater, timing it, and using energy = power × time.
06 / 10 · 2.2.3 Changes of State & Latent Heat

Melting, boiling, and the energy that hides

Keep heating a block of ice and something strange happens: while it melts, the thermometer stops rising even though you're still adding energy. Where does the energy go? Into breaking the bonds between particles, not speeding them up. That hidden energy is latent heat.

🧊 Why the temperature pauses while it melts
During a change of state, every joule you add goes into pulling particles apart against their bonds, not into making them move faster. Average kinetic energy stays the same → temperature stays the same. The thermometer only climbs again once the change is complete.
Specific latent heat, L — the energy needed to change the state of 1 kg without any temperature change: E = m·L. Fusion (solid↔liquid) and vaporisation (liquid↔gas) have different L values; vaporisation needs far more.

Boiling vs. evaporation — not the same thing

Boiling
Happens at one fixed temperature (the boiling point), throughout the whole liquid, with bubbles. Energy supplied from outside.
Evaporation
Happens at any temperature, only at the surface, with no bubbles. The fastest surface particles escape.
💧 Why evaporation cools — the marathon effect
Only the fastest particles have enough energy to escape from the surface. When they leave, the ones left behind are the slower ones — so the average kinetic energy of what remains drops, and the liquid cools. It's like the fastest runners leaving a group: the average speed of those left falls. This is exactly why sweating cools you down.
✅ Faster evaporation — three levers
Evaporation speeds up with higher temperature, larger surface area, and more draught / wind (which sweeps escaped particles away). That's why wet washing dries fastest on a warm, breezy day spread out wide.
07 / 10 · 2.3.1 Thermal Energy Transfer — Conduction

Conduction — heat passed particle to particle

Hold a metal spoon in hot soup and the handle soon warms, even though you never dipped it. That's conduction: thermal energy passing along a material without the material itself flowing. It's the main way heat moves through solids.

🔗 Hand it down the line
Heat one end of a solid: those particles vibrate harder and knock into their neighbours, passing energy along like a bucket chain. In metals there's a turbo boost — free electrons zip through the structure carrying energy quickly, which is why metals are the best conductors by far.
Good conductors
Metals (copper, aluminium) — thanks to free electrons. Used for saucepans, heat sinks, radiators.
Insulators (poor conductors)
Air, plastic, wood, wool — no free electrons to carry energy, and in gases the particles are too far apart to pass it on. Used for handles, clothing, lagging.
✅ Why trapped air is the secret
Most insulators work by trapping air (a poor conductor) in small pockets so it can't move: wool, foam, double glazing, fibreglass loft lagging. Liquids and gases are generally poor conductors because their particles are far apart.
08 / 10 · 2.3.2 Thermal Energy Transfer — Convection

Convection — hot fluid floats, cool fluid sinks

Convection moves heat through fluids (liquids and gases) by the fluid itself flowing. It cannot happen in solids — the particles can't travel.

Convection current — heat a fluid and it expands, becoming less dense, so it rises. Cooler, denser fluid sinks to take its place, gets heated in turn, and rises too. This circulating loop carries thermal energy through the whole fluid.
🎈 It's really just floating
Hot fluid rises for the exact same reason a hot-air balloon rises: heating spreads the particles out, so the same mass takes up more volume → lower density → it floats up through the denser cool fluid around it. "Hot rises" is just "less dense floats" wearing a different hat.
✅ Everyday convection to quote
Radiators are placed low so warm air rises and circulates a whole room. Sea breezes: land heats faster than sea by day, warm air over land rises, cool air flows in off the sea. The freezer compartment sits at the top of a fridge so cold, dense air sinks down through the food below.
09 / 10 · 2.3.3 Thermal Energy Transfer — Radiation

Radiation — heat that needs nothing to travel through

The Sun's warmth reaches us across empty space, where there are no particles to conduct or convect. It arrives as infrared radiation — a type of electromagnetic wave. Every object emits it; the hotter the object, the more it pours out.

🌞 No particles required
Conduction and convection both need matter to pass energy along. Radiation doesn't — it's a wave, so it crosses a vacuum. That's the one thing that makes radiation special, and it's the favourite exam question: how does heat get from the Sun to Earth? Answer: radiation, because space is empty.
Emitting & absorbing — a surface that is matt (dull) black is the best emitter and the best absorber of infrared radiation. A shiny, white or silvery surface is the worst emitter and absorber — it reflects radiation instead.

What increases the radiation emitted?

Higher temperature
A hotter surface emits more infrared per second — and emits it faster than a cooler one.
Larger / darker surface
Bigger surface area and a dull-black colour both increase the rate of emission.
✅ Applications that test all three
Houses painted white in hot countries reflect radiation and stay cool. Shiny emergency "space" blankets (used in disaster relief) reflect body radiation back in. A vacuum flask beats all three transfers at once: a vacuum stops conduction & convection, and silvered walls stop radiation — so your drink stays hot for hours.
⚠️ Don't mix the three up
Conduction = through solids, particle to particle. Convection = fluids flowing in currents. Radiation = waves, no medium needed. If a question mentions a vacuum or space, the answer can only be radiation.
