Waves, Light & Sound

One idea runs through the whole topic: a wave carries energy from place to place without carrying the stuff it travels through. Drag the blue panels — the wave, the bending ray, and the lens all move.

01 / 09 · 3.1 General Properties

What a wave is — energy on the move

Flick one end of a long rope and a pulse races to the far end — but the rope itself ends up exactly where it started. That is the whole idea: a wave transfers energy without transferring matter. The particles just vibrate about a fixed spot; only the disturbance travels.

🌊 The stadium-wave picture
A "Mexican wave" sweeps right around a cricket stadium, yet not one person moves seats — each only stands up and sits down. The wave travels; the people stay put. Every wave in this topic works exactly like that: motion is passed along, the medium is not.

The anatomy of a wave

Every wave is described by the same handful of terms. Here is one frozen in place:

TermMeaning
WavefrontA line joining all points vibrating in step (e.g. the crest of a water ripple)
Wavelength, λDistance between two neighbouring points in step — crest to crest (metres)
AmplitudeMaximum distance a point moves from its rest position — sets how much energy the wave carries
Frequency, fNumber of complete waves passing a point each second (hertz, Hz)
Wave speed, vHow fast a crest travels through the medium (m/s)
The wave equation  →  v = f λ. In one second, f whole waves leave the source, each λ long — so the front edge advances f × λ metres. That product is the speed.

See it move: speed = frequency × wavelength

Change the wavelength and frequency and watch the speed follow. Switch between a transverse wave (vibration across the travel direction) and a longitudinal one (vibration along it):

⚠️ Don't mix these up
Bigger amplitude = more energy (louder sound / brighter light). Bigger frequency = more waves per second (higher pitch / "bluer" light). They are independent — you can change one without the other.
02 / 09 · 3.1 Wave Behaviour

What waves do: reflect, refract, diffract

Send water ripples across a ripple tank and you can watch every wave behaviour in slow motion. All three happen to light and sound too — they are properties of waves, not of any one kind:

BehaviourWhat happensCause
ReflectionBounces off a plane barrier; angle in = angle outBarrier in the path
RefractionChanges direction when it speeds up or slows downA change of speed (in the tank, a change of water depth)
DiffractionSpreads out after passing through a gap or round an edgeA gap or obstacle comparable to λ
💧 The ripple-tank trick for refraction
Put a flat sheet of glass under part of the tank to make that region shallower. Waves slow down in shallow water, so they bunch up (shorter λ) and bend — exactly how a glass block bends light. Same physics, different wave.

Diffraction — how much do waves spread? (Extended)

How much a wave fans out through a gap depends on how the gap size compares with the wavelength:

The diffraction rules — a wave spreads out most when the gap is about the same size as its wavelength. A gap much wider than λ lets the wave pass nearly straight (only the edges curl). And the longer the wavelength, the more it diffracts — at both a gap and an edge.
📻 Why radio reaches behind a hill but light doesn't
Long-wavelength radio waves (metres long) diffract around hills and buildings, so the signal reaches you in the valley. Light's wavelength is under a millionth of a metre — far too small to bend round a hill — so shadows have sharp edges. Same wave behaviour, wildly different λ.
03 / 09 · 3.2.1 Reflection

Reflection — the mirror law

All angles in optics are measured from the normal — the line drawn at 90° to the surface where the ray hits. Measuring from the mirror instead is the classic mark-loser.

Law of reflection — the angle of incidence = angle of reflection (both measured from the normal). The incident ray, reflected ray and normal all lie in the same plane.

The image in a plane mirror

Look in a flat mirror and your reflection has a fixed, predictable set of properties — worth memorising as a list:

Same size as the object
Same distance behind the mirror
Virtual (can't be caught on a screen)
Upright, but laterally inverted
⚠️ "Laterally inverted", not upside-down
A mirror swaps left and right, not top and bottom — that's why text held to a mirror reads backwards but isn't flipped over. The image is the same way up.
✅ Drawing a mirror image (Extended)
To locate the image: draw it the same perpendicular distance behind the mirror as the object is in front. Rays from the object reflect (i = r) so that they appear to diverge from that point behind the mirror — which is why it's virtual.
04 / 09 · 3.2.2 Refraction

Refraction & total internal reflection

Light changes speed when it crosses into a different material — slower in glass or water than in air. If it hits the boundary at an angle, that speed change makes it bend. You can show this in the lab by shining a ray-box beam into a rectangular or semicircular glass block and tracing the ray as it enters and leaves. The trick to getting the direction right:

First — what "rarer" and "denser" mean — a rarer medium (like air) is one light travels fast in; a denser medium (like glass or water) is one light travels slower in. "Denser" here means optically denser — it's about the speed of light in the material, not its weight — so air is the rarer medium and glass the denser one. Every refraction is just one of two journeys: rarer → denser or denser → rarer.
🚗 Why it bends — the car-onto-sand picture
Roll a toy car at an angle off a smooth road onto soft sand. The wheel that reaches the sand first slows down while the other wheel is still racing on the road — so the car slews round towards the normal. That's rarer → denser: the wave slows and turns towards the normal.

Now play it in reverse — the car rolls out of the sand back onto the road. The wheel that reaches the road first speeds up first, swinging the car away from the normal. That's denser → rarer: the wave speeds up and turns away. The ray only bends because one side of the wave changes speed before the other.

You can watch the same thing happen to the wavefronts. Crossing from the rarer into the denser medium they slow, bunch closer together (shorter λ) and swing towards the normal:

Blue ticks = wavefronts (crests); the arrowed rays run at right angles to them. Notice the ticks crowd together and turn towards the normal in the denser glass.
⚠️ Which way does it bend? — the two journeys
Rarer → denser (air → glass / water): light slows down → bends towards the normal, so r < i.
Denser → rarer (glass / water → air): light speeds up → bends away from the normal, so r > i.
One line for all of it: slow down → bend towards; speed up → bend away. And a ray travelling straight along the normal (i = 0°) still changes speed but does not bend at all.

What you're seeing
Rarer → denser: light passes from air into optically denser glass, slows, and bends towards the normal (r < i). The refractive index n = 1.5 fixes how much.
Refractive index, n (Extended) — how strongly a material bends light, defined two equivalent ways: n = sin i / sin r  and as a ratio of speeds  n = v₁ / v₂ (speed in the rarer / less-dense medium ÷ speed in the denser medium). For glass n ≈ 1.5.

Total internal reflection & the critical angle

Going from glass to air, the ray bends away from the normal — so as you increase the angle, the refracted ray flattens toward the surface. At one special angle, the critical angle c, it refracts along the boundary. Beyond it, none escapes — it all reflects back inside.

Critical angle (Extended) — at the critical angle the angle of refraction is 90°. This links straight to the index: n = 1 / sin c. For glass (n = 1.5), c ≈ 41.8°. Above c you get total internal reflection (TIR).
🔦 Optical fibres — light in a pipe
A glass fibre is so thin that light always strikes its wall above the critical angle, so it totally-internally-reflects thousands of times and zig-zags along the fibre, losing almost nothing. This is how telecommunications (broadband, cable TV) and medical endoscopes carry signals and images down a bendy strand of glass.
05 / 09 · 3.2.3 Thin Lenses

Thin lenses — bending light to a focus

A converging (convex) lens is thickest in the middle; it refracts a parallel beam to meet at the principal focus, F. A diverging (concave) lens is thinnest in the middle; it spreads a parallel beam so it appears to come from F.

Converging (convex)
Fat middle. Brings parallel rays together at a real focus. Used in cameras, projectors, magnifying glasses, the eye.
Diverging (concave)
Thin middle. Spreads parallel rays apart; they seem to come from a focus behind. Used to correct short sight.
Key terms — the principal axis is the central line through the lens; the focal length, f is the distance from the lens to the principal focus F. A shorter focal length = a more powerful, more strongly bending lens.

🔍 What makes an image "virtual"
A real image is where light rays actually meet — you can catch it on a screen (a projector's picture). A virtual image is where rays only appear to come from when you trace them backwards (the dashed lines); no light is really there, so it can't land on a screen — but your eye still sees it, as with a magnifying glass.
✅ Correcting sight (Extended)
Short sight (can't see far) — the eye over-focuses, so use a diverging (concave) lens to spread rays out first. Long sight (can't see near) — the eye under-focuses, so use a converging (convex) lens to help bring rays together. A single converging lens held close also acts as a magnifying glass (object inside F).
06 / 09 · 3.2.4 Dispersion

Dispersion — splitting white light

White light is a mixture of every colour. A glass prism refracts each colour by a slightly different amount — violet bends most, red least — so they fan out into the visible spectrum. That is dispersion, and it's why you see a rainbow when sunlight passes through raindrops.

The visible spectrum — the traditional seven colours, in order: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet. Red has the longest wavelength / lowest frequency; violet the shortest wavelength / highest frequency.
⚠️ Memorise the order
R O Y G B I V — "Richard Of York Gave Battle In Vain." Going red → violet, frequency increases and wavelength decreases. Light of a single frequency (one pure colour) is called monochromatic. (Extended)

The electromagnetic spectrum

📡 Why microwaves for phones, radio for Bluetooth
Mobiles & wi-fi use microwaves — they pass through some walls and need only a short aerial. Bluetooth uses radio waves — they pass through walls but weaken doing so (so it stays short range). Optical fibres use visible light/infrared because glass is transparent to them and they carry huge data rates. Satellite communication is mainly by microwaves (low-orbit phones, geostationary TV). (Extended)
✅ Digital vs analogue (Extended)
An analogue signal varies smoothly; a digital signal is just on/off pulses (0s and 1s). Digital is better because it can be regenerated cleanly (noise stripped off at each repeater), giving greater range, and it carries data at a higher rate. Sound can be sent either way.
08 / 09 · 3.4 Sound

Sound — a longitudinal pressure wave

A vibrating source — a guitar string, a speaker cone, your vocal cords — pushes and pulls the air next to it. Those pushes travel outward as a longitudinal wave: regions where the air is squashed (compressions) and stretched (rarefactions) move away from the source.

Sound needs a medium — solid, liquid or gas. With no particles to pass the vibration along, sound cannot travel: a ringing bell in a vacuum jar falls silent as the air is pumped out. (Light, being electromagnetic, still gets through.)
Audible range
Humans hear roughly 20 Hz to 20 000 Hz (20 kHz). Sound above 20 kHz is ultrasound.
Speed of sound
In air, about 330–350 m/s. Faster in liquids, faster still in solids — particles are closer, so they pass the vibration on quicker. (solids > liquids > gases — Extended)
🔊 Loudness vs pitch — two different dials
Amplitude → loudness: bigger vibrations push the air harder, so the sound is louder. Frequency → pitch: more vibrations per second sound higher. A bass drum is loud and low (big amplitude, low frequency); a whistle is high (high frequency). They are independent.

Measuring the speed of sound

A direct method — stand a measured distance d from a large wall, clap, and time t from clap to echo. The sound travels 2d (there and back), so speed v = 2d / t. An echo is simply the reflection of sound off a hard surface.
✅ Ultrasound & sonar (Extended)
Ultrasound (> 20 kHz) reflects off boundaries, so it's used for non-destructive testing (finding cracks inside metal), medical scanning of soft tissue (e.g. a fetal scan — no harmful radiation), and sonar for depth. Sonar maths: a pulse takes time t for the round trip, so depth = v × t ÷ 2.
🐬 Worked sonar example
A ship sends an ultrasound pulse to the seabed; the echo returns after 0.04 s. Sound travels at ≈ 1500 m/s in seawater. Depth = (1500 × 0.04) ÷ 2 = 30 m. Always halve — the time is for the trip down and back.
