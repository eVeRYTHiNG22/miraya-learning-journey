Day, year, seasons & the Moon

Three motions set the rhythms of life on Earth — and the exam wants you to link each one to what we observe.

🌞 Why the Sun "moves" but doesn't
The Sun isn't crossing the sky — you are riding a spinning Earth. From Mumbai the Sun rises in the east because the ground is rotating eastward toward it. One full spin ≈ 24 hours gives one day–night cycle.
⚠️ The seasons myth
Seasons are NOT caused by the Earth being closer to or further from the Sun. They are caused by the Earth's tilted axis: the hemisphere tilted toward the Sun gets more direct, concentrated sunlight and longer days → summer. (India is actually slightly nearer the Sun during the northern winter — proof distance isn't the cause.)

Orbital speed (Extended)

Average orbital speed — for a roughly circular orbit, the body travels one circumference (2πr) in one period T: v = 2πr / T, where r is the average orbit radius and T the orbital period.
✅ Worked example — Earth's speed around the Sun
r = 1.5 × 10¹¹ m, T = 1 year = 3.15 × 10⁷ s. v = 2π × 1.5×10¹¹ ÷ 3.15×10⁷ ≈ 3.0 × 10⁴ m/s — about 30 km every second. Keep r and T in metres and seconds.
02 / 10 · 6.1.2 The Solar System

What's in the Solar System

The Solar System is held together by the Sun's gravity and contains:

  • One star — the Sun
  • Eight planets — in order from the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune
  • Minor (dwarf) planets — e.g. Pluto, and asteroids in the asteroid belt (between Mars and Jupiter)
  • Moons — natural satellites that orbit planets
  • Smaller bodies — comets and other natural satellites
⚠️ Learn the order (a mnemonic)
My Very Easy Method Just Speeds Up Names → Mercury · Venus · Earth · Mars · Jupiter · Saturn · Uranus · Neptune. The asteroid belt sits between Mars and Jupiter — the boundary between the rocky and gas planets.

Rocky inner four vs. gas-giant outer four

Inner 4 — small & rocky
Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. Close to the Sun, made of rock and metal, relatively small and dense.
Outer 4 — large & gaseous
Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. Far from the Sun, made mostly of gas, much larger and less dense.

Why the split? The accretion model

Accretion — the Solar System formed from a rotating interstellar cloud of gas and dust containing many elements. Gravity pulled material together; the cloud's rotation flattened it into an accretion disc, and matter clumped into the Sun and planets.
☀️ Why rock is near, gas is far
Close to the new Sun it was too hot for ices and gases to survive — only rock and metal could condense, so the inner planets are small and rocky. Far out, where it was cold, planets could hold on to vast amounts of light gas → the giant gas planets.
03 / 10 · 6.1.2 Gravity & Orbits

Gravity holds it all together

The Sun contains almost all the mass of the Solar System. Its gravitational attraction is the force that keeps every planet in orbit — the same kind of pull that gives you weight on Earth.

Gravitational field strength, g — at a planet's surface, g depends on the mass of the planet (more mass → stronger field). Around a planet, g decreases as you move further away. On Earth g ≈ 9.8 N/kg; on the Moon only ≈ 1.6 N/kg.
✅ Extended — orbital speed & distance
Because the Sun's gravity weakens with distance, planets further out move more slowly (Neptune crawls; Mercury races). In an elliptical orbit a single body speeds up when closer to the Sun and slows when further — explained by conservation of energy (it trades gravitational PE for kinetic energy as it falls inward).

Elliptical orbits (Extended)

Planets, minor planets and comets travel in ellipses, not perfect circles, and the Sun is not at the centre of the ellipse (it sits at a focus) — except when an orbit is nearly circular. Comets have very stretched ellipses, so they whip past the Sun fast and then spend years far away, moving slowly.

How long does light take?

Light-travel time — light is fast but not instant. Time = distance ÷ speed, with v = 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s.
✅ Worked example — sunlight to Earth
Sun–Earth distance ≈ 1.5 × 10¹¹ m. Time = 1.5×10¹¹ ÷ 3.0×10⁸ = 500 s ≈ 8.3 minutes. So you always see the Sun as it was ~8 minutes ago.

✅ What to say about the trends
As orbital distance increases: the orbital period (year) increases (further out = slower + longer path), and the surface temperature falls (less radiation reaches the planet). Surface g does not follow a simple distance trend — it depends on the planet's own mass and radius (Jupiter is huge, so its g is the largest).
🔢 Density from the data
You can also work out density if given mass and volume (ρ = m / V from Topic 1): the rocky inner planets come out dense (~5000 kg/m³), the gas giants come out light (Saturn is less dense than water!). The numbers themselves tell the rocky-vs-gas story.
05 / 10 · 6.2.1 The Sun as a Star

Our local star

The Sun — a medium-sized star made mostly of hydrogen and helium. It radiates most of its energy in the infrared, visible light and ultraviolet regions of the electromagnetic spectrum.
Infrared (most of the heat)
Visible light
Ultraviolet
Where the energy comes from (Extended) — stars are powered by nuclear reactions. In a stable star like the Sun, this is the fusion of hydrogen into helium, which releases enormous amounts of energy (the same fusion you met in Topic 5).
⚖️ Why the Sun doesn't collapse or explode
A star is a permanent tug-of-war: gravity pulling inward vs. the outward push of hot, high-pressure gas from fusion. While these balance, the star is stable and stays the same size. That balance is the definition of a main-sequence star.
06 / 10 · 6.2.2 Stars & Galaxies

Galaxies, light-years & the life of a star

Stars cluster into galaxies — each made of many billions of stars. Our Sun is one star in the galaxy called the Milky Way; every other star is far, far further from us than the Sun.

Light-year — the distance light travels through space in one year. 1 light-year = 9.5 × 10¹⁵ m. It is a distance, not a time. We need it because metres are hopeless for measuring between stars.

The life cycle of a star

Every star is born from a cloud of gas and dust — but how it dies depends on its mass. Switch between a Sun-like star and a much more massive one:

Low mass (like the Sun)
High mass (much heavier)
Low-mass star (like the Sun)
⚠️ The two endings to memorise
Low mass (Sun-like): red giant → planetary nebula + white dwarf at its centre.
High mass: red supergiant → supernova → leaving a neutron star or a black hole. The supernova throws out a nebula of hydrogen and new heavier elements, which may form new stars and planets.
♻️ You are made of dead stars
The heavy elements in your body — the iron in your blood, the calcium in your bones — were forged inside massive stars and scattered by supernovae. The Solar System condensed from exactly such enriched nebula material. That is the literal meaning of "we are stardust".
07 / 10 · 6.2.3 The Universe

Redshift & the expanding Universe

The Milky Way is just one of many billions of galaxies in the Universe. Our galaxy is about 100 000 light-years across. When we look at the light from distant galaxies, something striking shows up.

Redshift — light from galaxies that are moving away from us is stretched to a longer wavelength, shifting it toward the red end of the spectrum. The light from distant galaxies is redshifted compared with the same light measured on Earth.

Drag a galaxy further away and watch its dark spectral lines slide toward red — the further it is, the faster it recedes, the bigger the redshift:

What it means
Every distant galaxy is redshifted, and the further ones recede faster. The only sensible explanation: space itself is expanding, carrying the galaxies apart. Rewind that expansion and everything was once together — strong evidence for the Big Bang Theory.
08 / 10 · 6.2.3 Big Bang Evidence (Extended)

The Big Bang, the CMBR & the age of the Universe

Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR) — microwave radiation of a specific frequency, observed from every direction in space. It was produced shortly after the Universe formed; as the Universe expanded, this radiation stretched into the microwave region. It is a second, independent piece of evidence for the Big Bang.

Measuring the expansion: the Hubble constant

Speed & distance of galaxies — a galaxy's recession speed v can be found from the change in wavelength of its starlight (its redshift). Its distance d can be found from the brightness of a supernova in that galaxy.
Hubble constant, H₀ — the ratio of a galaxy's recession speed to its distance: H₀ = v / d. Current estimate: H₀ ≈ 2.2 × 10⁻¹⁸ per second.
✅ The age of the Universe
Rearranging Hubble's relation, d / v = 1 / H₀ gives an estimate of the age of the Universe. This works because, running the expansion backwards, all matter was once together at a single point.
Age ≈ 1 ÷ (2.2×10⁻¹⁸) ≈ 4.5 × 10¹⁷ s ≈ 14 billion years.
⚠️ Units & meaning of H₀
H₀ has units of per second (1/s), so 1/H₀ comes out in seconds — divide by ~3.15×10⁷ to get years. And "1/H₀ = age" is only an estimate that assumes the expansion rate has been steady.
