01 / 11 · 1.1 Physical Quantities

Measuring, and the two flavours of quantity

Physics is built on measurement. You find a length with a ruler, a volume with a measuring cylinder, and a time interval with a clock or digital timer. One trick worth knowing: to measure something tiny — a single sheet of paper, or one swing of a pendulum — measure many and divide. Time 20 swings, divide by 20: the human reaction-time error gets divided by 20 too.

⏱️ Why measure multiples
Your reaction time (~0.2 s) is the same whether you time 1 swing or 20. Spread across 20 swings it becomes 0.01 s per swing — the error shrinks without buying a better stopwatch. This is the cheapest precision upgrade in all of physics.

Scalars vs. vectors

Every quantity is one of two kinds. A scalar has size only. A vector has size and direction — and direction changes everything.

Scalars — size only
distance, speed, time, mass, energy, temperature. "5 kg" is complete. "20 °C" is complete. No arrow needed.
Vectors — size + direction
force, weight, velocity, acceleration, momentum, electric & gravitational field strength. "5 N" is incomplete — 5 N which way?
🧭 Speed vs. velocity, the classic
A car going round a roundabout at a steady 30 km/h has constant speed but changing velocity — because its direction keeps changing. Changing velocity means it is accelerating, even though the speedometer never moves. That single idea unlocks circular motion later.

Adding two perpendicular vectors

When two forces (or velocities) act at right angles, the combined effect — the resultant — is the diagonal of the rectangle they form. Drag the sliders and watch Pythagoras do its job:

02 / 11 · 1.2 Motion

Motion — and the graphs that describe it

Speed is v = s / t — distance per unit time. Velocity is speed in a stated direction. Acceleration is how fast velocity itself changes: a = Δv / Δt, measured in m/s². Near Earth's surface every dropped object speeds up at g ≈ 9.8 m/s².

📈 Read graphs like a sentence
Gradient of distance–time = speed. Steeper line, faster object; flat line, stopped. Gradient of speed–time = acceleration, and the area under speed–time = distance travelled. A curve that gets steeper means speeding up; a curve flattening out means slowing down.
⚠️ The trap examiners love
A horizontal line on a speed–time graph does NOT mean stopped — it means constant speed (steady, non-zero). Only a horizontal line on a distance–time graph means stationary. Mixing these up is the single most common Paper 4 slip.

Falling objects & terminal velocity

Drop something and gravity pulls it down at g. But as it speeds up, air resistance (drag) grows. When drag grows to exactly balance weight, the resultant force is zero, acceleration stops, and the object falls at a steady terminal velocity.

🪂 Skydiver story
Jump out: weight > drag, so you accelerate. Faster you go, bigger the drag, until drag = weight → zero resultant → terminal velocity (~55 m/s). Open the parachute: drag suddenly jumps above weight → you decelerate to a new, gentler terminal velocity (~5 m/s) for a safe landing.
03 / 11 · 1.3 Mass & Weight

Mass vs. weight — not the same thing

Mass — kg (scalar)
The amount of matter in an object. Unchanging — the same on Earth, the Moon, or deep space. Measures how hard it is to change motion (inertia).
Weight — N (vector)
The gravitational force on that mass. Changes with location — you'd weigh ~1/6 as much on the Moon. Found from W = mg.
Gravitational field strength, g — force per unit mass: g = W / m. On Earth g ≈ 9.8 N/kg. This number is numerically identical to the acceleration of free fall (9.8 m/s²) — and that is no coincidence, it's the same physics seen two ways.
⚠️ One-liner that wins marks
"Mass is a measure of the quantity of matter (kg); weight is the gravitational force acting on that mass (N)." Masses and weights are compared using a balance.
04 / 11 · 1.4 Density

Density — how tightly matter is packed

Density is mass per unit volume: ρ = m / V (kg/m³ or g/cm³). It decides whether things float.

⚖️ Float or sink, in one rule
An object floats if it is less dense than the liquid, and sinks if it is more dense. Steel sinks in water (ρ ≈ 7900 > 1000) — yet a steel ship floats, because its overall density (steel + trapped air) is less than water's. Density is about the whole object, not the material alone.

Measuring density

ObjectHow to find volume
Regular solid (cube, sphere)Measure dimensions, calculate geometrically (e.g. l×w×h)
Irregular solid (that sinks)Displacement — drop into a measuring cylinder / eureka can, read the volume of water pushed aside
LiquidMeasure a known volume in a cylinder, weigh it (subtract the empty cylinder's mass)
✅ Extended extra
Two liquids that don't mix will layer by density — the less dense liquid floats on top. Given density data, you can predict the order of layers (e.g. oil on water on syrup).
05 / 11 · 1.5 Forces

Forces — what changes motion and shape

A force is a push or pull. It can change an object's speed, direction, or shape. When forces act along the same line, add them (opposite directions subtract) to get the resultant.

Newton's First Law — an object stays at rest, or keeps moving in a straight line at constant speed, unless acted on by a resultant force. No resultant force ≠ no force; it means the forces are balanced.
Newton's Second Law — F = ma. The resultant force and the acceleration point the same way. Double the force → double the acceleration; double the mass → half the acceleration.

Springs & Hooke's Law

Pull a spring and it stretches in proportion to the force — up to a point. The stiffness is the spring constant: k = F / x (N/m). This holds only up to the limit of proportionality — the point on a load–extension graph where the straight line starts to bend.

⚠️ Limit of proportionality
Below the limit, extension ∝ load (straight line through the origin). Beyond it, the graph curves — equal loads now give bigger extensions. The syllabus wants you to identify this point on a graph; you don't need the elastic limit.

Friction, drag & circular motion

Friction is the force between two surfaces that opposes motion and produces heating. In a fluid it's called drag (in a gas, air resistance). A force acting perpendicular to motion bends the path into a circle — turn the steering wheel harder (more force) and the car curves more tightly (smaller radius).

🎡 Circular motion, qualitatively
Keep mass and radius fixed → more force = faster circle. Keep mass and speed fixed → more force = tighter circle (smaller radius). Heavier object → needs more force to keep the same speed and radius. (You don't need the F = mv²/r formula at IGCSE.)
06 / 11 · 1.5.2–1.5.3 Turning Effects

Moments — the turning effect of a force

A force applied at a distance from a pivot makes things rotate. The size of that turning effect is the moment:

Moment = force × perpendicular distance from the pivot  →  moment = F × d (N·m). Bigger force or longer arm = bigger turn. That's why door handles sit far from the hinge, and why a long spanner loosens a tight bolt.

Centre of gravity & stability

The centre of gravity is the single point where all an object's weight seems to act. Find it for an irregular lamina by hanging it from two points and drawing plumb lines — they cross at the C of G.

🏎️ Why race cars are low & wide
An object is stable when its centre of gravity is low and its base is wide. It topples only when its line of weight falls outside the base. Low + wide = you'd have to tilt it a long way before that happens — exactly why racing cars hug the ground.
07 / 11 · 1.6 Momentum

Momentum — “mass in motion”

Momentum = mass × velocity  →  p = mv (kg·m/s). A vector — it points the way the object moves. A heavy slow lorry and a light fast bullet can carry the same momentum.
Impulse = force × time it acts  →  impulse = FΔt = Δ(mv). And force is the rate of change of momentum: F = Δp / Δt — which is just F = ma in disguise.
🛟 Why airbags and crumple zones work
In a crash your momentum must drop to zero either way. Δp is fixed. But F = Δp/Δt says: stretch the time Δt and the force F drops. Airbags, crumple zones, and bending your knees on landing all do one thing — extend the stopping time to shrink the force on you.

Conservation of momentum

In any collision or explosion with no external forces, total momentum before = total momentum after. This is one of the most powerful tools in the topic — it lets you solve crashes you couldn't otherwise touch.

✅ Worked setup
A 2 kg trolley at 3 m/s hits a stationary 1 kg trolley and they stick. Before: p = 2×3 + 1×0 = 6 kg·m/s. After (combined 3 kg moving at v): 3v = 6 → v = 2 m/s. Momentum conserved, every time.
08 / 11 · 1.7 Energy, Work & Power

Energy — stored, transferred, never lost

Energy is never created or destroyed, only moved between stores and transferred by pathways (forces doing work, electric currents, heating, waves). The seven stores the 0625 syllabus names:

Kinetic (movement)
Gravitational potential
Chemical
Elastic (strain)
Nuclear
Electrostatic
Internal (thermal)
Kinetic energy Eₖ = ½mv² — note the v²: double the speed gives four times the KE (and four times the braking distance).
Gravitational PE change ΔEₚ = mgΔh — lift a mass higher and you store energy in the gravitational field. Drop it, and that store pours into kinetic.
🎢 The rollercoaster trade
At the top of the first hill: maximum GPE, near-zero KE. Plunging down: GPE → KE, so it's fastest at the bottom. Climbing the next hill: KE → GPE again. If there were no friction it would rise to exactly the same height forever. Friction quietly siphons some into thermal each loop — which is why later hills are always lower.

Work & power

Work done = energy transferred = W = Fd (joules). Push with a force F through a distance d in the force's direction — that's the energy you've moved.
Power = work done (or energy transferred) per second = P = W/t = ΔE/t (watts). Two cranes lift the same load up the same height — same work — but the faster one has more power.

✅ Extended must-knows
Radiation from the Sun is the main source of energy for almost everything — except geothermal, nuclear, and tidal. The Sun's own energy comes from nuclear fusion, and research is ongoing into harnessing fusion for large-scale electricity on Earth.

Efficiency

No transfer is perfect — some energy always ends up in less-useful stores (usually thermal, via friction). Efficiency measures how much you actually get out:

25% useful
75% wasted as heat & sound
A typical petrol car engine — energy in = energy out, but only about a quarter becomes useful movement. (A Sankey diagram, drawn to scale.)
Efficiency = (useful output ÷ total input) × 100%. Works with energy or power. It can never exceed 100% — that would break conservation of energy.
10 / 11 · 1.8 Pressure

Pressure — force spread over area

Pressure = force per unit area  →  p = F / A (pascals, Pa = N/m²). Same force, smaller area = bigger pressure. That's why a sharp knife cuts (tiny area → huge pressure) and snowshoes don't sink (big area → tiny pressure).
🌊 Why pressure grows with depth
Dive underwater and your ears hurt more the deeper you go. Each layer of water has the weight of all the water above it pressing down. Pressure under a liquid rises with depth and with the liquid's density.
Liquid pressure (Extended) — the extra pressure at depth h is Δp = ρgΔh. Denser liquid, or deeper down, or stronger gravity → more pressure. Notice area doesn't appear: pressure at a depth is the same regardless of the container's shape.
