Electricity & Magnetism

One idea ties this whole topic together: a moving charge and a magnetic field are two halves of the same coin. Charge, current and voltage build the circuit; magnetism turns current into motion — and motion back into electricity. Tap the panels; they respond.

01 / 12 · 4.1 Simple Phenomena of Magnetism

Magnets, poles & invisible fields

Every magnet has two poles — north (N) and south (S) — and the rule is the mirror of electric charge: like poles repel, unlike poles attract. A magnet also pulls on unmagnetised magnetic materials (it turns them into temporary magnets first — induced magnetism), but the pole-on-pole repulsion test is the only sure way to tell a true magnet from a lump of iron.

🧲 The one true test for a magnet
Attraction is a liar — a magnet attracts iron, but iron also attracts a magnet, and an unmagnetised nail will stick to either pole. Only repulsion proves both objects are magnets, because nothing but a like-pole pushes a pole away.
Magnetic field — a region in which a magnetic pole feels a force. We draw it with field lines that run from N to S outside the magnet. The line's direction is the direction of the force on a tiny N pole placed there; where lines are closer together, the field is stronger.

🛏️ Soft iron is a memory-foam magnet
Press memory foam and it takes the shape instantly, then springs back the moment you let go — that's soft iron: magnetises and demagnetises in a flash, perfect when you want a magnet you can switch off. ⚠️ "Magnetic" ≠ "charged" ≠ "a magnet"
A magnetic material (iron) is something a magnet can attract; it is not itself a magnet until magnetised. And magnetism has nothing to do with electric charge — a charged balloon and a bar magnet are different phenomena (until currents get involved, in Section 10).
✅ Plotting & drawing field lines
Plot a field with iron filings (show the shape) or a small plotting compass (shows shape and direction — the needle's N end points along the line). When you draw: arrows N→S, and field lines never cross (two field directions at one point is impossible). Uses: permanent magnets (compasses, fridge magnets, loudspeakers) keep a steady field; electromagnets (cranes, relays, MRI) give a field you can switch on, off and reverse.
02 / 12 · 4.2.1 Electric Charge

Static electricity — charge that sits still

There are two kinds of electric charge, positive and negative, and they follow the same like-repels / unlike-attracts rule as magnetic poles. But here we know the mechanism exactly: charge is just electrons — tiny negative particles — being in surplus or short supply.

⚡ Only the electrons move
Protons are locked in the nucleus; they never travel. So when you "charge something up," all that happens is electrons move from one object to the other. Gain electrons → negatively charged. Lose electrons → positively charged (left with a surplus of unmoved protons). That single fact explains every static experiment.
🎈 Balloon on your hair
Rub a balloon on your hair: friction scrapes electrons off your hair onto the balloon. The balloon, now electron-rich, is negative; your hair, electron-poor, is positive. Opposite charges attract, so your hair stands up reaching for the balloon. No electron is created — they're just moved.
Charging by friction — rubbing two insulators transfers negative charge (electrons) only, in one direction. The two objects end up with equal and opposite charge. Charge is measured in coulombs (C).

Conductors vs insulators

Conductors — charge flows
Metals (copper, steel) and graphite have free electrons that drift through the material. Touch a charged conductor and the charge escapes through you to earth.
Insulators — charge stays put
Plastic, rubber, glass, dry air: no free electrons. Charge sits where you put it — which is exactly why static builds up on them.
Electric field — a region in which a charge feels a force. Its direction is the direction of the force on a positive charge. Patterns to know: radial around a point charge or a charged sphere (arrows out for +, in for −), and uniform (evenly spaced parallel lines, + plate → − plate) between two oppositely charged parallel plates.
⚠️ Positive objects didn't gain "positives"
A positively charged rod did not collect positive particles — it lost electrons. Always explain charging in terms of electrons moving. Saying "protons transferred" loses the mark every time.
03 / 12 · 4.2.2 Electric Current

Current — charge that keeps flowing

Static charge that sits still isn't much use. Connect a charged-up source (a cell) into a complete loop and the charge flows continuously — that flow is electric current. In a metal wire, the moving charges are the free electrons.

Electric current — the charge passing a point per unit time: I = Q / t. Measured in amperes (A); 1 A = 1 C/s. Rearranged, the charge delivered is Q = I × t. An ammeter (in series) reads it.
💧 Current is a flow rate, not a fuel
Current is how fast charge passes — like litres-per-second past a point in a pipe, not the water itself. A common myth is that current gets "used up" as it goes round. It doesn't: in a single loop the same current flows everywhere. What gets used up is energy (that's voltage's job, next section).

Two directions, one current: the great convention mix-up

Conventional current
The agreed direction for diagrams: from + to − round the external circuit. Chosen ~250 years ago, before electrons were discovered.
Electron flow
What actually moves in a metal: electrons drift the opposite way, from − to +. Same current — just the real carriers.
🔁 Why we never fixed the arrow
Benjamin Franklin guessed the direction of flow and got it backwards. By the time electrons were found, every book, rule and motor diagram used "+ to −". Rather than reprint the world, physicists kept conventional current for all the rules (left-hand rule, field directions). Just remember: electrons go the other way.

d.c. vs a.c.

Direct current (d.c.)
Flows one way only, steady. From cells & batteries. Powers phones, torches, anything with a battery.
Alternating current (a.c.)
Reverses direction back-and-forth, many times a second. The mains supply is a.c. (in India, 50 Hz). Essential for transformers later.
04 / 12 · 4.2.3 e.m.f. & Potential Difference

Voltage — the energy each coulomb carries

If current is the flow, voltage is the push that drives it and the energy it delivers. Voltage always means joules per coulomb — energy per unit charge — and comes in two flavours depending on whether charge is gaining energy or spending it.

Electromotive force (e.m.f.) — the electrical work done by a source in driving unit charge around a complete circuit: E = W / Q. It's the energy the cell gives each coulomb. Measured in volts (V).
Potential difference (p.d.) — the work done by unit charge passing through a component: V = W / Q. It's the energy each coulomb spends in that component. Also measured in volts; 1 V = 1 J/C.
⛲ The fountain model
Picture charge as water in a fountain. The pump lifts every litre to the top — that energy-per-litre is the e.m.f. The water then falls through the wheels and jets, giving its energy up — the energy-per-litre handed to each component is the p.d. Add up every component's p.d. round the loop and you get back exactly the e.m.f.: energy in = energy out.
✅ e.m.f. gives, p.d. takes
Same unit, opposite roles: e.m.f. is energy supplied per coulomb (the cell); p.d. is energy transferred per coulomb (a lamp, resistor). Measure p.d. with a voltmeter in parallel — connected across the component, never in the main line.
⚠️ Voltmeter across, ammeter through
The classic wiring slip: ammeter goes in series (the current must pass through it); voltmeter goes in parallel (it samples the p.d. across two points). Swap them and you short out the component or read nothing.
05 / 12 · 4.2.4 Resistance

Resistance & the shape of I–V graphs

Push charge through any component and it pushes back — that opposition is resistance. The more a component resists, the smaller the current a given voltage can drive through it.

Resistance = p.d. ÷ current: R = V / I, in ohms (Ω). Rearranged, V = I R — the single most-used line in the topic. To measure R: put a voltmeter across the component and an ammeter in series, vary the supply with a variable resistor, and calculate R = V/I (ideally repeat and average).
🚿 What decides a wire's resistance
Think of charge squeezing down a pipe. A longer pipe resists more (R ∝ L); a fatter pipe resists less (R ∝ 1/A). So a long thin wire has high resistance; a short thick wire has low resistance — exactly why thick cables carry big currents without heating up.

Not everything obeys a straight-line rule. Tap the three components below and watch each one's current–voltage (I–V) characteristic draw itself:

Resistor (constant resistance)
Resistance stays constant, so current is directly proportional to voltage — a straight line through the origin (Ohm's law). Reverse the voltage and the line simply continues the other way: it is symmetric.
✅ Read each curve in one line
Resistor: straight line through the origin → constant R (ohmic). Filament lamp: bends over / flattens — as current heats the filament its resistance rises. Diode: conducts one way only; almost no current until ~0.6 V forward, then a steep rise; near-infinite resistance in reverse.
⚠️ "Ohm's law" isn't universal
A metal at constant temperature gives I ∝ V (straight line). A filament lamp gets hot, so it is non-ohmic — its line curves. The gradient of an I–V graph is not resistance; resistance is V/I at a point. For the curved lamp graph, R is different at every point.
06 / 12 · 4.2.5 Electrical Energy & Power

Power, energy & the electricity bill

A circuit's job is to transfer energy — from the cell or mains, into the components, and finally out into the surroundings (as light, heat, sound, movement). Power is how fast that happens.

Electrical power = current × p.d.: P = I V, in watts (W). Over a time t, the energy transferred is E = I V t (joules). Since P = IV, that's just E = P t.
💡 Why P = IV makes sense
Each coulomb hands over V joules (that's what p.d. means), and I coulombs pass every second (that's what current means). Joules-per-coulomb × coulombs-per-second = joules per second = watts. The formula is just the two definitions multiplied together.

Paying for it: the kilowatt-hour

Your electricity bill isn't in joules — a joule is tiny. Instead it uses the kilowatt-hour (kW·h), also called a "unit": the energy a 1 kW appliance uses in 1 hour.

Energy in kW·h = power (in kW) × time (in hours). Then cost = units (kW·h) × price per unit. One unit equals 3.6 MJ (1000 W × 3600 s).
✅ Worked example — a geyser in Mumbai (₹)
A 2 kW water geyser runs 1.5 hours a day.
Energy per day = 2 × 1.5 = 3 kW·h (3 units).
At ₹8 per unit: cost/day = 3 × 8 = ₹24.
Over a 30-day month: 24 × 30 = ₹720 — just for the geyser.

"kW" is power (a rate); "kW·h" is energy (a rate × a time). Don't divide by time again. And convert before you multiply: watts → kilowatts, minutes → hours. A 100 W bulb for 30 min = 0.1 kW × 0.5 h = 0.05 kW·h, not 100 × 30.
07 / 12 · 4.3.1–4.3.2 Series & Parallel Circuits

Series & parallel — the two ways to wire things

Components can be joined in a single loop (series) or on separate branches (parallel). The rules for each follow from two ideas: charge isn't created or destroyed (current), and energy is conserved (voltage).

Series — one path
• Same current everywhere.
• p.d.s add up to the supply.
• Resistances add: R = R₁ + R₂ + …
• Cell e.m.f.s in series add (two 1.5 V cells → 3 V).
Parallel — branches
• Same p.d. across every branch.
• Branch currents add to the main current.
• Combined R: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
• Total R is less than the smallest branch.
Kirchhoff, in plain words (Extended) — Junctions: current in = current out (charge can't pile up). Loops: the p.d.s across components in series add up to the total p.d. across them. These two rules crack almost every circuit question.

Set two resistors and compare wiring them in series vs parallel. Watch how parallel always drops below the smaller resistor:

What you're seeing
Series stacks the resistance up (R₁ + R₂). Parallel opens a second path for the current, so the combined resistance falls below either resistor — more routes, less opposition.

✅ Why home lights are in parallel
Every lamp gets the full mains voltage, each can be switched on its own, and if one bulb blows the rest stay lit (the others' branches are unbroken). Old series fairy-lights showed the downside: one dead bulb killed the whole string.
⚠️ Parallel resistance feels backwards
Adding a resistor in parallel lowers the total resistance (and the answer is always smaller than the smallest branch). Students often add them like series by mistake — always use 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂, then remember to flip back to get R, not 1/R.
08 / 12 · 4.3.3 Action & Use of Components

The potential divider — splitting a voltage

Put two resistors in series across a supply and they share out the voltage. Because the same current flows through both (series!), each one's p.d. is in proportion to its resistance — bigger resistor, bigger share.

Potential divider — for two series resistors, V₁ / V₂ = R₁ / R₂. The output taken across R₂ is V_out = V_in × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂). A variable divider (a potentiometer — slide the contact) gives any output from 0 up to the full supply.
✅ Worked example
A 12 V supply across R₁ = 2 kΩ and R₂ = 4 kΩ in series. The output across R₂:
V_out = 12 × 4 /(2 + 4) = 8 V.
Check the ratio: V₁ : V₂ = R₁ : R₂ = 2 : 4, so V₁ = 4 V and V₂ = 8 V — and 4 + 8 = 12 V. ✓
🌡️ How gadgets "sense" the world
Swap one resistor for a thermistor (resistance falls as it warms) or an LDR (resistance falls in bright light) and the divider's output voltage now tracks temperature or light. A dark room → high LDR resistance → it grabs a bigger share of voltage → that rising voltage switches a street-lamp on. This is the front end of almost every automatic sensor circuit.
⚠️ Which resistor's voltage are you finding?
V_out = V_in × R₂/(R₁+R₂) gives the p.d. across R₂ — the resistor in the formula's top. To get the other component's share, put its resistance on top. The biggest resistor always takes the biggest slice of the voltage.

Electrical safety — keeping current where it belongs

Mains electricity at 230 V is dangerous, so a house circuit is built with deliberate safety features. First, the hazards to recognise:

Damaged insulation (bare wires)
Overheating cables
Damp conditions (water conducts)
Overloading sockets / extension leads

The three mains wires

WireJob
Live (line)Carries the alternating supply to the appliance — the dangerous one, at 230 V.
NeutralCompletes the circuit, returning current; near 0 V.
EarthA safety path to the ground; carries no current normally.
Switch & fuse go in the LIVE wire — so that when switched off, the appliance is cut off from the 230 V live side and is genuinely safe to touch. Put the switch in the neutral wire and the appliance stays "live" even when off.
🔥 A fuse is a deliberate weak link
A fuse is a thin wire that melts if the current gets too big, breaking the circuit before the cabling overheats and starts a fire. Choose a rating just above the appliance's normal working current. A trip switch (MCB) does the same job magnetically — it snaps off on excess current and, unlike a fuse, you just reset it.
✅ Choosing a fuse rating
Find the normal current with I = P / V, then pick the next standard fuse above it. A 230 V, 920 W microwave draws I = 920 / 230 = 4 A → fit a 5 A fuse (a 13 A fuse would let a fault current flow far too long).

Earthing vs double insulation

Earthed metal case
If a live wire touches the metal body, a huge current flows straight to earth → the fuse blows → the appliance is dead and safe. The earth wire + fuse work as a team.
Double insulation
The case is non-conducting plastic (symbol ▣), so a loose live wire can never make it live. No earth wire needed — the fuse just protects the cabling.
10 / 12 · 4.5.3–4.5.5 · Electricity → Motion

When current makes magnetism — and motion

Here's where the two halves of the topic snap together: an electric current creates a magnetic field around it. Put that field in another magnet's field and the two push on each other — current produces a force, and force produces motion. Every electric motor lives on this idea.

Magnetic field of a current — around a straight wire the field lines are concentric circles; their direction comes from the right-hand grip rule (thumb points along the conventional current, curled fingers show the field). A solenoid (coil) makes a field just like a bar magnet, with a N and S end. Strengthen it with: more current, more turns, or a soft-iron core. Reverse the current and the whole field flips.
✅ Where this is used
A relay uses a small current's electromagnet to pull a switch shut in a separate high-power circuit (a little current safely controls a big one). A loudspeaker feeds the varying audio current into a coil sitting in a magnet's field — the coil is pushed in and out, vibrating the cone to make sound.

The motor effect — force on a current in a field

A current-carrying wire placed across a magnetic field feels a force (it gets pushed). This is the motor effect. Reverse either the current or the field and the force reverses; reverse both and it stays the same. The force is biggest when the wire is at 90° to the field, and zero when it lies along the field.

✋ Fleming's LEFT-hand rule (motors)
Hold the thumb and first two fingers of your left hand at right angles. First finger = Field (N→S), seCond finger = Current (conventional, + → −), and the thuMb shows the Motion (force). Left hand = the motor/force rule. (At IGCSE you find the direction only — there's no force formula to plug into.)
✅ Beams of charged particles (Extended)
A moving charge is a current, so a beam of charges crossing a magnetic field is deflected by the same rule. Treat the conventional-current direction as the way positive charges move — for an electron beam, the current points opposite to the electrons' motion, so apply the left-hand rule accordingly.

The d.c. motor

Bend the wire into a coil in a magnetic field: the motor effect pushes one side up and the other side down, so the coil spins. The turning effect grows with more turns, more current, or a stronger field.

Split-ring commutator — the clever bit. Every half-turn it swaps the current direction in the coil, so the push on each side keeps driving rotation the same way instead of stalling. Brushes press against it to feed current to the spinning coil. Commutator = d.c. motor; remember it, because the generator (next) uses slip rings instead.
11 / 12 · 4.5.1–4.5.2 & 4.5.6 · Motion → Electricity

Induction, generators & transformers

Run the last section in reverse. If a current makes magnetism, can magnetism make a current? Yes — and it powers the entire grid. Move a conductor across a magnetic field (or change the field through it) and you induce an e.m.f. in it. This is electromagnetic induction.

🧲 The demonstration to remember
Push a bar magnet into a coil joined to a sensitive galvanometer — the needle kicks. Pull it out — the needle kicks the other way. Hold it still inside — nothing. No movement, no change, no induced e.m.f. You're not "supplying" current; you're generating it from motion.
Size of the induced e.m.f. grows with: a faster movement (or faster-changing field), more turns on the coil, and a stronger magnet. Direction (Extended): the induced e.m.f. always opposes the change that causes it (Lenz's law) — for the generator effect, the relative directions of force, field and induced current follow Fleming's RIGHT-hand rule.
🙅 Nature pushes back (Lenz's law)
Push the magnet's N pole in and the coil induces a current that turns its near face into a N pole too — repelling you, resisting the push. Pull out and it becomes a S pole, trying to hold the magnet back. The induced current always fights the change. If it helped instead, you'd get energy from nothing — so this is really conservation of energy in disguise.
⚠️ Left hand or right hand?
Motor (current → motion) uses the LEFT hand. Generator (motion → current) uses the RIGHT hand. The fingers mean the same three quantities in both — first finger = field, second = current, thumb = motion — only the hand changes. Mix them up and your induced-current direction comes out backwards.

The a.c. generator

Spin a coil in a magnetic field and its sides keep cutting field lines, inducing an e.m.f. that reverses every half-turn — alternating current. Slip rings and brushes connect the spinning coil to the outside circuit (no commutator — we want the reversal here).

✅ Reading the e.m.f.–time graph
The output is a sine wave. The e.m.f. peaks when the coil's sides are sweeping across the field lines fastest — the moment the plane of the coil lies parallel to the field — and falls to zero a quarter-turn later, when the sides move along the field lines (plane of the coil perpendicular to the field) and cut none. Spin it faster → taller and more frequent peaks.

The transformer

A transformer changes the size of an a.c. voltage. Two coils — primary and secondary — share a soft-iron core. Alternating current in the primary makes a constantly-changing magnetic field in the core; that changing field links the secondary and induces an a.c. e.m.f. in it.

Transformer equation — voltages are in the same ratio as the turns: V_p / V_s = N_p / N_s. More turns on the secondary (N_s > N_p) → step-up (higher voltage out); fewer turns → step-down. It only works on a.c. — a steady d.c. gives an unchanging field and induces nothing.

Ideal transformer (Extended) — a perfect transformer wastes no power, so power in = power out: I_p V_p = I_s V_s. Step the voltage up and the current steps down by the same factor (and vice-versa). E.g. stepping 230 V down to 46 V at I_p = 1 A gives I_s = (230 × 1)/46 = 5 A.

Why the grid uses enormous voltages

Power stations send electricity across the country at hundreds of thousands of volts. The reason is energy lost heating the cables, given by P = I²R:

Low current, high V — tiny loss
High current, low V — huge I²R loss
Same power delivered two ways — the high-voltage route wastes almost nothing in the cables.
✅ The transmission argument (full marks)
For a fixed power P = VI, raising V lowers the current I. Because cable loss is P_loss = I²R — proportional to current squared — even a small drop in I slashes the wasted heat. Transformers step up the voltage for transmission (low current, low loss, thinner cheaper cables), then step down again to a safe 230 V for homes. Example: sending 20 kW through a 2 Ω line at 200 V needs I = 100 A → loss = 100² × 2 = 20 000 W (all of it!). At 20 000 V, I = 1 A → loss = 1² × 2 = just 2 W.
