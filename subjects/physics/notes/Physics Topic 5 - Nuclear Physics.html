Nuclear Physics

Inside the atom is a tiny, dense, charged nucleus — and some nuclei are unstable. This whole topic is the story of why they break apart, what shoots out, and how we count it. Tap the panels — they move.

01 / 10 · 5.1.1 The Atom

The atom — almost entirely empty space

An atom is a positively charged nucleus at the centre, with negatively charged electrons in orbit around it. The nucleus is unbelievably small compared with the whole atom — if the atom were a cricket stadium, the nucleus would be a grain of rice on the centre spot. Everything else is empty space the electrons sweep through.

⚛️ The core picture
Tiny dense centre, mostly empty outside. Almost all the mass sits in the nucleus; almost all the volume is the electron cloud. Hold that image — every later idea hangs off it.

Ions — atoms that have gained or lost electrons

A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons. Knock an electron off and the atom is left with more protons than electrons → a positive ion. Add an electron and it becomes a negative ion.

Lose electron(s) → positive ion
Fewer negatives than positives, so the overall charge is +. E.g. a sodium atom loses one electron to become Na⁺.
Gain electron(s) → negative ion
More negatives than positives, so the overall charge is −. E.g. a chlorine atom gains one electron to become Cl⁻.

How we know: the α-particle scattering experiment (Extended)

Geiger and Marsden (directed by Rutherford) fired fast alpha particles at an extremely thin sheet of gold foil and watched where they went.

ObservationWhat it proves about the nucleus
Most α-particles passed straight through undeflectedThe atom is mostly empty space
A few were deflected through large anglesThe nucleus is positively charged (it repels the positive α)
A very few bounced almost straight backThe nucleus is very small and holds most of the atom's mass
🎯 Why "bouncing back" is the killer clue
Firing a cannonball at tissue paper and having it ricochet back would stun you — it can only happen if something tiny, hard and massive is hidden in there. Rutherford said exactly that. A spread-out "plum-pudding" of charge could never throw a fast α straight back; only a concentrated nucleus can.

Inside the nucleus — protons & neutrons

The nucleus is a bundle of two particles, together called nucleons:

Proton number, Z (atomic number) — the number of protons in the nucleus. It alone decides which element the atom is. relative charge on nucleus = +Z.
Nucleon number, A (mass number) — the total number of protons + neutrons. relative mass of nucleus ≈ A, and the number of neutrons is N = A − Z.

Nuclide notation

Every nuclide is written with both numbers stacked before its chemical symbol:

⚠️ Read the notation
 AZX  — top = nucleon number A, bottom = proton number Z. Example: 2311Na has 11 protons, 23 nucleons, so 23 − 11 = 12 neutrons.

Isotopes

Isotopes — atoms of the same element (same proton number Z) but with different numbers of neutrons (different nucleon number A). An element can have several isotopes.
🧪 Same element, different weight
Carbon is always Z = 6. But ¹²₆C, ¹³₆C and ¹⁴₆C have 6, 7 and 8 neutrons. They behave identically in chemistry (the electrons decide that) but differ in mass and in nuclear stability — ¹⁴₆C is radioactive and is what archaeologists use for carbon-dating.
⚠️ Don't confuse the numbers
Change Z and you change the element. Change only the neutron count (so A changes but Z stays) and you get an isotope of the same element. The element's identity is fixed by protons, never by neutrons.
03 / 10 · 5.1.2 Fission & Fusion (Extended)

Splitting and joining nuclei

Nuclear fission — splitting
A large, heavy nucleus splits into two smaller nuclei (plus a few neutrons). Used in nuclear power stations. Releases energy.
Nuclear fusion — joining
Two small, light nuclei join to form a larger nucleus. Powers the Sun and stars (hydrogen → helium). Releases energy.
Fission (example, qualitative) — a uranium-235 nucleus absorbs a neutron and splits: ²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + ⁹²₃₆Kr + 3 ¹₀n. The spare neutrons can split more nuclei — a chain reaction.
Fusion (example, qualitative) — light hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium: ² ₁H + ³ ₁H → ⁴ ₂He + ¹₀n. This needs enormous temperatures and pressures — found in the core of stars.
✅ What the syllabus actually asks
You describe fission/fusion as the splitting or joining of nuclei, give the nuclide equation, and say qualitatively that mass changes into energy — but you are not asked for any numerical mass or energy values here. Both processes release energy.
04 / 10 · 5.2.1 Detecting Radioactivity

Background radiation & count rate

Radioactivity is everywhere at a low level, all the time — this is background radiation. The main sources the syllabus names:

Radon gas (in the air)
Rocks & buildings
Food & drink
Cosmic rays (from space)

We measure ionising radiation with a detector connected to a counter (e.g. a Geiger–Müller tube and counter). The reading is a count rate, in counts / s or counts / minute.

Corrected count rate (Extended) — because the background is always being counted too, you must subtract it: corrected = measured − background.
✅ Worked example
A detector near a source reads 510 counts/min. With the source removed, the background reads 30 counts/min. The true count rate from the source is 510 − 30 = 480 counts/min. Always correct for background before any half-life calculation that asks for it.
05 / 10 · 5.2.2 The Three Emissions

Alpha, beta & gamma — three very different things

Emission from a nucleus is spontaneous (you can't make it happen or stop it) and random in direction. Three kinds come out, and they differ in nature, ionising power and how far they penetrate. Pick one below and watch what stops it:

α — alpha
β — beta
γ — gamma
Alpha (α)
An alpha particle is a helium nucleus — 2 protons + 2 neutrons. It is heavy and highly charged, so it ionises strongly but is stopped by just a sheet of paper (or a few cm of air).

🔄 The inverse rule that ties it together
Most ionising = least penetrating, and vice-versa. Alpha is big and double-charged, so it crashes into atoms constantly — ionising hard but using up its energy fast (low penetration). Gamma has no charge and no mass, barely interacts, so it ionises weakly but sails a long way.

Deflection in fields (Extended)

Because α and β are charged, they curve in electric and magnetic fields; γ (no charge) goes straight.

  • α (+2) deflects slightly — it is heavy, so hard to push.
  • β (−1) deflects a lot, the opposite way to α — it is very light, and has opposite charge.
  • γ (0) is not deflected at all.
✅ Explaining ionising effect (Extended)
Account for ionising power using kinetic energy and electric charge: α has the most charge (+2) and is slow and massive, so it interacts most strongly and ionises the most; β has less charge; γ has no charge, so it ionises the least.
06 / 10 · 5.2.3 Radioactive Decay

Decay — how the nucleus changes

Radioactive decay is a change in an unstable nucleus that emits α, β and/or γ. It is spontaneous and random — you cannot predict when any one nucleus will decay, and nothing (heat, pressure, chemistry) speeds it up or slows it down.

🍿 Why "random" still gives a smooth curve
One popcorn kernel pops at an unpredictable moment — random. But a whole bag pops in a smooth, predictable pattern over time. Same here: individual decays are random, yet a huge sample decays in a beautifully regular way. That regularity is the half-life.

What each decay does to the nucleus (Extended)

Isotopes are radioactive when the nucleus has too many neutrons and/or is simply too heavy. Decay nudges the nucleus toward stability:

The beta decay trick — a neutron turns into a proton plus the electron that is fired out: neutron → proton + electron. That is why Z goes up by 1 while A stays the same. After α or β decay the nucleus is a different element.

07 / 10 · 5.2.4 Half-life

Half-life — the clock of the nucleus

Half-life — the time taken for half the (undecayed) nuclei of an isotope in a sample to decay. Equivalently, the time for the count rate (activity) to fall to half its value.

Each half-life that passes, whatever is left halves again: 100% → 50% → 25% → 12.5% … It never quite reaches zero. Drag the slider and watch the sample decay:

Reading it back
After 0 half-lives the fraction left is (½)0 = 1. To find a half-life from data, count how many times the activity has halved, then divide the total time by that number.
✅ Worked example — find the half-life
A source's corrected count rate falls from 800 → 400 → 200 → 100 counts/s over 18 hours. That is 3 halvings (800→400→200→100). So one half-life = 18 ÷ 3 = 6 hours.
⚠️ Two classic slips
(1) Half-life is the time to halve again from wherever you are — it is constant, not "time to reach zero". (2) For a half-life from real data, subtract the background first (the Extended version uses data from which background has not already been removed).

Choosing the right isotope for a job (Extended)

UseRadiation + half-life chosen because…
Smoke alarmsα source — its radiation is stopped by smoke (so the alarm "sees" smoke); long half-life so it lasts for years
Irradiating food to kill bacteriaγ — penetrates the packaging and kills microbes without making the food radioactive
Sterilising equipmentγ — penetrates sealed packaging to kill all microbes
Measuring/controlling thickness (paper, foil)β — partly absorbed by the sheet; the amount getting through reveals the thickness
Diagnosis & treatment of cancerγ — penetrates the body to image or to destroy tumour cells
08 / 10 · 5.2.5 Safety Precautions

Why radiation is dangerous — and how to stay safe

Effects on living things — ionising radiation can cause cell death, mutations (damaged DNA), and cancer. The more dose received, the greater the risk.

The three ways to reduce exposure (Extended)

⏱️ Time
Spend less time near a source — total dose builds up with exposure time.
📏 Distance
Stay further away — intensity falls off quickly with distance. Use tongs/handling tools, never bare hands.
🧱 Shielding
Put absorbing material between you and the source — lead, thick concrete, or lead-lined aprons absorb the radiation before it reaches you.
⚠️ Handling, using & storing safely
Keep sources in lead-lined containers, handle with long tongs (never bare hands), point them away from people, keep exposure brief, and store them locked in a clearly-labelled shielded box. Time + distance + shielding, every time.
