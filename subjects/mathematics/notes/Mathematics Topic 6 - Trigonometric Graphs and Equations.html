Trig Graphs & Equations

A one-page cheat sheet for the trig-functions part of Topic 6: the three graphs y = sin x, y = cos x, y = tan x for 0°–360°, the exact values you must know, and the two-solution method for solving trig equations. Tap the panels — they move.

01 / 07 · The big picture

Why sine and cosine make waves

Imagine a point walking anticlockwise around a circle of radius 1. At every angle x it has a height and a sideways position. Plot the height against the angle and you get y = sin x; plot the sideways position and you get y = cos x. The circle repeats every 360°, so the graphs repeat every 360° — that's why they're waves.

💡 The core idea
sin = height, cos = across. As the point climbs to the top of the circle the height rises to its maximum of 1 (at 90°); at the bottom it's −1 (at 270°). The height can never be more than the radius, so sin x and cos x are always trapped between −1 and +1. tan x is height ÷ across, which blows up to infinity whenever "across" hits zero.
🎡 A picture you know
Ride the Giant Wheel at a mela. Your height above the centre traces a sine wave as the wheel turns: up to the top, back to the middle, down to the bottom, back up — one full turn, one full wave. Keep spinning and the same wave repeats forever. That "repeat length" is the period.

⚠️ Classic slip
tan x is not a wave between −1 and 1. It shoots up to +∞, then reappears from −∞ on the other side of a vertical asymptote at 90° and 270° (the dashed red lines). Its period is 180°, not 360° like sine and cosine.

🧠 The "1, 2, 3" hand trick
Write 0 1 2 3 4 under the angles 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°. For sin, take √(that number) ÷ 2: so sin gives √0/2, √1/2, √2/2, √3/2, √4/2 = 0, ½, √2⁄2, √3⁄2, 1. cos is the same list reversed. And tan x = sin x ÷ cos x.
✅ Where marks are won
Notice cos 60° = ½ and sin 30° = ½ — equal, because 60° and 30° are complementary. And tan 45° = 1 exactly (height = across at 45°). If a "solve" question gives you cos x = ½, you should instantly know one answer is 60° without touching the calculator.
04 / 07 · E6.4 · The key skill

Why every equation has two answers

Your calculator's sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹ only ever give one angle — the "principal" one. But a horizontal line y = k usually cuts the wave twice between 0° and 360°. The exam wants both crossings, so you always find the calculator answer, then use the graph's symmetry to get its partner.

💡 See it as a line crossing a wave
"Solve sin x = 0.5" just means: where does the flat line y = 0.5 meet the sine curve? Draw the line across the graph — it hits the curve twice. The calculator hands you the first crossing (30°); the shape of the graph gives you the second (150°).
The three partner rules (0° ≤ x ≤ 360°)
If the calculator gives you a first angle x₁, the second solution is:
  • sin x = k  →  second angle = 180° − x₁
  • cos x = k  →  second angle = 360° − x₁
  • tan x = k  →  second angle = 180° + x₁
Then add 360° to any answer that came out negative, and keep only those in 0°–360°.
⚠️ Memory hook
Sine → 180 − x   Cos → 360 − x   Tan → 180 + x. Sine and cosine both "reflect"; tangent "repeats every 180°".

⚠️ No solution
Slide sine or cosine to the very top or bottom: a line above y = 1 or below y = −1 never touches the wave. So sin x = 2 has no solution — sine can't exceed 1. Tangent, however, reaches every value, so tan x = k always has two answers.
06 / 07 · Exam technique

Worked examples — the full method

Example 1 · Solve 2 cos x + 1 = 0 for 0° ≤ x ≤ 360°

rearrange to cos x = … 2 cos x + 1 = 0 → cos x = −½
calculator angle from +½ cos⁻¹(½) = 60°
cos is negative → answers left & right of 180° 180 − 60 = 120°,  180 + 60 = 240°
both in range x = 120°, 240°
✅ Tip
When k is negative, work out the calculator angle from the positive value first (60° here), then place the two answers using the rule. It's cleaner than trusting the calculator's negative output.

Example 2 · Solve sin x = 0.6 for 0° ≤ x ≤ 360° (1 d.p.)

first angle x₁ = sin⁻¹(0.6) = 36.9°
sine rule: partner = 180 − x₁ 180 − 36.9 = 143.1°
answer x = 36.9°, 143.1°

Example 3 · Solve tan x = −1.5 for 0° ≤ x ≤ 360° (1 d.p.)

angle from +1.5 tan⁻¹(1.5) = 56.3°
tan is negative → answers in 2nd & 4th quadrants 180 − 56.3 = 123.7°,  360 − 56.3 = 303.7°
check with partner rule 123.7 and 123.7 + 180 = 303.7 ✓
answer x = 123.7°, 303.7°

Example 4 · Solve sin x = 2/3  (using exact-value feel)

first angle x₁ = sin⁻¹(0.6667) = 41.8°
partner = 180 − 41.8 138.2°
answer x = 41.8°, 138.2°
⚠️ Read the range
Everything above uses 0° ≤ x ≤ 360°. If a question gives a different range (e.g. −180° to 180°), find all answers with the partner rule then shift each by ±360° to land inside the stated range.
