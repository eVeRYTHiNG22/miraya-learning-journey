01 / 08 · E5.1 · Units of measure

Units of measure — and the square/cube trap

Measuring anything is just counting how many copies of a unit fit. A length counts units laid in a line; an area counts unit squares tiling a surface; a volume counts unit cubes packing a box. That single idea is the whole topic — everything after is a shortcut for the counting.

💡 The core idea
When you shrink the length unit by a factor, area shrinks by that factor squared and volume by it cubed — because area is length × length, and volume is length × length × length. Get this one rule and every conversion below falls out.
🧩 Why 1 m² is not 100 cm²
A 1 m ruler is 100 cm — factor 100. But a 1 m × 1 m tile, cut into cm squares, is 100 squares along each side: 100 × 100 = 10 000 little cm² squares. The factor got squared. A 1 m³ cube holds 100³ = 1 000 000 cm³.
Scale-factor rule — if lengths convert by ×k, then areas convert by ×k² and volumes by ×k³.

⚠️ The most common mensuration slip
Writing 1 m² = 100 cm². It is 10 000 cm². Convert the length factor first (m → cm is ×100), then square it for area (×10 000) or cube it for volume (×1 000 000).
✅ Exam move
Do the unit change before you compute, and keep every length in the same unit. Mixing cm and m in one area or volume formula is the fastest way to lose all the marks on an otherwise correct method.
02 / 08 · E5.2 · Area & perimeter

Perimeter & area of straight-sided shapes

Perimeter is the distance once around — just add the sides. Area is how many unit squares cover the surface. Every polygon formula below is secretly the rectangle, bent into a new shape.

💡 Every formula is a rectangle in disguise
Parallelogram: slice the triangle off one end and slide it to the other — it becomes a rectangle, so A = base × height. Triangle: two copies make a parallelogram, so a triangle is ½bh. Trapezium: average the two parallel sides, then multiply by the gap between them.

⚠️ Perpendicular height, not the slant
For a parallelogram, triangle or trapezium the h is the perpendicular distance — straight across, at 90° — never the length of a sloping side. A slanted side is longer, so it inflates the area.
✅ Only the triangle formula is given
The List of formulas gives A = ½ × base × height for the triangle and nothing else here. Rectangle, parallelogram and trapezium areas you must know cold.
03 / 08 · E5.3.1 · Circles

Circles — circumference & area

💡 What π actually is
Roll any circle along the floor for one full turn: the distance is always about 3.14 diameters. That fixed number is π. So the way around a circle is π × diameter — that is the entire meaning of C = πd.
Circle formulas — circumference C = 2πr = πd, area A = πr², where r is the radius and d = 2r the diameter.
🧩 Why area is πr²
Cut the disc into very thin rings and unroll them. They stack into a triangle: base = 2πr (the outer circumference), height = r. Its area is ½ × 2πr × r = πr². The formula isn't magic — it's a triangle in disguise.
circle, radius 7 cm C = 2π(7) = 14π ≈ 43.98 cm
A = π(7)² = 49π ≈ 153.9 cm²
circular table, diameter 1.4 m → r = 0.7 m edge = 2π(0.7) = 1.4π ≈ 4.40 m
lace edging at ₹150/m → 4.398 × 150 ≈ ₹660
✅ "Give your answer in terms of π"
Then do not press the π button — leave it as 14π or 49π. It is exact and often quicker. Only turn it into a decimal when the question asks for one (or gives a value of π).
⚠️ Radius vs diameter
Area needs the radius. If you're told the diameter, halve it first. Putting d into πr² makes the area four times too big.
⚠️ Know on sight
C = 2πr = πd A = πr² d = 2r
04 / 08 · E5.3.2 · Arcs & sectors

Arcs & sectors — slices of a circle

💡 A sector is a fraction of the whole pie
A sector is a pizza slice; its angle θ tells you what fraction of the full 360° circle you took. So everything — the curved edge (the arc) and the area — is just that fraction θ/360 of the whole circle's circumference and area.
Arc & sector — arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr, sector area = (θ/360) × πr². The perimeter of a sector = arc + the two straight radii = arc + 2r.

🧩 Minor and major
A slice and the rest of the pie are both sectors. The smaller (θ < 180°) is the minor sector; the larger left behind is the major sector, with angle 360 − θ. Same formula, just the other fraction.
sector garden bed — r = 3 m, θ = 120° area = (120/360) × π(3)² = 3π ≈ 9.42 m²
turf at ₹80/m² → 9.4248 × 80 ≈ ₹754
⚠️ A sector's perimeter includes the two radii
The perimeter is not just the curved arc — a sector is bounded by the arc and two straight radii. Forgetting the + 2r is the classic dropped mark.
✅ Reduce the fraction first
Simplify θ/360 before multiplying: 72/360 = 1/5, 120/360 = 1/3. Cleaner numbers, fewer slips, and it keeps exact "in terms of π" answers tidy.
05 / 08 · E5.4 · Surface area & volume

Surface area & volume — the solid toolkit

💡 Two different questions
Volume = how much space fills the solid (how many unit cubes pack inside). Surface area = how much wrapping paper covers the outside (unfold every face flat and add them up). They are answered by different formulas — never confuse the two.
Prism — any solid with the same cross-section all the way through. Its volume is V = cross-section area × length. A cuboid is a prism with a rectangular cross-section; a cylinder is a prism with a circular one. Learn the prism, and both come free.

⚠️ "Curved surface" is not the total
The given formula 2πrh is only the label around the tin. For a closed cylinder add the two circular ends (+ 2πr²); for an open pipe or trough add none. Always read whether the solid is open or closed before counting faces.
✅ Which formulas are given
The List of formulas gives the volumes of prism, cylinder, pyramid, cone and sphere, plus the curved areas of cylinder and cone and the surface area of a sphere. It does not give cuboid volume or any total surface area — you assemble those yourself.
06 / 08 · E5.4 · Curved & pointed solids

Cones, pyramids & spheres

💡 Why the "⅓" for spikes
A cone or pyramid is the pointed solid that fits inside the cylinder or prism on the same base and height — and it fills exactly one third of it. That is the whole reason cone volume is ⅓πr²h and pyramid volume is ⅓ × base × height: a third of the box that would contain it.
Slant height — the distance up the sloping side of a cone, l = √(r² + h²) by Pythagoras. The curved surface uses this slant l: πrl. The volume uses the vertical height h: ⅓πr²h.
🧩 Why curved area is πrl
Snip a cone up its side and flatten the curved surface — it opens into a sector of radius l. Its area works out to πrl. The slant l is the radius of that flattened fan, which is why the height h never appears in the surface formula.
cone — r = 3 cm, h = 4 cm slant l = √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5 cm
volume = ⅓π(3)²(4) = 12π ≈ 37.70 cm³
curved surface = π(3)(5) = 15π ≈ 47.12 cm²; total = 15π + π(3)² = 24π ≈ 75.40 cm²
sphere — r = 6 cm volume = 4⁄3 π(6)³ = 288π ≈ 904.8 cm³
surface area = 4π(6)² = 144π ≈ 452.4 cm²
square pyramid — base 6 × 6 cm, height 4 cm V = ⅓ × 36 × 4 = 48 cm³
⚠️ Slant l for area, vertical h for volume
The single biggest cone error: using the same length for both. V = ⅓πr²h needs the vertical height; πrl needs the slant. If you're given one and need the other, Pythagoras connects them: l² = r² + h².
✅ Hemisphere care
A solid hemisphere has volume ½ × 4⁄3 πr³ = 2⁄3 πr³, and its surface is the curved dome 2πr² plus the flat circular base πr² = 3πr². Don't forget the flat face.
07 / 08 · E5.5 · Compound shapes & parts

Compound shapes & parts of solids

💡 No new formulas — just add and subtract
A compound shape is built from shapes you already know: split it into rectangles, triangles and semicircles, find each area, then add. A part of a shape (a ring, a solid with a hole, a chopped-off cone) is a whole shape minus what was removed. That's the entire skill.
Compound area — a "stadium"
Rectangle 40 m × 20 m with a semicircle (r = 10) on each end.
area = 40×20 + π(10)² = 800 + 100π ≈ 1114 m²
perimeter = 2×40 + 2π(10) = 80 + 20π ≈ 142.8 m (two semicircles = one whole circle)
Compound solid — a toy
Cone (r = 3, h = 4) sitting on a hemisphere (r = 3).
V = ⅓π(3)²(4) + 2⁄3 π(3)³
= 12π + 18π = 30π ≈ 94.25 cm³
Frustum — a cone (or pyramid) with the tip sliced off parallel to the base, like a bucket or a matka. Its volume is the big cone minus the small cone that was removed. Find the small cone's height by similar triangles first.
frustum — full cone r = 10 cm, height 20 cm; top cone (r = 5, h = 10) removed big cone = ⅓π(10)²(20) = 2000π/3
small cone = ⅓π(5)²(10) = 250π/3
frustum = 2000π/3 − 250π/3 = 1750π/3 ≈ 1832.6 cm³
⚠️ Rings: subtract the areas, not the radii
For a flat ring (annulus) of outer radius R and inner radius r, the area is πR² − πr² = π(R² − r²). You cannot do π(R − r)² — square each radius first, then subtract.
✅ Show the split for method marks
Label each piece, compute it separately, then combine — even if the arithmetic goes wrong, a clearly shown "area = rectangle + semicircle" earns the method marks. Watch shared edges: when two pieces join, the buried faces are not part of the outer surface.
