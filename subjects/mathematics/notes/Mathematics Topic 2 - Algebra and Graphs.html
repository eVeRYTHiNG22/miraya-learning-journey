01 / 15 · E2.1–E2.2 · Manipulation

Expressions — expanding and factorising

A letter just stands for a number we haven't fixed yet. Everything you already do with numbers — add, multiply, take out common factors — still works; you simply can't collapse 3a into one number until you know a. Substituting means putting the number back in: if a = 4, then 3a − 1 = 11.

💡 The core idea
Expanding and factorising are the same door, walked through in opposite directions. Expanding multiplies brackets out; factorising puts the brackets back. 3x(x+2) ⇄ 3x² + 6x. If you can do one, you can always check the other by reversing it.

Collecting like terms & expanding

Only terms with the identical letter part combine: 2a² + 5a² = 7a², but a² and a never merge. To expand, every term in one bracket multiplies every term in the other.

expand & simplify (3x + y)(x − 4y)
= 3x·x + 3x·(−4y) + y·x + y·(−4y)
= 3x² − 12xy + xy − 4y² = 3x² − 11xy − 4y²

The syllabus also expects three brackets — expand two, then multiply the result by the third.

✅ How to factorise x² + bx + c
Find two numbers that multiply to c and add to b. For x² + 5x + 6: which pair multiplies to 6 and adds to 5? → 2 and 3. So it factorises to (x + 2)(x + 3). Always expand back mentally to check.
⚠️ "Factorise fully"
Don't stop early. 2x² − 8 = 2(x² − 4) is not finished — the bracket is a difference of two squares: 2(x + 2)(x − 2). Take out the common factor first, then look again.
02 / 15 · E2.2.5 · Completed-square form

Completing the square

Some quadratics don't factorise into neat brackets — but every quadratic can be rewritten as "a squared bracket, plus a leftover". That form is gold: it instantly hands you the turning point and lets you solve by square-rooting.

🧩 Folding a square
x² + 6x is almost the area of a square of side (x + 3) — which has area x² + 6x + 9. You're 9 short, so you write (x + 3)² − 9: build the perfect square, then subtract whatever you over-added. Halve the x-coefficient (6 → 3), that's what goes in the bracket.
Completed-square form — write x² + bx + c = (x + b/2)² + (c − b²/4). The turning point is then at x = −b/2 — a minimum when the x² coefficient is positive (the squared bracket can't go below 0), or a maximum when it is negative.
complete the square x² + 6x + 5
= (x + 3)² − 9 + 5
= (x + 3)² − 4  →  minimum at (−3, −4); roots x = −3 ± 2 = −1 and −5

When a ≠ 1

Take the a out of the first two terms first, complete the square inside, then multiply back in.

a ≠ 1 2x² − 8x + 3
= 2(x² − 4x) + 3 = 2[(x − 2)² − 4] + 3
= 2(x − 2)² − 5  →  minimum at (2, −5)
✅ Why examiners love it
"Find the turning point of y = ax² + bx + c by completing the square" is named explicitly in the syllabus (E2.11). The completed-square form a(x − p)² + q gives the vertex (p, q) with zero plotting.
03 / 15 · E2.3 · Algebraic fractions

Algebraic fractions

These obey the exact same rules as number fractions — common denominator to add, multiply tops and bottoms, "flip and multiply" to divide. The only new move is factorising first so you can cancel.

Adding & subtracting

common denominator 6 x/3 + (x − 4)/2
= 2x/6 + 3(x − 4)/6 = (2x + 3x − 12)/6 = (5x − 12)/6

Multiplying & dividing

3a/4 × 9a/10 = 27a²/40   ·   3a/4 ÷ 9a/10 = 3a/4 × 10/9a = 30a/36a = 5/6

Simplifying rational expressions

Factorise top and bottom, then cancel the common bracket:

(x² − 2x)/(x² − 5x + 6) = x(x − 2) / [(x − 2)(x − 3)] = x/(x − 3)
⚠️ You can only cancel factors, never terms
In (x + 2)/(x + 3) you cannot "cancel the x's" — they are added, not multiplied. Cancelling is only allowed once top and bottom are written as products of brackets and a whole bracket matches.

2⁻³ is not −8. A negative index means reciprocal: 2⁻³ = 1/2³ = 1/8, a small positive number. And a^(2/3) means "cube root then square" — root the bottom, power the top.
05 / 15 · E2.5 · Equations & formulae

Solving equations & rearranging formulae

⚖️ An equation is a balance
The = sign means the two sides weigh the same. Whatever you do — add, subtract, multiply, divide — do it to both sides and the balance holds. Solving is just peeling operations off the unknown, in reverse order.

Linear equations

solve 5 − 2x = 3(x + 7)
5 − 2x = 3x + 21  →  5 − 21 = 3x + 2x  →  −16 = 5x  →  x = −3.2

Fractional equations

Multiply through by the denominators to clear the fractions, then solve as normal.

solve x/(2x + 1) = 4  →  x = 4(2x + 1) = 8x + 4  →  −7x = 4  →  x = −4/7

Changing the subject

A formula is just an equation you rearrange for a different letter — same balance moves. The Extended twist: the new subject may appear twice, or under a power or root.

make r the subject A = πr²  →  r² = A/π  →  r = √(A/π)
✅ When the subject appears twice
Gather every term with that letter on one side, factorise it out, then divide. E.g. make x the subject of ax = b + cx → ax − cx = b → x(a − c) = b → x = b/(a − c).

Quadratic equations

A quadratic ax² + bx + c = 0 can have two, one, or no real solutions — and its graph, a parabola, shows exactly which by how it meets the x-axis. Drag the sliders: watch the curve, its roots, and its lowest/highest point move together.

Three ways to solve

Factorising
Best when factors are whole. x² − 2x − 3 = 0 → (x−3)(x+1)=0 → x = 3 or −1.
Quadratic formula

Completing the square

x = (−3 ± √(9 + 40)) / 4 = (−3 ± 7)/4  →  x = 1 or x = −2.5
⚠️ Set to zero first
The factorising and formula methods only work from … = 0. Rearrange everything to one side before you start, and keep the sign of c.
07 / 15 · E2.5.4–5 · Simultaneous equations

Simultaneous equations

📍 Two facts, one meeting point
Each equation is a line (or curve). Solving them together finds the point that satisfies both at once — graphically, where they cross. Two straight lines cross once; a line and a curve can cross twice.

Two linear equations — eliminate

Add or subtract the equations to cancel one letter, solve for the other, then substitute back.

2x + y = 7  …(1)
3x − y = 8  …(2)
(1) + (2): 5x = 15 → x = 3  →  from (1): y = 7 − 6 = 1
x = 3, y = 1

One linear, one non-linear — substitute

Rearrange the linear one for y, substitute into the curve, and you get a quadratic to solve. Expect two pairs of answers.

y = x + 1  and  y = x² − 1
substitute: x² − 1 = x + 1 → x² − x − 2 = 0 → (x − 2)(x + 1) = 0
x = 2 → y = 3  ·  x = −1 → y = 0  →  (2, 3) and (−1, 0)
✅ Pair them up correctly
Every x has its own y — substitute each x back into the linear equation (it's simpler) and keep the pairs together. Two x-values means two coordinate answers, not four.
08 / 15 · E2.6 · Inequalities

Inequalities & regions

An inequality is solved almost like an equation — with one famous extra rule. Instead of a single answer it gives a range of values.

⚠️ The one rule that's different
Multiplying or dividing by a negative number flips the inequality sign. −2x < 6 → divide by −2 → x > −3 (the < becomes >). This is the single most common inequality slip.
solve the double inequality −3 ⩽ 3x − 2 < 7
+2 throughout: −1 ⩽ 3x < 9
÷3 throughout: −1/3 ⩽ x < 3

On a number line

  • Open circle ○ for strict inequalities < or > (value not included).
  • Closed circle ● for ⩽ or ⩾ (value included).

Regions in two variables

An inequality like y ⩾ x + 1 shades a whole half of the plane. Conventions: a solid line for ⩽ / ⩾, a broken line for < / >, and shade the unwanted region (unless told otherwise), leaving the wanted region clear.

✅ Test a point you can see
Not sure which side to shade? Try (0, 0) in the inequality. If it makes the statement true, the origin is in the wanted region; if false, it's in the unwanted one. (0,0) is the fastest test point — when the line doesn't pass through it.
⚠️ Not on this syllabus
Cambridge 0580 explicitly states linear programming problems are not included. You represent and read regions, but you are not asked to optimise an objective function over them — don't over-prepare that.
09 / 15 · E2.7 · Sequences

Sequences & the nth term

A sequence is a list with a rule. The nth term is a formula that jumps straight to any term — the 100th without writing the first 99. The trick is to look at the differences. Tap a type:

Linear
Quadratic
Cubic
Geometric
Linear sequence
nth term:
Linear (arithmetic)
Constant first difference d. nth term = dn + (a − d), where a is the 1st term. Spot d, then find the "+ something".
Quadratic
Constant second difference. The n² coefficient is (second difference ÷ 2). Then match the rest.
Cubic
Constant third difference. Leading term involves n³ — e.g. the cube numbers 1, 8, 27, … have nth term n³.
Geometric (exponential)
Constant ratio r (you multiply each time). nth term = a·rn−1.
✅ Subscript notation
Cambridge may write the nth term as Tₙ ("T sub n"). So Tₙ = 4n − 1 means the term in position n; T₅ = 19. The term-to-term rule (e.g. "add 4") and the position-to-term rule (the nth term) are different tools — read which one is asked.

Direct & inverse proportion

🔗 ∝ becomes = once you add k
"y ∝ x" (y is proportional to x) just says they rise together by a fixed factor. Turn the ∝ into = by inserting a constant: y = kx. Find k from one given pair, then you can answer anything.
StatementEquationAs x doubles, y…
Direct: y ∝ xy = kxdoubles
Square: y ∝ x²y = kx²×4
Square root: y ∝ √xy = k√x×√2 ≈ 1.41
Inverse: y ∝ 1/xy = k/xhalves
Inverse square: y ∝ 1/x²y = k/x²×¼
✅ The three-step method
(1) Write the equation with k. (2) Substitute the given pair to find k. (3) Rewrite with k and answer the question.
y inversely proportional to x², and y = 8 when x = 2
y = k/x² → 8 = k/4 → k = 32 → y = 32/x²
when x = 4: y = 32/16 = 2
⚠️ Inverse ≠ "subtract"
Inverse proportion means divide (y = k/x): as one grows the other shrinks multiplicatively. Their product xy = k stays constant — a handy check.
11 / 15 · E2.9 · Practical graphs

Graphs in practical situations

Real graphs (travel, conversion) carry meaning in their gradient and the area beneath. Reading those two things answers almost every practical-graph question.

Distance–time graph
Gradient = speed. Steeper = faster; horizontal = stopped; a curve = changing speed. The gradient at one instant is the gradient of the tangent there.
Speed–time graph
Gradient = acceleration (negative = deceleration). Area under = distance travelled.
🚗 A journey, two graphs
On a distance–time graph a flat line means you've parked (distance not changing). On a speed–time graph that same parked moment sits on zero, while a flat line above zero means cruising at steady speed. Same trip, two different pictures — don't mix up which is which.
✅ Area = distance (split into shapes)
Break the speed–time graph into triangles and rectangles (the syllabus keeps areas to linear sections).
accelerates 0→20 m/s in 8 s, then steady 20 m/s for 10 s
distance = ½ × 8 × 20 + 20 × 10 = 80 + 200 = 280 m
acceleration in first phase = 20 ÷ 8 = 2.5 m/s²
⚠️ Gradient of a curve = tangent
To find a rate of change at an instant on a curved graph, draw a tangent at that point and find its gradient (estimate). You can't read the gradient of a curve directly — and your estimate depends on a well-drawn tangent.

✅ Solve equations graphically
To solve f(x) = g(x), draw both and read the x-coordinates where they intersect. To solve f(x) = 0, read where the curve crosses the x-axis (its roots). Allowed forms: axⁿ for n = −2, −1, −½, 0, ½, 1, 2, 3 (and sums of up to three), plus a·bˣ + c.
⚠️ Asymptotes are never crossed
Reciprocal and exponential curves get infinitely close to their asymptote but never touch or cross it. Sketch the curve hugging the line, not meeting it — and remember a reciprocal has two separate branches.

Differentiation — the gradient of a curve

📐 Gradient at a single point
A straight line has one gradient everywhere. A curve's steepness changes from point to point. Differentiation is the machine that gives the gradient at any point — the gradient of the tangent there. Slide the point along the curve and watch the tangent (and its gradient) change:
Curve
y = x³ − 3x
Derivative
dy/dx = 3x² − 3
Gradient here
0.00
Meaning
stationary — turning point
Stationary points
Where dy/dx = 0 the tangent is flat — a maximum or minimum (a turning point). Here 3x² − 3 = 0 gives x = ±1: a maximum at (−1, 2) and a minimum at (1, −2).
The rule for axⁿ — d/dx(axⁿ) = a·n·x^(n−1): multiply by the power, then drop the power by one. Examples: d/dx(5x³) = 15x², d/dx(7x) = 7, d/dx(4) = 0. (Here n is a positive integer or zero.)
✅ Finding and classifying a turning point
y = x² − 4x + 1   dy/dx = 2x − 4
set 2x − 4 = 0 → x = 2 → y = 4 − 8 + 1 = −3
second derivative = 2 (positive) → minimum at (2, −3)
Classify with the second differential (positive → minimum, negative → maximum) or by inspecting the gradient either side. Points of inflection are not required.

Functions — notation, composite, inverse

⚙️ A function is a machine
f(x) is a rule that takes an input x and returns one output. f(4) means "feed in 4". Set the input and watch it flow through the machines — chained (composite) and reversed (inverse):

Composite gf(x) = g(f(x))
g(7) = 49  ·  gf(x) = (2x + 3)²
Composite fg(x) = f(g(x))
f(4) = 11  ·  fg(x) = 2x² + 3
Inverse f⁻¹(x) = (x − 3)/2
f⁻¹(2) = −0.5
Composite gf(x) means do f first, then g: gf(x) = g(f(x)). Order matters — gf(x) ≠ fg(x) in general (read right-to-left, like nested brackets).
Inverse f⁻¹(x) undoes f. Find it by writing y = f(x), swapping the roles, and making x the subject: from y = 2x + 3 → x = (y − 3)/2 → f⁻¹(x) = (x − 3)/2.
⚠️ f⁻¹ is not 1/f
The "−1" is not a power: f⁻¹(x) is the inverse function (the undo), not 1/f(x). And gf means f acts first — don't reverse the order. (You're not asked for domains/ranges of composite functions at 0580.)
