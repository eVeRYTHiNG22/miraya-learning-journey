Geometry

Intuition-first notes for the whole of Topic 4 (E4.1–E4.8) — the language of shapes, the rules that pin every angle down, similarity, symmetry and the full set of circle theorems. Nearly every rule here is really a picture; the blue angles, indigo polygon and amber circle widgets all move.

01 / 10 · E4.1 · Geometrical terms

The vocabulary — naming everything precisely

Geometry marks are won and lost on exact words. "Isosceles", "chord", "co-interior", "congruent" — each is a promise about a property. Reasons in the exam must quote these terms, so this first section is the dictionary the rest of the topic speaks in.

💡 A name is a property in disguise
Every term below guarantees something. Say a triangle is isosceles and you've promised two equal sides and two equal base angles — for free, no measuring. Naming a shape correctly instantly hands you its facts.

Lines & angles

Core line words — parallel lines never meet (same direction, marked with matching arrows); perpendicular lines cross at 90°; a perpendicular bisector cuts a line in half at 90°; a vertex is a corner where lines meet.

Angles are named with three letters: angle ABC is the angle at B, formed by arms BA and BC. The middle letter is always the vertex — get that wrong and the reason mark goes.

Polygons & solids

A polygon is a closed straight-sided shape; regular means all sides and all angles equal. Key ones: pentagon (5), hexagon (6), octagon (8), decagon (10). Solids have faces (flat surfaces), edges (where faces meet) and vertices (corners): cube, cuboid, prism, cylinder, pyramid, cone, sphere, hemisphere, and a frustum (a cone/pyramid with the tip cut off).

Parts of a circle

Circle words — the radius joins centre to edge; the diameter (= 2 radii) crosses through the centre; the circumference is the edge itself; a chord joins two points on the edge; a tangent just touches at one point; an arc is part of the circumference; a sector is a "pizza slice" (bounded by two radii); a segment is cut off by a chord.
⚠️ Chord vs tangent vs arc
A chord is a straight line inside the circle; an arc is the curved edge; a tangent is a straight line outside touching at one point. These three power every circle theorem in §08–09, so lock them now.
02 / 10 · E4.6 · Angles

Angle rules & parallel lines

Most "find the angle" questions are solved by chaining a few unbreakable facts. Learn these five, then the parallel-line trio, and you can unlock almost any diagram.

💡 Every angle rule is about a full turn or a straight line
Spin all the way round a point and you've turned 360°. Stop halfway — a straight line — and that's 180°. Every rule below is just this fact applied to a picture: angles that together make a line add to 180, angles round a point add to 360.
RuleReason to quote
Angles on a straight line add to 180°angles on a straight line
Angles round a point add to 360°angles at a point

🧩 Why a triangle is always 180°
Tear the three corners off any paper triangle and lay them side by side — they always fit exactly along a straight line. Three corners, one straight line, 180°. It never depends on the triangle's shape.

Parallel lines — the F, Z and C angles

When a straight line (a transversal) cuts two parallel lines, only two angle sizes appear: some value a and its partner 180 − a. Which pairs are equal has names — and you must quote them:

The parallel-line trio — Corresponding (an "F" shape) are equal · Alternate (a "Z" shape) are equal · Co-interior / allied (a "C" or "U" shape) sum to 180° (supplementary).

⚠️ Name the rule, not just the number

✅ The exterior-angle shortcut
The exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles (because both routes fill the same straight line). It saves a whole step — spot it before doing 180° twice.
03 / 10 · E4.6 · Polygon angles

Interior & exterior angles of polygons

💡 Split any polygon into triangles
From one corner, fan lines to every other corner: an n-sided polygon splits into exactly (n − 2) triangles. Each triangle is 180°, so the interior angles must total (n − 2) × 180°. That single trick is the interior-angle formula.
Polygon angle formulas — interior angle sum = (n − 2) × 180°. Sum of exterior angles = 360° always. For a regular n-gon: each exterior = 360 ÷ n, each interior = 180 − (360 ÷ n).
🧩 Why exterior angles always total 360°
Walk once around the edge of the polygon. At every corner you turn by the exterior angle, and by the time you're back facing your start you've turned through one full circle = 360° — no matter how many sides. So the exterior angles always add to 360°.

✅ Go through the exterior angle
To find the number of sides, always use the exterior angle (n = 360 ÷ exterior) — it's one clean division. Find the exterior first (180 − interior) if you're given the interior.
⚠️ "Interior sum" is not "one interior angle"
(n − 2) × 180 is the total of all interior angles. For one angle of a regular polygon you must divide by n. Mixing these up is the classic polygon slip.
04 / 10 · E4.2 · Constructions & nets

Constructions & nets

💡 A pair of compasses draws "all points a fixed distance away"
That's the whole idea of construction. Open the compasses to a length and every point of the arc is exactly that far from the sharp point. To build a triangle from three sides, each arc says "the third corner is this far from here" — where two arcs cross is the only point that satisfies both.

Constructing a triangle from 3 sides (SSS)

Ruler-and-compass triangle — draw the base with a ruler. From one end, open compasses to the 2nd side and draw an arc; from the other end, open to the 3rd side and draw a second arc. Where the arcs cross is the apex. Leave your construction arcs showing — they're worth marks.
  • Draw base AB = the given length with a ruler.
  • Compasses to length AC; centre A; draw an arc above the base.
  • Compasses to length BC; centre B; draw an arc crossing the first.
  • The crossing point is C. Join AC and BC with a ruler.
✅ Accuracy & arcs
Lengths must be within about ±2 mm and angles within ±2°, so use a sharp pencil. Never rub out the arcs — the examiner needs to see the method. A ruler must draw every straight edge. (You can build a rhombus by constructing two such triangles back-to-back.)
⚠️ Not required this cycle
For 0580 (2025–2027) you are not asked to construct perpendicular bisectors or angle bisectors with compasses. You still need the words (§01), but not the construction. Don't waste exam time on them.

Nets

Net — a solid "unfolded flat", so every face is shown in one 2-D drawing. Fold it back up and the edges that meet again must be equal length. Nets are the bridge between a 3-D solid and its surface area.
Cube / cuboid net
6 rectangles in a cross or T. Opposite faces are equal; matching edges that fold together must be the same length.
Prism & pyramid nets
A prism = 2 identical end-faces + rectangles for the sides. A square-based pyramid = 1 square + 4 triangles. Use the net's measurements to find surface area.
⚠️ Not every arrangement folds up
Six squares in a line is not a cube net — faces would overlap. When identifying a net, mentally fold it: each face must land in a different position with no gaps or clashes.
05 / 10 · E4.3 · Scale drawings & bearings

Scale drawings & bearings

💡 A scale is just a multiplier for length
"1 cm represents 5 m" means every centimetre on paper stands for 5 real metres. To go from the map to the world you multiply by the scale; to go from the world to the map you divide. A scale drawing keeps every shape's proportions — it's a similarity (§06) you can measure with a ruler.
a room is drawn to scale 1 cm : 2 m; on paper a wall is 6.5 cm real length = 6.5 × 2 = 13 m

Three-figure bearings

Bearing — a direction given as a three-figure angle measured clockwise from North, from 000° to 360°. Always three digits: due east is 090°, due south 180°, due west 270°, and a small angle like 5° is written 005°.

Back bearing — the bearing of A from B is the reverse direction, so it differs by exactly 180°: add 180 if the bearing is under 180, subtract 180 if it's 180 or more. bearing of B from A = 025° ⇒ A from B = 205°.
from Jaipur, Kota is on a bearing of 210°. What is the bearing of Jaipur from Kota? 210 ≥ 180, so subtract: 210 − 180 = 030°
⚠️ Clockwise, from North, three digits
Bearings are not measured from the east or anticlockwise. Start pointing North, turn clockwise, and always write three figures (a bearing of 7° is 007°, never 7° or 70°).
✅ Draw the North line at every point
In a bearings problem, sketch a small "N" arrow at each point before measuring. Because North lines are parallel, the angles between them are alternate/co-interior angles (§02) — that's how most bearing calculations are actually solved.
06 / 10 · E4.4 · Similarity

Similarity — same shape, scaled size

💡 Similar = a perfect enlargement
Two shapes are similar if one is just the other zoomed in or out: all angles stay equal and every length is multiplied by the same number, the scale factor k. A passport photo enlarged to a poster is similar to the original — nothing is stretched, only scaled.
Similar shapes — equal angles, and corresponding sides in the same ratio k = (a length on B) ÷ (matching length on A). Two triangles are similar if their angles match (AAA) — then their sides are automatically in proportion.
⚠️ The k, k², k³ rule — the heart of this section
If lengths scale by k, then areas scale by k² and volumes scale by k³. Areas are length×length, volumes length×length×length — so the factor gets squared, then cubed.

two similar triangles; small has base 6 cm, large has base 9 cm; small area = 20 cm² k = 9 ÷ 6 = 1.5 → area factor = 1.5² = 2.25
large area = 20 × 2.25 = 45 cm²
two similar water tanks; heights 40 cm and 60 cm; small holds 16 litres k = 60 ÷ 40 = 1.5 → volume factor = 1.5³ = 3.375
large tank = 16 × 3.375 = 54 litres
finding a missing length in similar triangles (base 4→10 cm) k = 10 ÷ 4 = 2.5; a side of 6 cm on the small → 6 × 2.5 = 15 cm on the large
⚠️ Don't use k for area, or k² for volume
Going the "wrong power" is the number-one similarity error. Areas need k², volumes need k³. And going backwards (from area ratio to length ratio) you must square-root: length factor = √(area factor).
✅ Match corresponding sides carefully
Put the two shapes the same way up and pair the side opposite equal angles. Then k = new ÷ old. Getting the ratio upside-down flips an enlargement into a reduction — always sanity-check that bigger shape ⇒ k > 1.
07 / 10 · E4.5 · Symmetry

Symmetry — lines, rotations & planes

💡 Two kinds of "looks the same"
Line symmetry: fold the shape along a line and the two halves match exactly (a mirror line). Rotational symmetry: spin it about its centre and it looks identical before a full turn — the number of times it matches in one full 360° turn is its order.
Order of rotational symmetry — how many positions in one full turn look identical to the start. A shape with no rotational symmetry has order 1 (only the full 360° turn brings it back), not order 0.

🧩 The parallelogram surprise
A parallelogram has no lines of symmetry (folding never matches) yet rotational order 2 — turn it half-way and it lands on itself. Line and rotational symmetry are independent: a shape can have one without the other.

Symmetry of solids

Plane of symmetry — a flat "cut" that slices a solid into two mirror halves. A cuboid (unequal sides) has 3 planes of symmetry; a cylinder has infinitely many through its axis plus one across the middle; a square-based pyramid has 4; a cone has infinitely many through its axis. A cylinder and cone also have a rotational axis of symmetry down the centre.
✅ Link symmetry to the shape's facts
Symmetry explains properties: an isosceles triangle's base angles are equal because its line of symmetry maps one onto the other. Quoting "by symmetry" is a valid reason when a diagram clearly has a mirror line.

The six angle theorems
  • Semicircle: the angle in a semicircle (subtended by a diameter) = 90°.
  • Centre: the angle at the centre = 2 × the angle at the circumference (same arc).
  • Same segment: angles in the same segment (on the same arc) are equal.
  • Cyclic quadrilateral: opposite angles sum to 180°.
  • Tangent–radius: a tangent meets the radius at the point of contact at 90°.
  • Alternate segment: the angle between a tangent and a chord = the angle in the alternate segment.
AB is a diameter, C is on the circle; angle CAB = 34°. Find angle ABC. angle ACB = 90° (angle in a semicircle)
angle ABC = 180 − 90 − 34 = 56° (angle sum of a triangle)
the angle at the centre standing on arc PQ is 140°. Find the angle at the circumference on the same arc. circumference angle = 140 ÷ 2 = 70° (angle at centre = twice angle at circumference)
in a cyclic quadrilateral ABCD, angle A = 85°. Find angle C. angle C = 180 − 85 = 95° (opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral)
⚠️ "Same segment" needs the same arc
Angles are only equal if they stand on the same chord/arc from the same side. Two angles that look similar but sit on different arcs are not equal — always check both angles "see" the same two endpoints.
✅ One theorem per line, with its name
Multi-step circle questions want the reason at each step. Write the theorem's exact wording ("angle in a semicircle", "angles in the same segment") beside every angle you find — that's where the marks live.
09 / 10 · E4.8 · Circle theorems II · Extended

Circle theorems II — chords & tangents

💡 These three come straight from the circle's symmetry
A circle is symmetrical about every diameter, so anything you do on one side is mirrored on the other. Equal chords sit the same distance from the centre, the centre lies on a chord's perpendicular bisector, and the two tangents from a point are equal — all just the mirror at work.
The three symmetry theorems
  • Equal chords are equidistant from the centre (and chords equidistant from the centre are equal).
  • The perpendicular bisector of a chord passes through the centre — equivalently, the perpendicular from the centre bisects the chord.
  • Tangents from an external point are equal in length (and the line to the centre bisects the angle between them).
🧩 The kite hiding in two tangents
Draw both tangents from an external point P to a circle, touching at A and B, plus the radii OA, OB. Because PA = PB and OA = OB, the shape OAPB is a kite — with two right angles (tangent ⟂ radius). Spotting this kite cracks most tangent questions.
a chord of length 16 cm sits in a circle of radius 10 cm. How far is it from the centre? the perpendicular from the centre bisects the chord → half-chord = 8 cm
distance d = √(10² − 8²) = √(100 − 64) = √36 = 6 cm (Pythagoras)
PA and PB are tangents from P; angle APB = 48°. Find angle OAP and angle AOB. angle OAP = 90° (tangent ⟂ radius); PA = PB so OAPB is a kite
angles of kite sum to 360°: angle AOB = 360 − 90 − 90 − 48 = 132°
⚠️ The perpendicular from the centre is the one that bisects
Only the line from the centre that meets the chord at 90° cuts it exactly in half. A random line from the centre does not bisect the chord — you need the perpendicular. This is what lets Pythagoras find the chord–centre distance.
✅ Tangent problems: draw the radii
The instant you see a tangent, draw the radius to the point of contact (right angle) — and if there are two tangents from one point, mark PA = PB and look for the kite. These two moves unlock almost every Extended tangent question.
