01 / 11 · E6.1 · Right-angled triangles

Pythagoras' theorem

In any right-angled triangle, the square drawn on the longest side (the hypotenuse, always opposite the right angle) has exactly the same area as the two smaller squares added together.

💡 The core idea
a² + b² = c², where c is the hypotenuse. To find the hypotenuse you add and square-root; to find a shorter side you subtract and square-root (b = √(c² − a²)). Only ever works when there's a right angle.

Worked example — a leaning ladder

A 5 m ladder rests against a wall with its foot 1.5 m from the base of the wall. How far up the wall does it reach?

ladder = hypotenuse (5), base = 1.5, height = h h² + 1.5² = 5²
subtract to find a shorter side h² = 25 − 2.25 = 22.75
square root h = √22.75 = 4.77 m (3 s.f.)
⚠️ Classic slip
Adding when you should subtract. If the unknown is not the hypotenuse, you must take the difference of the squares. A quick sanity check: the hypotenuse is always the biggest side, so a shorter side can never come out longer than it.
02 / 11 · E6.2 · Sides & angles

The three ratios — SOH-CAH-TOA

Pythagoras links the three sides. Trigonometry brings in the angle. In a right-angled triangle the three sides get names relative to the angle θ you're looking at: the hypotenuse (opposite the right angle), the opposite (facing θ), and the adjacent (the remaining side, next to θ).

SOH · CAH · TOA
sin θ = O / H   cos θ = A / H   tan θ = O / A
🪜 A picture you know
A ramp at a shop. Make the ramp steeper (bigger angle) and for the same length it rises higher — sin θ grows. Flatten it (smaller angle) and it barely rises but stretches far — cos θ grows. Drag the slider below and watch the two legs trade off while the ramp (hypotenuse) stays the same length.

Choosing the right ratio

Label the sides (O, A, H), see which two the question uses, and pick the ratio that pairs them. To find an angle, use the inverse: θ = sin⁻¹(O/H), etc.

Find side: opp = ?, angle 32°, hyp 9 sin 32° = O/9 → O = 9 sin 32° = 4.77
Find angle: opp 4, adj 7 tan θ = 4/7 → θ = tan⁻¹(0.5714) = 29.7°
✅ Method that never fails
1. Mark the right angle and the angle you care about. 2. Label O, A, H. 3. Underline the two sides in play. 4. Pick SOH / CAH / TOA that uses exactly those two. Round angles to 1 d.p. as the syllabus asks.
03 / 11 · E6.2 · Applications

Elevation, depression & bearings

These are just right-angled-triangle problems dressed in words. The skill is drawing the triangle correctly from the description.

Angle of elevation
Look up from the horizontal to an object. Measured from a horizontal line at your eye up to the line of sight.
Angle of depression
Look down from the horizontal to an object. The angle of depression from A to B equals the angle of elevation from B to A (alternate angles between parallel horizontals).

Worked example — height of a tree

Standing 30 m from the foot of a tree, Meera measures the angle of elevation of the top as 35°. How tall is the tree (eye level ≈ ground)?

opposite = height, adjacent = 30, angle 35° tan 35° = h / 30
rearrange h = 30 × tan 35° = 30 × 0.7002 = 21.0 m (1 d.p.)
Bearings
A bearing is a direction measured clockwise from North, always written with three figures — e.g. due East is 090°, South-West is 225°, and a small angle like 7° is written 007°.
⚠️ Two classic slips
(1) Bearings need 3 digits — write 040°, not 40°. (2) "Perpendicular distance from a point to a line is the shortest distance" — it's the perpendicular (right-angle) drop, not a slanted one, so these often hide a right-angled triangle you must spot.

Exact trig values

For special angles you're expected to write the value exactly (surd or fraction) with no calculator. This is a "recall" mark.

🧠 The "0 1 2 3 4" hand trick
Write 0 1 2 3 4 under the angles 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°. For sin take √(that number) ÷ 2: √0/2, √1/2, √2/2, √3/2, √4/2 = 0, ½, √2⁄2, √3⁄2, 1. cos is the same list reversed. And tan x = sin x ÷ cos x.
✅ Where marks are won
cos 60° = ½ and sin 30° = ½ are equal (complementary angles), and tan 45° = 1 exactly. If a "solve" question gives cos x = ½, you should instantly know 60° is one answer.

06 / 11 · E6.4 · The key skill

Solving trig equations

Your calculator's sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹ give only one angle, but a line y = k usually cuts the wave twice in 0°–360°. Find the calculator angle, then use symmetry for its partner.

The three partner rules (0° ≤ x ≤ 360°)
If the calculator gives a first angle x₁, the second solution is:
  • sin x = k → second angle = 180° − x₁
  • cos x = k → second angle = 360° − x₁
  • tan x = k → second angle = 180° + x₁
Then add 360° to any negative answer and keep only those in 0°–360°.

Worked example — 2 cos x + 1 = 0, for 0° ≤ x ≤ 360°

rearrange cos x = −½
calculator angle from +½ cos⁻¹(½) = 60°
cos negative → answers left & right of 180° 180 − 60 = 120°, 180 + 60 = 240°
answer x = 120°, 240°
⚠️ No solution
A line above y = 1 or below y = −1 never meets the sine/cosine wave, so sin x = 2 has no solution. Tangent reaches every value, so tan x = k always has two answers in 0°–360°.
07 / 11 · E6.5 · Any triangle

The sine rule

Real triangles aren't always right-angled. The sine rule links each side to the sine of the angle opposite it, so it works on any triangle. It's given in the exam's List of formulas — the skill is knowing when to use it.

Sine rule
a / sin A = b / sin B = c / sin C
Use the "side-up" version to find a side; flip it (sin A / a = …) to find an angle. Side a is always opposite angle A.
💡 When to reach for it
Use the sine rule when you have a matching side–angle pair (a side and the angle facing it) plus one more piece. That "opposite pair" is the signature — if you don't have one, use the cosine rule instead.

Worked example

In triangle ABC, angle A = 40°, angle C = 65° and side c = 12 cm. Find side a.

side up (finding a side) a / sin 40° = 12 / sin 65°
rearrange a = 12 × sin 40° ÷ sin 65°
evaluate a = 12 × 0.6428 ÷ 0.9063 = 8.51 cm (3 s.f.)
⚠️ The ambiguous case (SSA)
When you use the sine rule to find an angle, remember sin θ = sin(180° − θ) — so there can be two valid triangles (an acute and an obtuse answer). Always check whether the obtuse option still fits (the three angles must total 180°).
08 / 11 · E6.5 · Any triangle

The cosine rule

When the sine rule can't start — because you have no opposite side–angle pair — the cosine rule steps in. It's Pythagoras with a correction term for the angle, and it's also given in the exam.

Cosine rule
a² = b² + c² − 2bc·cos A  → find a side
cos A = (b² + c² − a²) / (2bc)  → find an angle
💡 Two triggers
Use the cosine rule when you know two sides and the angle between them (to find the third side), or all three sides (to find any angle). If A = 90°, cos 90° = 0 and it collapses back to a² = b² + c² — plain Pythagoras.

Worked example — find a side

Two paths leave a junction at 58° to each other, one 7 km long, the other 9 km. How far apart are their far ends?

two sides + included angle a² = 7² + 9² − 2·7·9·cos 58°
evaluate a² = 49 + 81 − 126 × 0.5299 = 63.23
square root a = 7.95 km (3 s.f.)

Worked example — find an angle

A triangle has sides 6, 7 and 8. Find the angle opposite the side of length 6.

cos A = (b² + c² − a²)/(2bc) cos A = (49 + 64 − 36)/(2·7·8) = 77/112
= 0.6875 → inverse cosine A = cos⁻¹(0.6875) = 46.6° (1 d.p.)
09 / 11 · E6.5 · Area

Area of a triangle = ½ ab sin C

The familiar ½ × base × height needs a perpendicular height. When you instead know two sides and the angle between them, this formula (also given in the exam) gets the area directly.

Area formula
Area = ½ · a · b · sin C
where C is the angle between the two sides a and b (the included angle).
💡 Why it works
The height of the triangle is a·sin C (drop a perpendicular from the tip — it's the opposite side of a little right-angled triangle). Put that into ½ × base × height with base b and you get ½ ab sin C.

Worked example

A triangular plot has two sides of 9 m and 12 m meeting at 43°. Find its area.

two sides + included angle Area = ½ × 9 × 12 × sin 43°
evaluate = 54 × 0.6820 = 36.8 m² (3 s.f.)
✅ Include the right angle
The angle must sit between the two sides you multiply. If you're given a different angle, either relabel or find the included angle first (angles in a triangle sum to 180°).
10 / 11 · E6.6 · Three dimensions

Pythagoras & trig in 3D

3D problems are solved by finding a right-angled triangle inside the solid and working in that flat plane. The trick is to sketch the triangle on its own before touching the calculator.

Space diagonal of a cuboid
d = √(l² + w² + h²) — Pythagoras used twice: first across the base (√(l²+w²)), then up to the far corner.

Worked example — angle between a line and a plane

A box measures 6 cm × 4 cm × 3 cm. Find the angle the space diagonal makes with the base.

base diagonal first (Pythagoras on the base) √(6² + 4²) = √52 = 7.211 cm
angle θ between diagonal and base: opposite = height 3, adjacent = base diagonal tan θ = 3 / 7.211
inverse tan θ = tan⁻¹(0.4160) = 22.6° (1 d.p.)
✅ The angle-between-line-and-plane recipe
1. Drop the line straight down onto the plane to get its "shadow" (projection). 2. The required angle is between the line and that shadow. 3. It lives in a right-angled triangle — find its sides with Pythagoras, then use SOH-CAH-TOA.
