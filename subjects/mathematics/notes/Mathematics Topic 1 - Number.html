01 / 12 · 1.1 Types of Number

The number family — who lives where

Numbers come in nested families. Start with counting, then keep asking "what new number do I need?" — each answer grows the family outward.

🪆 The nesting-doll idea
Natural numbers (1, 2, 3…) are for counting. Allow zero and negatives and you get the integers (…−2, −1, 0, 1, 2…). Allow fractions of integers and you get the rationals. The few numbers that can't be written as a fraction (like √2 and π) are irrational. Rationals + irrationals together = the real numbers. Each family sits inside the next.

⚠️ The classic prime slips
1 is NOT prime (it has only one factor, not two). 2 IS prime — and it is the only even prime. Every other even number is divisible by 2, so it has 2 as an extra factor.

Factors, multiples, HCF & LCM

A factor divides into a number exactly; a multiple is in its times-table. Break a number into its prime factors and finding the highest common factor (HCF) and lowest common multiple (LCM) of two numbers becomes mechanical.

✅ HCF & LCM from prime factors
24 = 2³ × 3  and  36 = 2² × 3².
HCF = the shared primes to their lowest powers = 2² × 3 = 12.
LCM = all primes to their highest powers = 2³ × 3² = 72.
Quick check: HCF × LCM = 12 × 72 = 864 = 24 × 36. ✓
Reciprocal — the number you multiply by to get 1. The reciprocal of n is 1/n; the reciprocal of a fraction is that fraction flipped: the reciprocal of 2/3 is 3/2.

A ∪ B — the union
Everything in A or B (or both). With ξ = {1,…,10}, A = even = {2,4,6,8,10}, B = multiples of 3 = {3,6,9}: A ∪ B = {2,3,4,6,8,9,10}, so n(A ∪ B) = 7.
⚠️ Set notation to memorise
∈ "is an element of"  ·  ∉ "is not in"  ·  ∪ union (or)  ·  ∩ intersection (and)  ·  A′ complement (not A)  ·  ⊆ subset  ·  n(A) number of elements  ·  ∅ empty set.
⚠️ Union vs intersection
∪ (union) is the bigger region — "in either" — its symbol opens upward like a cup that holds more. ∩ (intersection) is the small overlap — "in both". Read ∩ as "and", ∪ as "or".
03 / 12 · 1.3 & 1.7 Powers, Roots & Indices

Indices — repeated multiplication, shrunk to shorthand

An index (power) counts how many times you multiply a base by itself: 2⁴ = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 16. Every index law comes straight from that meaning — you never need to memorise them blind.

🧱 Why the laws must be true
2³ × 2⁴ is (2·2·2) × (2·2·2·2) — seven 2's multiplied — so it's 2⁷. That's the whole reason you add powers when multiplying. Divide and you cancel 2's, so you subtract.

✅ Fractional powers, worked
16^(3/4) = "fourth-root, then cube" = (⁴√16)³ = 2³ = 8. The denominator is the root; the numerator is the power. Do the root first to keep the numbers small.
⚠️ a⁰ = 1, not 0
Anything (except 0) to the power zero is 1. It follows from the divide law: aⁿ ÷ aⁿ = aⁿ⁻ⁿ = a⁰, but a number divided by itself is 1. So a⁰ must equal 1.

05 / 12 · 1.4 Fractions, Decimals & Percentages

Three costumes for the same number

A fraction, a decimal and a percentage are just three ways of writing one value. Being able to switch instantly is half of the exam.

🔁 One value, three faces
3/8 = 0.375 = 37.5%. To go fraction → decimal, divide (3 ÷ 8). Decimal → percentage, × 100. Percentage → fraction, put it over 100 and simplify. "Per cent" literally means "per hundred".

✅ Recurring decimal → fraction (Extended)
Write 0.4444… as a fraction. Let x = 0.444…, so 10x = 4.444…. Subtract: 9x = 4, giving x = 4/9. For two recurring digits (e.g. 0.27̇), multiply by 100 instead: 0.2727… = 27/99 = 3/11.

The four operations on fractions

  • × : multiply tops, multiply bottoms — 2/3 × 4/5 = 8/15.
  • ÷ : flip the second fraction and multiply — 2/3 ÷ 4/5 = 2/3 × 5/4 = 10/12 = 5/6.
  • + / − : make a common denominator first — 1/2 + 1/3 = 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6.
06 / 12 · 1.5 & 1.6 Ordering & Order of Operations

Ordering numbers & the order you work

Comparing sizes

Use the inequality symbols to rank quantities by magnitude:

⚠️ The five comparison symbols
= equal  ·  ≠ not equal  ·  > greater than  ·  < less than  ·  ⩾ greater than or equal  ·  ⩽ less than or equal. The wide-open end always points at the bigger value.
⚠️ Negatives flip your instincts
−7 < −2, even though 7 > 2. The further left on the number line, the smaller the value. Always picture the number line for negatives.

BIDMAS — the order of operations

When a calculation mixes operations, there's a fixed running order: Brackets, Indices, Division/Multiplication (left to right), then Addition/Subtraction.

✅ Why 3 + 4 × 2 = 11, not 14
Multiplication runs before addition: 3 + (4 × 2) = 3 + 8 = 11. Add brackets to override the order: (3 + 4) × 2 = 14. Bigger example: 2 + 3 × (8 − 2²) = 2 + 3 × (8 − 4) = 2 + 12 = 14.

🥤 Direct vs inverse proportion
Direct: double the squashes, double the cost — y ∝ x, a straight line through the origin. Inverse: twice as many workers, half the time — y ∝ 1/x. More of one means less of the other, and the product stays constant.
✅ Rates & "best buy"
A rate compares two different units. Average speed: 150 km ÷ 2.5 h = 60 km/h. Best buy: a 500 g pack of dal for ₹90 works out at ₹180/kg, but a 750 g pack for ₹126 is only ₹168/kg — the bigger pack is better value. Always compare cost per single unit.
08 / 12 · 1.13 Percentages

Percentages — the multiplier trick

Almost every percentage question becomes easy once you think in multipliers. A 25% rise is "× 1.25"; a 30% fall is "× 0.70". One multiplication does what used to take three steps.

Increase by 25%
100% + 25% = 125% = × 1.25. So 80 → 80 × 1.25 = 100.
Decrease by 30%
100% − 30% = 70% = × 0.70. So 60 → 60 × 0.70 = 42.
Percentage of an amount — turn the % into a decimal and multiply: 15% of 240 = 0.15 × 240 = 36.
Percentage change — (change ÷ original) × 100. From 40 to 50: change = 10, so (10 ÷ 40) × 100 = 25% increase.
⚠️ Reverse percentage — the favourite trap
A shirt costs ₹960 after a 20% increase. The original is not 960 − 20%. The ₹960 represents 120% of the original, so divide: 960 ÷ 1.2 = ₹800. Whenever a price already includes a change, you divide by the multiplier to get back.

Compound growth — interest on the interest

With simple interest you earn the same amount every year. With compound interest each year's interest joins the balance, so next year you earn interest on a bigger pile — and the gap explodes over time. Drag the sliders and watch the two paths separate:

Compound interest — A = P(1 + r/100)ⁿ. With P = ₹500, r = 8%, n = 10: 500 × 1.08¹⁰ = ₹1079.46 — that's ₹179.46 more than the ₹900 a simple-interest account would total (which earns just ₹400 interest).

✅ Don't do it year-by-year if you can avoid it
For "after n years" questions the power formula is one calculator line. Only step through year-by-year if the rate changes from year to year. Round money to 2 decimal places at the very end, not partway through.
10 / 12 · 1.9, 1.10 & 1.13 Estimation, Bounds & Surds

Rounding, bounds & exact surds

Estimation

To estimate, round every number to 1 significant figure and work with those. It's a fast sanity-check that your real answer is sensible.

✅ Quick estimate
4.8 × 19.6 ≈ 5 × 20 = 100 (true value 94.08 — close, so the calculator answer is trustworthy).

Limits of accuracy (upper & lower bounds)

A measurement rounded to the nearest unit hides a range. A length given as 24 cm to the nearest cm could really be anything from 23.5 cm up to (but not including) 24.5 cm — that's ± half the rounding unit.

✅ Bounds in a calculation (Extended)
A rectangle measures 12 cm by 8 cm, each to the nearest cm. For the largest possible area, use the upper bounds: 12.5 × 8.5 = 106.25 cm². Smallest area uses the lower bounds: 11.5 × 7.5 = 86.25 cm².

Surds (Extended)

A surd is a root left in exact form, like √2. Keeping surds exact avoids rounding errors. Simplify by pulling out the largest square factor.

√ Splitting and rationalising
Simplify: √12 = √4 × √3 = 2√3  and  √50 = √25 × √2 = 5√2.
Rationalise (clear the root from the bottom) by multiplying top and bottom by that root: 1/√2 = √2/2  and  6/√3 = 6√3/3 = 2√3.
⚠️ √a + √b ≠ √(a+b)
You can only combine surds with the same root: 2√3 + 5√3 = 7√3. But √2 + √3 does not simplify, and √4 + √9 = 2 + 3 = 5, which is not √13. Splitting only works for × and ÷, never + and −.
11 / 12 · 1.14–1.17 Money, Time & the Calculator

Everyday number — time, money & exchange

Time

Time is base-60, not base-10 — 0.5 hours is 30 minutes, not 50. Use the 24-hour clock to avoid am/pm slips: 3:45 pm = 15:45.

✅ Time intervals
From 09:50 to 14:20: 09:50 → 14:00 is 4 h 10 min, then +20 min = 4 h 30 min. Count up to the next whole hour first — it beats subtracting in your head.

Money & exchange rates

An exchange rate is just a multiplier between currencies. To convert, multiply; to convert back, divide by the same rate.

✅ Converting currency
With $1 = ₹80: convert $250 to rupees → 250 × 80 = ₹20,000. Going the other way, ₹20,000 → 20000 ÷ 80 = $250. Money answers get 2 decimal places.
⚠️ Using the calculator well
Type long calculations in one go using brackets and the fraction key, so the calculator obeys BIDMAS for you. Don't round partway through — keep full accuracy and round only the final answer. Always glance back: does the answer's size match your estimate?
