01 / 10 · E7.1 · The big picture

Object, image, and the four moves

A transformation is a rule that picks up a shape (the object) and redraws it somewhere else (the image). The syllabus asks for exactly four of them, and every exam question is one of these four.

💡 The core idea
Three of the four moves only relocate the shape — they never change its size or lengths, so object and image are congruent (identical, just repositioned): reflection (flip), rotation (turn), translation (slide). The fourth — enlargement — resizes it, so object and image are similar (same shape, different size).
📷 A picture you know
Think of a photocopier. "Copy" at 100% and slide the paper around the glass = translation; flip the sheet over = reflection; turn it a quarter-turn = rotation. Hit "150% zoom" and you've done an enlargement — same picture, bigger. Only zoom changes the size.
Congruent moves
Reflection, rotation, translation. Lengths and angles are unchanged; the image is the same size as the object. Orientation may flip (reflection) or turn (rotation).
Similar move
Enlargement. Every length is multiplied by the scale factor k; area is multiplied by k². Angles stay the same, so the shape is unchanged — only its size.
✅ The whole topic in one sentence
Two skills earn every mark: (1) perform a transformation (draw the image accurately with a ruler), and (2) describe a transformation fully (name it and state its numbers). Section 6 is the checklist for skill 2 — where most marks are lost.
02 / 10 · E7.1 · Flip

Reflection

A reflection flips the shape over a mirror line. Every point of the image is the same perpendicular distance from the mirror as the matching point of the object — just on the other side.

🪞 A picture you know
Fold the paper along the mirror line. The object and its image land exactly on top of each other. Points sitting on the mirror line don't move at all (they're on the fold).
How to reflect a point
Drop a line from the point perpendicular to the mirror, then continue the same distance past it. For the common mirror lines, learn these coordinate rules cold:
Mirror lineRuleExample
x-axis (y = 0)(x, y) → (x, −y)(3, 2) → (3, −2)
y-axis (x = 0)(x, y) → (−x, y)(3, 2) → (−3, 2)
y = x(x, y) → (y, x)(3, 2) → (2, 3)
y = −x(x, y) → (−y, −x)(3, 2) → (−2, −3)
x = a (vertical)(x, y) → (2a − x, y)mirror x=5: (3,2)→(7,2)
y = b (horizontal)(x, y) → (x, 2b − y)mirror y=1: (3,2)→(3,0)

Worked example

Reflect triangle P(2,1), Q(5,1), R(2,3) in the line y = x.

rule for y = x is (x, y) → (y, x): just swap the coordinates
P(2,1) → P′(1,2) · Q(5,1) → Q′(1,5) · R(2,3) → R′(3,2)
⚠️ Classic slips
(1) Reflecting in the wrong axis — always read the mirror's equation, don't assume. (2) Measuring distance slanted instead of perpendicular to the mirror. (3) Forgetting that a reflection reverses orientation — the image is a mirror-image (like a left hand becoming a right hand).
03 / 10 · E7.1 · Turn

Rotation

A rotation turns the shape about a fixed point — the centre of rotation — through an angle, in a given direction. In IGCSE the angle is always a multiple of 90°.

🕐 A picture you know
A clock hand. The tip sweeps round but the pin at the centre never moves. That pin is the centre of rotation; every point of the shape keeps its distance from the pin and just swings round by the angle.
Rules about the origin (0, 0)
90° anticlockwise: (x, y) → (−y, x)
90° clockwise: (x, y) → (y, −x)
180° (either way): (x, y) → (−x, −y)

Worked example

Rotate the point (4, 2) about the origin.

90° anticlockwise → (−y, x) (4, 2) → (−2, 4)
90° clockwise → (y, −x) (4, 2) → (2, −4)
180° → (−x, −y) (4, 2) → (−4, −2)
✅ Finding an unknown centre
If you must find the centre of rotation from an object and its image: join two matching points to their images and draw the perpendicular bisector of each join — the centre is where the bisectors cross. In the exam, tracing paper is the fast check: pin your pencil at a trial centre and rotate until the object lands on the image.
⚠️ Direction matters
Anticlockwise is the positive direction (the way a clock's hands do not go). A 90° clockwise turn is the same image as a 270° anticlockwise turn — but you must still state a direction unless the angle is 180°.
04 / 10 · E7.1 · Resize

Enlargement

An enlargement resizes a shape from a fixed point — the centre of enlargement — by a scale factor k. Draw a ray from the centre through a vertex; the image vertex sits k times as far along that ray.

💡 A picture you know
A projector. The bulb is the centre of enlargement; light rays fan out through the slide and hit the screen. Push the screen twice as far from the bulb and the picture is twice as wide and twice as tall — scale factor 2. The rays are the straight lines you draw from the centre.
The ray rule
image = C + k · (point − C), where C is the centre.
Centre the origin makes it simple: (x, y) → (kx, ky). Every length is multiplied by k; every area by k².

Positive, fractional and negative scale factors

k > 1
Image is bigger, same side of the centre.
SF 3, centre (1,1), vertex (2,3): image = (1,1)+3·(1,2) = (4,7).
0 < k < 1
Image is smaller (still an "enlargement"!).
SF ½, centre O, vertex (6,4): → (3,2).
k < 0
Image is on the opposite side of the centre and inverted.
SF −2, centre O, vertex (2,1): → (−4,−2).

A negative scale factor sends the rays backwards through the centre, so the image turns up on the far side, upside-down, and |k| times as big:

✅ Reading the scale factor off a diagram
Count how far a vertex is from the centre, then how far its image is: k = (image distance) ÷ (object distance). If the image is on the opposite side of the centre, k is negative. Confirm with a second vertex.
⚠️ Two traps
(1) "Enlargement" includes shrinking — a fractional scale factor is still an enlargement, never call it a "reduction". (2) Area scales by k², not k: an SF-3 enlargement has 9× the area, not 3×.
05 / 10 · E7.1 → E7.2 · Slide

Translation & the column vector

A translation slides every point of the shape the same distance in the same direction — no turning, no flipping, no resizing. The slide is recorded as a column vector.

The column vector
A translation is written (ab): the top number a is the move across (right +, left −), the bottom number b is the move up/down (up +, down −). So (x, y) → (x + a, y + b).
🧩 A picture you know
Dragging an app icon across a phone screen, or sliding a carrom coin. It ends up somewhere new but points the same way and is the same size. The column vector is just the instruction "go this far right, this far up" — and it's the same instruction wherever on the grid you start.

Worked example

Translate the triangle with a vertex at (5, 2) by the vector (−34).

add the vector: x + (−3), y + 4
(5, 2) → (5 − 3, 2 + 4) = (2, 6)
🧠 The bridge to vectors
A translation is a vector. Everything in the rest of this topic — adding, subtracting, magnitude — is just doing arithmetic on these (ab) instructions. To reverse a translation, negate both parts.
⚠️ Describe it as a vector
To describe a translation you must give the column vector. Writing "moved 3 left and 4 up" in words, or swapping the two numbers, loses the mark. Top = across, bottom = up.

✅ One transformation, one name
"Describe the single transformation" means give one — never a chain. And never mix two: "reflection and rotation" scores zero. If the mapping doesn't fit one of the four cleanly, re-check the image; you've likely misread a coordinate.

Combinations (Extended)

Extended questions may combine transformations — do the first, then apply the second to that image — and then ask for the single equivalent transformation.

🔗 Worth memorising
A reflection in the x-axis followed by a reflection in the y-axis is the same as a single rotation of 180° about the origin: (x,y) → (x,−y) → (−x,−y). Two reflections in intersecting lines always combine to a rotation about their crossing point.
07 / 10 · E7.2 · Two dimensions

Vectors — add, subtract, scale

A vector has magnitude (length) and direction. It's written as a column vector (xy), or with a bold/underlined letter a, or as AB (the journey from A to B). Position doesn't matter — the same instruction anywhere on the grid is the same vector.

Arithmetic on vectors — do each part separately
add: component-wise (a+c over b+d)  ·  subtract: (a−c over b−d)  ·  scalar × : k·(x over y) = (kx over ky)
🚶 A picture you know
A vector is a journey: "4 blocks east, 1 block north". To add two journeys, do the first, then start the second from where you finished — tip to tail — and the single arrow from your start to your end is the sum. Drag the sliders: the blue arrow is a, the amber arrow is b laid on a's tip, and the green arrow is the resultant a + b.

⚠️ What subtraction means
a − b is a + (−b) — reverse b (flip its arrow), then add tip-to-tail. On a diagram, a − b is the vector that goes from the tip of b to the tip of a. Muddling the order flips the sign of both components.
08 / 10 · E7.3 · Length

Magnitude of a vector

The magnitude (or modulus) of a vector is its length, written |a| or |AB|. The components form the two short sides of a right-angled triangle, so magnitude is just Pythagoras.

Magnitude
For (xy):   |v| = √(x² + y²). It is always ≥ 0, and |AB| equals the straight-line distance from A to B.
🐦 A picture you know
"As the crow flies." Walk 3 m east then 4 m north and you've walked 7 m — but you're only 5 m from the start in a straight line: √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5. Magnitude is that direct distance. Drag the sliders to see the triangle rebuild.

Worked example — distance between two points

Find |AB| where A(1, 2) and B(4, 6).

AB = (end − start) = (4−1, 6−2) AB = (3, 4)
magnitude = √(x² + y²) |AB| = √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5
⚠️ Square, add, then root
|v| is not x + y. Square both components first, add, and only then take the square root. A negative component is fine — squaring removes the sign: |(−6, 8)| = √(36 + 64) = 10.
09 / 10 · E7.4 · Reasoning & proof

Vector geometry

The hardest marks in Topic 7 ask you to express a vector in terms of two others and then reason: show lines are parallel, points are collinear, or split a line in a ratio. The whole toolkit is one idea plus one rule.

Position vectors
Give every point an arrow from the origin: a = OA, b = OB. Then to travel A→B, go back to O and out to B: AB = AO + OB = −a + b = b − a. The midpoint of AB has position vector ½(a + b).
🧠 The one rule that unlocks it
AB = b − a — always (where you're going) − (where you start). Get this the right way round and every "express in terms of a and b" question falls out.

Worked example — express & find

OA = a and OB = b. M is the midpoint of AB, and P lies on AB with AP : PB = 1 : 2.

(a) AB AB = b − a
(b) OM = OA + ½AB = a + ½(b − a) = ½a + ½b = ½(a + b)
(c) AP is ⅓ of AB (1 part of 1+2) OP = a + ⅓(b − a) = ⅔a + ⅓b

Proving parallel & collinear

Parallel
Two vectors are parallel when one is a scalar multiple of the other: u = k v.
e.g. (2, 3) and (4, 6) = 2(2, 3) — parallel.
Collinear
Three points lie on one straight line when two vectors between them are parallel and share a point.
If AC = k·AB, then A, B, C are collinear.

Worked example — show three points are collinear

Show that A(1, 2), B(3, 5) and C(7, 11) lie on a straight line.

AB = B − A AB = (2, 3)
AC = C − A AC = (6, 9)
is AC a multiple of AB? (6, 9) = 3 × (2, 3) = 3·AB ✓
AC ∥ AB and both pass through A → A, B, C are collinear
✅ How to write a vector proof
1. Express each needed vector in terms of a and b (or as columns). 2. Factor out the scalar to show one = k × other. 3. Write the conclusion in words — "parallel", or "collinear because they share point A". State the ratio of lengths too: AC = 3·AB means AC is 3 times as long.
