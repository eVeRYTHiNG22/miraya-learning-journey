Stoichiometry

The chemist's accounting. Atoms are too small to count one by one, so we weigh them and count by the mole. Every calculation in this topic — masses, gases, solutions, yields — comes back to one idea: a balanced equation is a recipe in particles. Tap the teal panels — they compute live.

01 / 11 · 3.1–3.3 · The big idea

Why chemists count by weighing

A balanced equation says how many particles react: 2 hydrogen molecules for every 1 oxygen. But you can't pick up two molecules — a single grain of sand already holds billions of billions of atoms. So chemists do something clever: instead of counting particles, they weigh them, because equal numbers of particles always have masses in a fixed ratio.

🧮 The whole topic in one line
Chemistry happens by number, but we measure by mass. Stoichiometry is just the bridge between the two — turning grams you can weigh on a balance into the numbers of particles the equation actually cares about, and back again. Master that bridge and every calculation in Topic 3 is the same calculation.
🌾 Counting rice by the sack
No shopkeeper in Chennai counts rice grain by grain — they sell it by the kilo, knowing roughly how many grains that is. The mole is the chemist's version: a fixed, enormous count (6.02 × 10²³) that you reach for by weighing, never by counting. "One mole of carbon" is to atoms what "one sack of rice" is to grains.

This section sets up three tools you'll use everywhere: formulae (the recipe for one particle), relative mass (how heavy that particle is), and the mole (how we turn mass into number). The rest of the topic is just applying them.

02 / 11 · 3.1 Formulae

Chemical formulae — the recipe for one particle

A chemical formula tells you which atoms, and how many of each, make up one unit of a substance. Subscripts multiply only the symbol before them; brackets multiply everything inside.

Molecular formula — the actual number and type of each different atom in one molecule. For example H₂SO₄ = 2 H, 1 S, 4 O; Ca(OH)₂ = 1 Ca, 2 O, 2 H (the bracket doubles the OH).

Deducing a formula from charges (ionic compounds)

Ionic compounds aren't molecules — they're giant lattices — so we write the simplest ratio of ions that makes the overall charge zero. Trick: the size of each charge becomes the subscript of the other ion ("swap and drop"), then simplify.

NH₄+SO₄2−2 (+1) + 1 (−2) = 0(NH₄)₂SO₄
⚠️ Brackets are not optional
When you need more than one of a group of atoms (a polyatomic ion like OH−, NO₃−, SO₄2−, NH₄+), you must use brackets: Ca(OH)₂, Al(NO₃)₃, (NH₄)₂SO₄. Writing "CaOH₂" or "CaO₂H₂" changes the recipe and loses the mark.
✅ Extended — empirical formula
The empirical formula is the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (or ions) in a compound. For an ionic compound the formula you write is the empirical formula. For molecules it can differ: glucose is molecular C₆H₁₂O₆ but empirical CH₂O; ethane is C₂H₆ but empirical CH₃.
⚠️ State the common formulae from memory
You're expected to recall these: water H₂O, carbon dioxide CO₂, ammonia NH₃, methane CH₄, hydrochloric acid HCl, sulfuric acid H₂SO₄, nitric acid HNO₃, sodium hydroxide NaOH, calcium carbonate CaCO₃. Diatomic elements come as molecules: H₂, O₂, N₂, Cl₂.

Equations — and why they must balance

A word equation names reactants → products. A symbol equation uses formulae and must be balanced: atoms are never created or destroyed in a reaction, so every element has the same number of atoms on both sides. You balance by putting numbers (coefficients) in front of formulae — never by changing a subscript, which would change the substance itself.

⚖️ Balancing = conservation of atoms
The arrow is not "becomes" — it's "rearranges into". The same atoms that walked in must walk out, just bonded differently. So if the left has 4 H atoms, the right must have 4 H atoms. Balancing is bookkeeping for atoms, nothing more.

Try it. Combustion of methane: set the four coefficients so every element matches. The beam levels and turns green only when C, H and O all balance at once:

State symbols

Add the physical state of each substance in the reaction: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous (dissolved in water). Example: CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g).

Ionic equations [Extended]

In solution, ionic compounds are split into free ions. An ionic equation shows only the particles that actually change — the rest are spectator ions and are cancelled from both sides.

Worked example — precipitating silver chloride
Full: AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq).
Na+ and NO₃− are unchanged spectators, so the ionic equation is just Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s). Check it balances for both atoms and charge (+1 −1 = 0 each side).
✅ Two-second balance check
After balancing, recount each element on a scrap of paper. For ionic equations also check the total charge is equal on both sides — a common dropped mark. The number of atoms and the charge must both conserve.
04 / 11 · 3.2 Relative masses

Relative atomic & molecular mass

Atoms are far too light to weigh in grams individually, so chemists compare them to a standard. Everything is measured against the carbon-12 atom.

Relative atomic mass, Ar — the average mass of the isotopes of an element, compared to 1/12 of the mass of one atom of carbon-12. It's an average because most elements are mixtures of isotopes (e.g. chlorine's Ar = 35.5). It has no units — it's a ratio.
Relative molecular mass, Mr — the sum of the Ar values of all the atoms in the formula. For ionic compounds (which have no molecules) the same number is called the relative formula mass, still written Mr.

Working out Mr

Just add up the Ar of every atom in the formula:

⚠️ Brackets and subscripts trip people up
In Ca(OH)₂ the subscript 2 multiplies both the O and the H inside the bracket — that's 2 O and 2 H, not 1 each. Mis-reading brackets is the most common Mr error. (Useful Ar values: H 1, C 12, N 14, O 16, Na 23, Mg 24, S 32, Cl 35.5, K 39, Ca 40, Fe 56, Cu 64.)
05 / 11 · 3.2 Relative masses

Reacting masses by simple proportion [Core]

You can solve many problems without the mole, using only the balanced equation and Mr values. The balanced equation gives a fixed mass ratio; scale it up or down like a recipe.

🍪 Scaling a recipe
If 100 g of flour makes 8 biscuits, then 25 g makes 2 — you just scaled by ¼. A reaction is identical: once you know the mass ratio from the equation, every other amount is a simple proportion of it.
Worked example — burning magnesium ribbon
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO. From Mr: 2 Mg = 2×24 = 48 g makes 2 MgO = 2×40 = 80 g.
So how much MgO from 12 g of Mg? Scale by 12/48 = ¼ → 20 g of MgO. (Check: 48 → 80 means ×5/3, and 12 × 5/3 = 20. ✓)
✅ The 3-step method
1. Balance the equation. 2. Find the Mr of the substance you're given and the one you want, multiplied by their coefficients. 3. Set up the proportion: (want) = (given) × ratio. This Core method needs no moles at all.

The mole — turning mass into number [Extended]

The mole (mol) — the unit of amount of substance. One mole of any particle contains 6.02 × 10²³ of them (atoms, ions, molecules, electrons…). That number is the Avogadro constant, NA.

The magic of the mole: the mass of one mole of a substance, in grams, equals its Ar or Mr. So one mole of carbon weighs 12 g, one mole of water weighs 18 g. That single fact connects the balance in your hand to the particle count in the equation:

The key relationship  →  amount (mol) = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol), i.e. n = m / M. Rearrange for whichever quantity is unknown. To get the number of particles: N = n × 6.02 × 10²³.

Pick a substance and slide the mass. The converter does n = m/M and then multiplies by NA — watch how a weighable mass becomes an almost unimaginable particle count:

⚠️ "Mole" is a number, not a mass
A mole is a count (like "a dozen"), so 1 mol of lead and 1 mol of helium contain the same number of atoms — but very different masses, because the atoms differ in weight. Don't confuse the amount (mol) with the mass (g).

Gas volumes — the molar gas volume [Extended]

Gases are awkward to weigh but easy to measure by volume. A remarkable fact (Avogadro's law) rescues us: at the same temperature and pressure, equal volumes of any gas contain equal numbers of molecules. So we don't even need the Mr to count a gas — just its volume.

Molar gas volume — at room temperature and pressure (r.t.p.), one mole of any gas occupies 24 dm³ (= 24 000 cm³). So amount of gas (mol) = volume (dm³) ÷ 24, and volume = mol × 24.
🎈 Why volume can count molecules
Gas is mostly empty space — the molecules are so far apart that their own size barely matters. What sets the volume is just how many molecules are bouncing around (plus temperature and pressure). Fix the temperature and pressure, and volume becomes a direct headcount of molecules. That's why 24 dm³ works for hydrogen, oxygen, CO₂ — anything.
Worked example — what volume does 0.5 mol of CO₂ occupy at r.t.p.? V = 0.5 × 24 = 12 dm³. And how many moles are in 6 dm³ of hydrogen at r.t.p.? n = 6 ÷ 24 = 0.25 mol.
⚠️ Only at r.t.p., only for gases
The 24 dm³ rule applies to gases at r.t.p. only — never to liquids or solids, and not at other temperatures/pressures. And mind your units: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³. If a volume is in cm³, divide by 24 000 instead of 24.

Concentration of solutions

Concentration measures how much solute is packed into a given volume of solution. The 0620 syllabus uses two units — and you must be able to switch between them.

grams per dm³ [Core]
g/dm³ — mass of solute per dm³ of solution. Just mass ÷ volume.
moles per dm³ [Extended]
mol/dm³ — moles of solute per dm³ of solution: c = n / V.
Linking the two units  →  concentration (g/dm³) = concentration (mol/dm³) × molar mass. Multiplying mol/dm³ by g/mol cancels the moles and leaves g/dm³.
⚠️ Volumes are almost always in cm³
Lab volumes come in cm³, but concentration is per dm³. Convert first: V(dm³) = V(cm³) ÷ 1000. Forgetting this gives an answer 1000× too big or small — the single biggest source of lost marks in titration questions.
Worked example — concentration
Dissolve 0.5 mol of NaOH to make 250 cm³ of solution. V = 250 ÷ 1000 = 0.25 dm³, so c = 0.5 ÷ 0.25 = 2 mol/dm³. In g/dm³: 2 × 40 = 80 g/dm³ (Mr of NaOH = 40).

Titrations [Extended]

A titration finds an unknown concentration by reacting it exactly with a solution you do know. The method is always the same chain: volume + known concentration → moles → (use the equation's ratio) → moles of the other → unknown concentration.

Worked example — finding a base's concentration
25.0 cm³ of NaOH is neutralised by 20.0 cm³ of 0.10 mol/dm³ HCl. Reaction NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O (1 : 1).
Moles HCl = 0.10 × (20.0 ÷ 1000) = 0.0020 mol → moles NaOH = 0.0020 (1:1).
Concentration NaOH = 0.0020 ÷ (25.0 ÷ 1000) = 0.080 mol/dm³.

Reacting moles & the limiting reactant [Extended]

With the mole, full reacting-mass problems become a clean three-step chain: mass → moles → (equation ratio) → moles → mass. The equation's coefficients are the mole ratio.

🚲 Building bicycles — the limiting part
One bicycle needs 1 frame + 2 wheels. If a workshop in Pune has 10 frames but only 6 wheels, it can build just 3 bicycles (6 wheels ÷ 2) — then stops, with 7 frames left over. The wheels ran out first: they are the limiting reactant. The frames are in excess. A reaction stops the instant one reactant is used up, no matter how much of the other remains.

Reaction: Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂. The acid is needed in a 2 : 1 ratio to the magnesium. Slide the amounts and see which runs out first — the limiting one is tagged, and it alone decides how much H₂ forms:

✅ How to spot the limiting reactant
Convert both reactants to moles, then divide each by its coefficient in the balanced equation. The smallest answer is the limiting reactant — it's the one that runs out first, and all product amounts are calculated from it. Never assume the one with fewer grams is limiting; check the moles ÷ ratio.

Empirical formulae, yield & purity [Extended]

Empirical & molecular formula from data

Given the mass (or %) of each element, you can recover the formula. Method: convert each mass to moles (÷Ar), then find the simplest whole-number ratio (÷ by the smallest).

Worked example — empirical formula
4.8 g of magnesium combines with 3.2 g of oxygen. Mg: 4.8 ÷ 24 = 0.20 mol; O: 3.2 ÷ 16 = 0.20 mol. Ratio 0.20 : 0.20 = 1 : 1 → empirical formula MgO.
Empirical → molecular
A compound has empirical formula CH₂ and Mr = 42. The empirical unit CH₂ has mass 14, so 42 ÷ 14 = 3 units → molecular formula C₃H₆.

Percentage calculations

Percentage composition by mass  →  % of element = (total Ar of that element ÷ Mr) × 100. E.g. % oxygen in water = (16 ÷ 18) × 100 = 88.9%. % calcium in CaCO₃ = (40 ÷ 100) × 100 = 40%.
Percentage yield  →  (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100. Real reactions lose product (side reactions, spills, reversible steps), so yield is below 100%. If theory predicts 8.0 g but you collect 6.0 g: (6.0 ÷ 8.0) × 100 = 75%.
Percentage purity  →  (mass of pure substance ÷ mass of impure sample) × 100. A 5.0 g sample of limestone containing 4.5 g of pure CaCO₃ is (4.5 ÷ 5.0) × 100 = 90% pure.
⚠️ Yield vs purity — don't mix them up
Yield compares how much product you got to how much the equation predicts. Purity compares how much of a sample is the real substance versus impurities. Different ideas, both capped at 100%. Read the question for which one it's asking.
