Metals & the reactivity ladder

One idea runs through this whole topic: a metal reacts by giving away electrons to form positive ions — and how eagerly it does that is its "reactivity". Line the metals up from most-eager to least-eager and you get the reactivity series — a single ladder that predicts their reactions, their displacement fights, how they corrode, and how we tear them out of their ores. Click your way up and down it below.

01 / 10 · The organising idea

Everything hangs off one ladder: the reactivity series

🪜 The one mental model
A metal atom is happiest when it has handed over its outer electrons and become a positive ion. Some metals are desperate to do this (potassium almost throws its electrons away); some are reluctant (gold clings to its). Rank every metal by how eagerly it forms a positive ion and you have built the reactivity series. Once you have that ranking, nearly every fact in this topic — which metals fizz in water, who displaces whom, what rusts, how each is extracted — is just "read off the ladder".
Reactivity series — a list of metals arranged in order of their reactivity, most reactive at the top. The Cambridge order (with the non-metals carbon and hydrogen slotted in as reference points) is: K Na Ca Mg Al (C) Zn Fe (H) Cu Ag Au.
🛗 The "keenness to let go" ladder
Picture the metals queued on a staircase by how keen they are to let go of electrons. Potassium at the top is practically shoving its electrons at anything nearby — violent. Gold at the bottom refuses to let go at all, which is exactly why gold jewellery dug up after 2000 years is still shiny. Every metal's behaviour is just its height on this staircase.
⚠️ Learn the order cold — a mnemonic helps
Potassium Sodium Calcium Magnesium Aluminium — Carbon Zinc Iron — Hydrogen — Copper Silver Gold "Please Stop Calling Me A Careless Zebra In Hot Class, Sir Guy"
✅ Why carbon and hydrogen are on a "metals" list
They aren't metals — they are reference markers. A metal below carbon can be extracted by heating its ore with carbon; a metal below hydrogen won't react with dilute acids. Remembering exactly where C and H sit is worth easy marks.
02 / 10 · 9.1 Physical properties

What makes a metal a metal — the physical properties

🌊 One structure explains all of it
A metal is a lattice of positive ions sitting in a "sea" of delocalised electrons that can drift freely (this is metallic bonding from Topic 2). That free-electron sea is the secret behind almost every physical property: the electrons carry charge (electrical conductor), they carry energy (thermal conductor), and the layers of ions can slide without the bonding breaking (malleable and ductile).

Compare metals with non-metals side by side — this contrast is a favourite exam table:

PropertyMetalsNon-metals
Thermal conductivityGood conductorsPoor (insulators)
Electrical conductivityGood conductorsPoor (except graphite)
Malleable / ductileYes — bend, hammer, draw into wireBrittle — shatter when solid
Melting & boiling pointsGenerally highGenerally low (many are gases)
AppearanceShiny (lustrous), often denseDull, low density
Malleable — can be hammered or bent into shape without breaking. Ductile — can be drawn out into wires. Both work because the layers of metal ions slide over one another while the electron sea holds them together.
🔨 Ductile in your hand
A copper wire, a steel spoon, a gold chain, aluminium foil pressed round leftovers — each one has been drawn, bent, hammered or rolled into shape. Try that with a lump of sulfur or a graphite pencil "lead" and it just snaps: non-metals are brittle because they have no sliding electron sea to hold shifted atoms together.
⚠️ "High melting point" isn't universal
"Generally high" — but there are famous exceptions. Mercury is a liquid at room temperature, and the Group I metals (sodium, potassium) are soft and melt at low temperatures. Say "generally" or "most" in your answer, not "all".
03 / 10 · 9.1 Chemical reactions

The three reactions of metals — acid, water, oxygen

Metals attack three everyday things, and how vigorously depends entirely on their place on the ladder. Learn the pattern, not a list of isolated facts:

Metal + dilute acid
→ salt + hydrogen (squeaky-pop gas). Only metals above hydrogen react.
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
Metal + water / steam
→ hydroxide/oxide + hydrogen. Reactive metals in cold water; middling metals only in steam.
2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂
Metal + oxygen
→ metal oxide. Reactive metals burn brightly; gold won't react at all.
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
🧠 Why the vigour tracks the ladder
In every one of these reactions the metal has to give up electrons. So a metal high on the ladder (keen to lose electrons) reacts fast and fiercely; one low down reacts slowly, or not at all. That is why the same ranking predicts acid fizz, water fizz and how brightly it burns — it's all the same tendency.

Reaction with water vs steam

Cold water (only K, Na, Ca)
metal + water → metal hydroxide + hydrogen.
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂
Potassium is violent (lilac flame), sodium vigorous (whizzing ball), calcium steady.
Steam (e.g. Mg, Zn, Fe)
metal + steam → metal oxide + hydrogen.
Mg + H₂O → MgO + H₂
These are too unreactive for cold water but react when water is hot vapour.
✅ Watch the product: hydroxide vs oxide
With cold water you get a metal hydroxide (e.g. NaOH, Ca(OH)₂). With steam you get a metal oxide (e.g. MgO, Fe₃O₄). Both release hydrogen. Naming the correct product — and hydrogen — is where the marks are.
⚠️ Below hydrogen = no acid reaction
Copper, silver and gold sit below hydrogen, so they do not react with dilute acids — no fizz, no hydrogen. Writing "Cu + HCl → CuCl₂ + H₂" is a classic error; that reaction simply doesn't happen.

05 / 10 · 9.4 Displacement & ions (Supplement)

Displacement — a reactivity fight in a beaker

🥊 The rule of the fight
Put a metal into a solution containing the ions of another metal and they fight over the electrons. A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive one from its solution — it forces the less-keen metal to take its electrons back and drop out as solid metal, while the winner dissolves as ions. Reactivity here means one thing: tendency to form positive ions.
Displacement reaction — a reaction in which a more reactive metal takes the place of a less reactive metal in a compound (usually a solution of its ions). The more reactive metal loses electrons and becomes ions; the less reactive metal ions gain electrons and become atoms.

🔵 The classic blue-fades experiment
Stand an iron nail in blue copper(II) sulfate solution. Iron is above copper, so it wins: the blue slowly fades to pale green (Fe²⁺) and the nail gets coated in pink-brown copper. You've watched reactivity decide the outcome in real time.

Aluminium's disappearing act

Why aluminium seems unreactive — aluminium is high on the ladder, yet it doesn't fizz in water and resists corrosion. That's because a thin, tough, unreactive layer of aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) forms instantly on its surface and seals the metal underneath, so it appears far less reactive than it truly is.
✅ Answering "explain reactivity in terms of ions"
Full-mark phrasing: "The more reactive metal has a greater tendency to form positive ions (to lose electrons), so it displaces the less reactive metal from solution." For aluminium: "its oxide layer prevents reaction, making it appear unreactive."
06 / 10 · 9.2 Uses of metals

Property → use — why each metal earns its job

🎯 Every use is a property in disguise
Exam questions here are always "explain why metal X is used for Y". The answer is never "because it's a metal" — it's the specific property that suits the job. Match the property to the need and the mark is yours.
Aluminium — aircraft
Used because of its low density (it's light), so the aircraft is lighter and uses less fuel.
Aluminium — overhead power cables
Low density (light enough to string between pylons) and good electrical conductivity.
Aluminium — food containers & foil
Its resistance to corrosion (that protective oxide layer) keeps food safe and untainted.
Copper — electrical wiring
Good electrical conductivity and ductility — it carries current well and is easily drawn into thin wires.
✅ The four uses Cambridge asks for by name
Aluminium → aircraft (low density) · overhead cables (low density + conductivity) · food containers (corrosion resistance). Copper → wiring (conductivity + ductility). Quote the property AND the use together — one without the other usually drops the mark.
🍱 The foil around your roti
That aluminium foil keeping tiffin-box rotis warm works precisely because aluminium resists corrosion — its oxide layer means it never rusts into the food. Overhead near a highway, the same metal hangs as long grey power cables: light enough not to sag, conductive enough to carry the current.
07 / 10 · 9.3 Alloys

Alloys — why a mixture beats a pure metal

Alloy — a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (usually other metals or carbon). Examples: brass = copper + zinc; stainless steel = iron + chromium, nickel and carbon.
🧱 Pure metals are almost too neat
In a pure metal the atoms are all the same size, stacked in tidy layers — and tidy layers slide over each other easily, which is why pure metals are relatively soft and bend readily. Mix in atoms of a different size and they jam the layers: the rows can no longer slip past one another, so the alloy is harder and stronger.

Press "Push the top layers →" and watch what happens to a pure metal versus an alloy under the same force:

Pure metal — same-size atoms
Neat, equal layers — they slide freely, so a pure metal is softer.
Alloy — different-size atoms
Different-sized atoms wedge in — layers jam, so the alloy is harder.
Brass
A mixture of copper and zinc. Harder than pure copper — used for taps, screws, instruments and decorative fittings.
Stainless steel
A mixture of iron with chromium, nickel and carbon. Hard and rust-resistant — used for cutlery because it stays sharp and doesn't rust.
✅ Full-mark reason alloys are harder (Supplement)
"The different-sized atoms in an alloy mean the layers can no longer slide over each other easily, so the alloy is harder and stronger than the pure metal." You may also be asked to identify an alloy from a diagram — look for a lattice containing atoms of two different sizes (a pure metal shows one size only).
08 / 10 · 9.5 Corrosion of metals

Rusting — and how to stop it

🌧️ Rust needs two things, not one
Iron rusts only when both oxygen and water can reach it. Take either one away — keep it bone-dry, or seal out the air — and it can't rust. That single fact is the key to every prevention method: they all work by removing oxygen or water from the metal's surface.
Rusting — the corrosion of iron and steel. It needs oxygen (from air) AND water, and the product is hydrated iron(III) oxide, Fe₂O₃·xH₂O — the flaky orange-brown solid we call rust.

Barrier methods — seal the surface

Painting
A coat of paint keeps out air and water — used on cars, gates, bridges.
Greasing / oiling
Oil or grease seals moving parts (bike chains, tools) from oxygen and water.
Coating with plastic
A plastic layer (e.g. on a dish rack or fridge shelf) forms a waterproof barrier.
✅ How barrier methods work — say the mechanism
All three "work by forming a barrier that excludes oxygen and/or water from the iron." Naming the barrier isn't enough — you must say it keeps out air/water to score the explanation mark.

Galvanising & sacrificial protection (Supplement)

Galvanising — coating iron or steel with a layer of zinc. It protects in two ways at once: as a barrier (the zinc keeps out air and water) and by sacrificial protection — even if the coating is scratched, the iron is still protected.
🛡️ Why a scratch doesn't matter — sacrifice
Zinc is above iron in the reactivity series, so it is more eager to lose electrons. Where the coating is scratched and both metals are exposed, the zinc corrodes instead of the iron — it "gives up its electrons" first, feeding them to the iron so the iron cannot be oxidised. The zinc is sacrificed to save the iron; blocks of zinc or magnesium bolted to ships' hulls and underground pipelines do the same job.
Sacrificial protection — attaching a more reactive metal (zinc or magnesium) to iron. Being more reactive, it loses electrons in preference to the iron and corrodes away, protecting the iron. Explained by the reactivity series (it sits above iron) and by electron loss (it is oxidised instead of the iron).
⚠️ Galvanising is BOTH — don't give half the answer
Zinc galvanising is a barrier and sacrificial protection. If the question is worth two marks, give both. And note: the sacrificial metal must be more reactive than iron — a less reactive coating (like tin) protects only as a barrier, and a scratch then makes the iron rust faster.
09 / 10 · 9.6 Extraction of metals

Extraction — the ladder decides the method

⛏️ Reactive metals are the hardest to win
A metal is found in the ground combined with other elements (an ore) precisely because it's reactive enough to have bonded. So the more reactive the metal, the harder it clings to its ore, and the more energy its extraction needs. Gold at the bottom is found lying around as pure metal; aluminium near the top needs electricity to prise it out.
Position on ladderExtraction methodExamples
Very unreactive (bottom)Found native (uncombined)Gold, silver
Below carbonReduction by heating with carbonZinc, iron, copper
Above carbonElectrolysis of molten compoundAluminium, and K, Na, Ca, Mg
✅ The carbon cut-off is the whole idea
Carbon can only pull the oxygen off a metal less reactive than itself. So metals below carbon (zinc, iron, copper) are extracted by carbon reduction; metals above carbon (aluminium upward) are too reactive and must be extracted by electrolysis.

Aluminium from bauxite — electrolysis

Aluminium's main ore is bauxite (aluminium oxide, Al₂O₃). Because aluminium is above carbon, it's extracted by electrolysis of the purified, molten oxide:

Al³⁺ ions move to the cathode (−); O²⁻ ions move to the anodes (+).
At the cathode (−)
Aluminium ions gain electrons — aluminium metal forms and sinks.
Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
At the anode (+)
Oxide ions lose electrons — oxygen gas forms.
2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
✅ Cryolite & the burning anodes (Supplement)
Cryolite: aluminium oxide melts at ~2000 °C, so it's dissolved in molten cryolite to lower the melting point — saving huge amounts of energy. Anodes: at that heat the oxygen made at the positive carbon anodes reacts with the carbon (C + O₂ → CO₂), burning them away, so they must be replaced regularly.
⚠️ Don't mix up the electrodes
The metal always forms at the cathode (−) — positive metal ions are attracted to the negative electrode. Oxygen forms at the anode (+). Getting these the wrong way round is the commonest electrolysis slip.
