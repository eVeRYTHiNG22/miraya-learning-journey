Acids, Bases & Salts

One idea threads the whole topic: an acid hands out H⁺ ions, an alkali hands out OH⁻ ions, and when they meet they cancel to water. Everything else — the tests, the reactions, the salts you can make — flows from that single trade. Drag the pH slider, then let the salt-route selector pick the right preparation method.

01 / 10 · 7.1 The H⁺ / OH⁻ idea

It all comes down to two ions: H⁺ and OH⁻

💧 The one mental model
Dissolve an acid in water and it releases hydrogen ions, H⁺. Dissolve an alkali in water and it releases hydroxide ions, OH⁻. Every single reaction, colour change and test in this whole topic is just those two ions doing their thing. Get comfortable with "acid = H⁺ factory, alkali = OH⁻ factory" and the rest clicks into place.
Acid — a substance that, when dissolved in water, produces hydrogen ions, H⁺(aq). It is these H⁺ ions that make a solution acidic.
Alkali — a soluble base that, when dissolved in water, produces hydroxide ions, OH⁻(aq). It is these OH⁻ ions that make a solution alkaline.
🍋 From the Indian kitchen
Squeeze a nimbu (lemon) and the sourness is citric acid pumping H⁺ into the juice. Stir a spoon of baking soda / washing soda into water and it turns slippery-alkaline as OH⁻ ions appear. A pinch of chuna (slaked lime) on paan is strongly alkaline for the same reason. Sour ≈ H⁺; soapy/bitter ≈ OH⁻.
✅ Mark-scoring phrasing
"Aqueous solutions of acids contain H⁺ ions; aqueous solutions of alkalis contain OH⁻ ions." Naming the ion — not just "it's acidic" — is what scores the mark.
⚠️ The three words that mean three different things
BASE = metal oxide or hydroxide (neutralises acid) ALKALI = a base that DISSOLVES in water Every alkali is a base; not every base is an alkali
02 / 10 · 7.1 Reactions of acids

What acids do — three reactions to know cold

An acid attacks three kinds of substance, and each gives a salt plus something predictable. Learn the three "recipes" and their gas tests:

Acid + metal
→ salt + hydrogen
Fizzing; the gas gives a squeaky pop with a lit splint.
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂
Acid + base
→ salt + water
Neutralisation — no gas. Often the solid base just dissolves.
CuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂O
Acid + carbonate
→ salt + water + CO₂
Fizzing; the gas turns limewater milky.
CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
🧠 Why these three, and why a salt every time
In each reaction the acid's H⁺ is the aggressor and its negative part (Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻…) is a spectator waiting to pair up. Whatever metal comes out of the other reactant takes the acid's place — that new "metal + acid-leftover" compound is the salt. So a salt is simply what you get when the H⁺ of an acid is replaced by a metal (or ammonium) ion.
🥤 The imli & the marble floor
Drop tangy tamarind (imli) or nimbu on a marble (CaCO₃) shelf and it slowly fizzes and dulls — acid on carbonate giving CO₂. It's the same reaction that eats away marble monuments in polluted, acidic city air.
✅ The three gas tests (worth easy marks)
Hydrogen → lit splint gives a "squeaky pop".  ·  Carbon dioxide → bubbles through limewater turn it milky/cloudy.  ·  (Ammonia, next section) → turns damp red litmus blue.
⚠️ "Acid + metal always works" — not quite
Only metals above hydrogen in the reactivity series (Mg, Zn, Fe…) give the salt + H₂ reaction. Very unreactive metals like copper don't react with dilute acid at all, and very reactive metals (Na, K) are far too violent to do this safely.
03 / 10 · 7.1 Bases & alkalis

Bases and alkalis — the H⁺ mop-up crew

Base — an oxide or hydroxide of a metal (e.g. CuO, CaO, NaOH). A base neutralises an acid to give a salt and water.
Alkali — a soluble base. Because it dissolves, it releases OH⁻ ions into solution (e.g. NaOH, KOH, aqueous ammonia).
🧽 What a base is "for"
A base is anything that soaks up H⁺. Its oxide (O²⁻) or hydroxide (OH⁻) grabs the acid's hydrogen ions and locks them away as water — that's neutralisation. If the base happens to dissolve in water, we upgrade its name to alkali; that's the only difference.

Two reactions of bases to know

Base + acid
→ salt + water (neutralisation — the reverse view of "acid + base").
2NaOH + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
Alkali + ammonium salt
→ salt + water + ammonia. Warm it and pungent NH₃ gas comes off.
NH₄Cl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O + NH₃
✅ The test for an ammonium salt (NH₄⁺)
Add an alkali (e.g. NaOH) and warm. Ammonia gas is released, which turns damp red litmus paper blue — the standard confirmation that a compound contains the ammonium ion. This is why alkalis react with ammonium salts at all: they knock the H⁺ off NH₄⁺ to free NH₃.
💊 The antacid in your medicine box
Feeling acidity after a heavy meal? An antacid tablet (Digene, Eno) is a mild base — magnesium hydroxide or a carbonate — that neutralises the excess HCl in your stomach, turning it into a harmless salt and water. Same chemistry as the lab, done in your gut.
04 / 10 · 7.1 Indicators & the pH scale

Indicators & pH — putting a number on acidity

An indicator is a dye that changes colour depending on whether it sits in H⁺ or OH⁻. Universal indicator is a mixture of dyes that runs through a whole rainbow, matched to the pH scale (0–14) — a measure of hydrogen-ion concentration.

🌈 What pH really tracks
pH is just a ruler for how much H⁺ is around. Lots of H⁺ → low pH → strongly acidic (red on universal indicator). Almost no H⁺ (lots of OH⁻ instead) → high pH → strongly alkaline (purple). Right in the middle, pH 7, H⁺ and OH⁻ balance out — neutral (green).

⚠️ "Low pH = a little acid" — backwards
The lower the pH, the more acidic (more H⁺). pH 1 is far more acidic than pH 3. And universal indicator only tells you relative acidity — it can't by itself prove whether an acid is "strong" or "weak" (that's the next idea, about dissociation).
05 / 10 · 7.1 Neutralisation

Neutralisation — where H⁺ meets OH⁻

🤝 The reaction under every reaction
Strip an acid–alkali reaction down to the ions that actually matter and only one thing happens: a hydrogen ion and a hydroxide ion snap together to make water. The metal ion and the acid's negative ion are just spectators floating in solution — they become the salt when the water evaporates.
Neutralisation (ionic equation) — H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l). An acid and an alkali react to produce a salt and water; this ionic equation is the heart of it.

Zoom out and the full "molecular" equation just adds the spectators back in — for example:

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
2HNO₃ + Ca(OH)₂ → Ca(NO₃)₂ + 2H₂O
H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
🌾 Neutralisation you can eat and grow
Indian farmers spread lime (chuna / calcium hydroxide) on soil that has gone too acidic, raising its pH so crops grow. Bee-sting acidic? Dab on a mild alkali. Too-alkaline soil? Add an acidic treatment. Controlling pH by neutralisation is everyday agriculture and first aid.
✅ Know the ionic equation by heart
Whatever the acid or alkali, examiners want H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l) — with state symbols. It's a guaranteed mark that never changes.
06 / 10 · 7.1 Strong & weak acids (Supplement)

Strong vs weak — it's about how much dissociates

💥 Not "how much acid" — "how fully it splits"
"Strong" and "weak" have nothing to do with concentration. They describe what fraction of the acid molecules actually break apart to release H⁺ in water. A strong acid splits completely; a weak acid only partly splits, leaving most molecules whole. Same concentration, very different H⁺ count.
Acid = proton donor — an acid is a substance that donates H⁺ ions (protons). A base = proton acceptor — it accepts those H⁺ ions.
Strong acid — fully dissociated
Every molecule splits into ions in water.
HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
Single arrow — the reaction goes to completion. e.g. hydrochloric, sulfuric, nitric acids.
Weak acid — partially dissociated
Only a fraction splits; the rest stay as molecules.
CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + CH₃COO⁻(aq)
Reversible arrow ⇌ — an equilibrium. e.g. ethanoic (vinegar), citric, carbonic acids.
⚠️ The arrow tells the whole story
Strong acid → complete split → single arrow → Weak acid → partial split → equilibrium arrow ⇌ HCl = strong · CH₃COOH (ethanoic) = weak
🍶 Vinegar vs the lab bottle
Sirka (vinegar) is dilute ethanoic acid — you can safely sprinkle it on food because, even undiluted, only a few percent of its molecules release H⁺ (weak). The same-strength bottle of hydrochloric acid would be dangerous, because every molecule releases its H⁺ (strong). Weak ≠ harmless-because-dilute; it means "reluctant to split".
⚠️ Concentrated ≠ strong
You can have concentrated weak acid and dilute strong acid. Concentration = how much acid per litre; strength = how completely it ionises. Mixing these two up is the classic Supplement trap.
07 / 10 · 7.2 Oxides

Oxides — acidic, basic, or both

🧭 Let the Periodic Table sort them
An oxide's behaviour follows the character of the element burned with oxygen. Metals → basic oxides (they neutralise acids). Non-metals → acidic oxides (they neutralise bases). A few in-between "borderline" metals give oxides that do both — amphoteric.
Basic oxides
Metal oxides. React with acids → salt + water.
e.g. CuO, CaO, Na₂O, MgO.
Acidic oxides
Non-metal oxides. React with bases/alkalis → salt + water.
e.g. SO₂, CO₂, NO₂.
Amphoteric oxides
React with both acids and bases → salt + water.
e.g. Al₂O₃, ZnO. (Supplement)
Amphoteric oxide — an oxide that reacts with both acids and bases to produce a salt and water. The two classic examples to quote are aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) and zinc oxide (ZnO).
🌫️ Acidic oxides you breathe
SO₂ from burning sulfur-rich fuels (with oxides of nitrogen) dissolves in rain to make acid rain — the chemistry that eats the marble of monuments like the Taj Mahal. CO₂ from every fire is an acidic oxide too, but it only makes rain weakly acidic (carbonic acid). Acidic oxides are literally "acids waiting for water".
✅ Quick classification rule
Metal oxide → basic (reacts with acid). Non-metal oxide → acidic (reacts with alkali). If it reacts with both → amphoteric (only Al₂O₃ and ZnO are asked for). Match the reactant to the class and you've placed the oxide.

Which salts dissolve — the rules that decide your method

Before you can make a salt you must know whether it's soluble (make it in solution, then crystallise) or insoluble (make it by precipitation). These rules are worth memorising — they drive every salt-preparation choice:

⚠️ Learn the exceptions, not just the trend
The marks live in the exceptions: silver & lead chloride, barium/calcium/lead sulfate, and the Na/K/ammonium escape clause for carbonates and hydroxides. "Calcium hydroxide is partially soluble" (that's limewater) is a favourite detail.
09 / 10 · 7.3 Preparation of salts

Preparing salts — pick the method from the solubility

🧪 One decision decides everything
Every salt prep starts with one question: is the salt I want soluble or insoluble? If it's soluble, react an acid with the right partner and crystallise. If it's insoluble, just mix two solutions and let it precipitate out. Within "soluble", how you react the acid depends on whether the other reactant dissolves too.

Pick a target salt and the selector shows which of the methods fits, why, the balanced equation, and the practical steps:

    The four routes to a soluble salt

    Acid + alkali (titration)
    Both reactants dissolve, so you can't filter off an excess. Titrate to find the exact volumes (indicator), then repeat without indicator and crystallise.
    Acid + excess metal
    Add excess metal to the acid; it fizzes until the acid is used up. Filter off the leftover solid metal, then crystallise the salt solution.
    Acid + excess insoluble base
    Warm the acid and stir in excess insoluble base (metal oxide/hydroxide) until no more dissolves. Filter off the excess, then crystallise.
    Acid + excess insoluble carbonate
    Add excess carbonate to the acid; it fizzes off CO₂ until spent. Filter off the excess solid, then crystallise. (Same "add excess, filter, crystallise" pattern as a base.)
    ✅ Why titration is the odd one out
    For an insoluble metal/base/carbonate you can add an excess and simply filter the leftover out — the endpoint looks after itself. With a soluble base (an alkali), nothing is left to filter, so you must use titration to measure the exact neutralising volume, then remake the mixture without indicator so the salt isn't contaminated.

    Making an insoluble salt — precipitation (Supplement)

    Precipitation — mix two soluble salt solutions so that the ions of the wanted insoluble salt meet and drop out as a solid. Filter, wash with distilled water, and dry.

    For example, to make insoluble silver chloride: AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq). The AgCl precipitates; the NaNO₃ stays dissolved and washes away.

    Getting dry crystals — hydrated vs anhydrous

    Hydrated
    Chemically combined with water (water of crystallisation). e.g. CuSO₄·5H₂O — blue crystals; CoCl₂·6H₂O — pink.
    Anhydrous
    Contains no water. e.g. anhydrous CuSO₄ — white powder; anhydrous CoCl₂ — blue. Heating drives the water off.
    Water of crystallisation — the water molecules chemically present in hydrated crystals, shown by the "·nH₂O" in a formula such as CuSO₄·5H₂O and CoCl₂·6H₂O.
    ⚠️ Don't boil your crystals dry
    To crystallise a soluble salt you heat to concentrate the solution, then let it cool slowly so crystals grow, and finally pat them dry. Evaporating all the water by strong heating gives a powder — and can drive off the water of crystallisation, so a hydrated salt loses its shape and colour.
