Chemical Energetics

One idea runs through the whole topic: every reaction is a trade in bond energy. When a reaction gives heat out it is exothermic; when it soaks heat in it is endothermic. Tap the panels — redraw the energy profile, then let the bond-energy calculator predict ΔH.

01 / 09 · 5.1 Energy & reactions

Energy is stored in bonds — reactions just move it

🔋 The one mental model
Think of every chemical bond as a tiny stretched spring holding energy. A reaction breaks some springs and builds new ones. Breaking always costs energy; building always pays back energy. Whether the reaction warms its surroundings or cools them comes down to one question: did building the new bonds pay back more than breaking the old ones cost?
💸 The energy bank account
It's like spending and earning money. Breaking bonds is spending (energy goes out of the chemicals); making bonds is earning (energy comes back). If you earn more than you spend, you finish with spare cash to give away — that "spare" is heat released to the room (exothermic). If you spend more than you earn, you must pull money in from outside — heat is taken from the room (endothermic).

System vs. surroundings — say which one you mean

The system is the reacting chemicals. The surroundings are everything else — the solution they sit in, the test tube, the air, the thermometer. Energy that leaves the system goes into the surroundings (and vice-versa), so the temperature you read on a thermometer is really telling you about the surroundings.

Exothermic — gives out
Thermal energy transfers from the system to the surroundings. Surroundings warm up → thermometer reading rises.
Endothermic — takes in
Thermal energy transfers from the surroundings into the system. Surroundings cool down → thermometer reading falls.
✅ The temperature test — how you tell them apart in the lab
Put a thermometer in the mixture. Temperature up = exothermic. Temperature down = endothermic. Always answer in terms of the surroundings' temperature — that is exactly what the Core objectives ask you to state.
02 / 09 · 5.1 Exothermic reactions

Exothermic — heat flows out, surroundings warm up

Exothermic reaction — a reaction that transfers thermal energy to the surroundings, leading to an increase in the temperature of the surroundings.
🔥 Why it feels hot
The products are at a lower energy than the reactants. That "lost" chemical energy doesn't vanish — it leaves the chemicals as heat, so the test tube, the solution and the air around it get warmer.

Everyday exothermic reactions

Combustion (burning fuels)
Neutralisation (acid + alkali)
Respiration in your cells
Most reactive-metal + acid reactions
🍳 From the Indian kitchen
The LPG cylinder on a home stove burns methane/butane in air — combustion, strongly exothermic — which is the whole point: the heat released cooks the food. A chemical hand-warmer a trekker carries to Shimla works the same way: a slow exothermic reaction (iron rusting) releases heat into cold fingers.
✅ Mark-scoring phrasing
"Energy is transferred to the surroundings, so the temperature of the surroundings increases." Naming the direction (to the surroundings) and the temperature effect together is what scores both marks.
03 / 09 · 5.1 Endothermic reactions

Endothermic — heat flows in, surroundings cool down

Endothermic reaction — a reaction that takes in thermal energy from the surroundings, leading to a decrease in the temperature of the surroundings.
🧊 Why it feels cold
The products end up at a higher energy than the reactants. That extra energy has to come from somewhere — it is pulled out of the surroundings as heat, so the mixture and everything touching it gets colder.

Everyday endothermic changes

Thermal decomposition (e.g. heating CaCO₃)
Photosynthesis (energy taken from sunlight)
Dissolving some salts in water
The reaction in an instant cold pack
🏏 The cricketer's cold pack
When a batsman is hit and the physio cracks an instant cold pack, a salt dissolves inside and that endothermic process draws heat out of the swelling, so it feels icy. A spoon of fruit-salt like Eno (or sherbet) on a warm tongue does the chemical version: the fizzing acid–bicarbonate reaction is endothermic and gives that cool tingle.
⚠️ "Endothermic = the reaction gets hotter" — NO
An endothermic reaction makes the surroundings colder, not hotter. The chemicals are absorbing energy, so the thermometer (reading the surroundings) goes down. Don't confuse "needs heating to start" with "endothermic" either — many exothermic reactions (like combustion) still need a spark to begin.
⚠️ Lock these directions in
EXO = EXit → energy out → surroundings WARMER ENDO = INTO → energy in → surroundings COOLER
04 / 09 · 5.1 Reaction pathway diagrams

Reaction pathway diagrams — energy as a journey

A reaction pathway diagram (energy level diagram) plots energy up the side against the progress of the reaction along the bottom. It tells the whole energy story at a glance: where the reactants and products sit, the size of the energy barrier, and whether energy is released or absorbed.

⛰️ Reading the hill
Picture pushing a boulder over a hill. You must shove it up to the top first (that climb is the activation energy) before it can roll down the far side. Where it finally settles — lower than the start (exothermic) or higher (endothermic) — is decided by the products' energy level, and the height difference is the enthalpy change, ΔH.

✅ How to label a pathway diagram (Supplement)
A full-mark diagram has five labels: axes (energy ↑, progress of reaction →), the reactants level, the products level, the activation energy Eₐ (reactants up to the peak), and the ΔH arrow (reactants to products). For exothermic the ΔH arrow points down; for endothermic it points up.
⚠️ Measure Eₐ from the reactants, not the floor
Activation energy is the climb from the reactant level up to the peak — not from the bottom of the graph. And ΔH is measured reactants → products, not products → peak. Mixing these up is the commonest pathway-diagram error.
05 / 09 · 5.1 Enthalpy change, ΔH (Supplement)

Enthalpy change, ΔH — putting a number and a sign on it

Enthalpy change, ΔH — the transfer of thermal energy during a reaction. ΔH is negative for exothermic reactions and ΔH is positive for endothermic reactions. It is usually measured in kJ/mol.
➖ Why exothermic is negative
ΔH is the energy of the products minus the energy of the reactants. In an exothermic reaction the products are lower, so products − reactants is a negative number — the chemicals lost energy (it left as heat). Endothermic: products are higher, so ΔH is positive — the chemicals gained energy.
Reaction typeSurroundingsProducts vs reactantsSign of ΔH
ExothermicWarm upProducts lowerΔH negative (−)
EndothermicCool downProducts higherΔH positive (+)
⚠️ Never drop the sign
A ΔH answer is only complete with its sign and units: e.g. ΔH = −184 kJ/mol, not just "184". The minus sign is the information that the reaction is exothermic.
06 / 09 · 5.1 Activation energy, Eₐ (Supplement)

Activation energy, Eₐ — the barrier every reaction must clear

Activation energy, Eₐ — the minimum energy that colliding particles must have in order to react.
🔥 Why even exothermic reactions need a spark
Before new bonds can form, the old ones must start to break — and breaking bonds needs an input of energy. That up-front cost is the activation energy: the small "hill" particles must get over before the reaction can release its energy. It's why a petrol–air mixture is happy to sit in the tank, yet a single spark sets it off: the spark supplies Eₐ.
🎢 The push before the drop
A roller-coaster car at the station won't move on its own — even though it would love to be at the low point of the track. It must first be hauled up to the crest (activation energy). Once over, it rushes down. Exothermic reactions are downhill overall, but still start with that uphill climb.
✅ Spot it on the diagram
Eₐ is always the gap from the reactant energy level up to the top of the peak. A larger peak means a bigger barrier and (other things equal) a slower reaction — the bridge to Topic 6.2, where catalysts work by lowering Eₐ.
07 / 09 · 5.1 Bonds & energy (Supplement)

Bond breaking vs bond making — where ΔH actually comes from

⚠️ The two facts everything else rests on
Bond BREAKING is ENDOthermic — needs energy in Bond MAKING is EXOthermic — gives energy out
🧲 Why breaking costs and making pays
Atoms in a bond are held together by attraction — pulling them apart is like stretching a magnet off a fridge: you must put energy in. Letting atoms snap together into a new bond is the magnet slamming back — energy is released. So every reaction first spends energy breaking, then earns energy making.
Breaking bonds (in reactants)
Endothermic — energy is absorbed to pull bonded atoms apart. Counts as energy IN (+).
Making bonds (in products)
Exothermic — energy is given out as new bonds form. Counts as energy OUT (−).

The rule that decides exo or endo

ΔH = energy to break bonds − energy released making bonds
ΔH = Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds made)
Making releases MORE than breaking costs
Σ(made) > Σ(broken) → ΔH is negative → exothermic. Spare energy leaves as heat.
Breaking costs MORE than making releases
Σ(broken) > Σ(made) → ΔH is positive → endothermic. Extra energy must be pulled in.
⚠️ Don't flip breaking and making
It is a guaranteed lost mark to write "breaking bonds releases energy". Breaking always needs energy; making always releases it. The overall sign of ΔH comes from which total wins, never from one bond alone.
08 / 09 · 5.1 ΔH from bond energies (Supplement)

Calculating ΔH from bond energies

A bond energy is the energy needed to break one mole of a particular bond (and, equally, the energy released when that bond forms). Add up every bond broken in the reactants (energy in), subtract every bond made in the products (energy out), and you get ΔH:

Method — ΔH = Σ(bond energies broken) − Σ(bond energies made). A negative answer means exothermic; a positive answer means endothermic.

ΔH = Σ broken − Σ made
✅ Worked example — H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl
Broken: 1 (H–H) + 1 (Cl–Cl) = 436 + 242 = 678. Made: 2 (H–Cl) = 2 × 431 = 862. ΔH = 678 − 862 = −184 kJ/mol. Negative → exothermic (more energy released forming the H–Cl bonds than was needed to break the H–H and Cl–Cl bonds).
⚠️ Count every bond, and mind the coefficients
In CH₄ there are four C–H bonds, and "2O₂" means two O=O bonds. Multiply each bond energy by how many of that bond there are, on both sides. Dropping a bond or ignoring a "2" in front is the usual way these calculations go wrong.
