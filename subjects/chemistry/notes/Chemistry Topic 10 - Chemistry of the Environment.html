The Chemistry of Water, Air & Climate

This whole topic is chemistry applied to the two things you can't live without — the water you drink and the air you breathe. What's dissolved or floating in them, how we test and clean them, what we've been adding that shouldn't be there, and how we're trying to fix it. 01 / 10 · The thread

Shared resources, and the chemistry of keeping them clean

🌍 The one mental model
Water and air are shared tanks that everyone draws from and everyone pours into. Nature keeps them fit to use — rivers carry dissolved oxygen, plants pull CO₂ back out. But when we add things faster than nature removes them (sewage, nitrates, soot, extra CO₂), the tank goes off. Every fact in this topic is either "what's in the tank", "how do we test/clean it", or "what did we add and how do we stop".
🚰 The apartment water tank
Think of the overhead tank on an Indian apartment building. Rain and the municipal supply fill it (inputs); taps drain it (outputs). Let leaves, dust and algae build up faster than the annual cleaning removes them, and the water turns undrinkable — not because anyone did one big bad thing, but because small additions outran the clean-up. The atmosphere and rivers behave exactly the same way, just far bigger.
✅ How Topic 10 splits for the exam
Three sub-topics: 10.1 Water (tests, purity, what's in natural water, treatment), 10.2 Fertilisers (ammonium salts, nitrates, NPK), and 10.3 Air quality & climate (clean air, five pollutants, the greenhouse effect, acid rain, photosynthesis). Learn the three tests, the five pollutants and their fixes cold — they are near-guaranteed marks.
02 / 10 · 10.1 Testing for water & its purity

Two different questions: "is it water?" and "is it pure?"

🔬 Don't mix up the two tests
"Is water present?" is answered by a chemical colour change — a dry salt that turns colour the instant water touches it. "Is this water pure?" is a completely different question about impurities, answered by checking its boiling and melting points. A colour test can shout "water!" at a sample of muddy river water — that doesn't make it pure.

Chemical tests — is water present?

Anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride
Dry paper is blue. Add water and it turns pink.
blue → pink
Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate
Dry powder is white. Add water and it turns blue.
white → blue
⚠️ Lock in the two colour changes
Cobalt chloride: BLUE → PINK with water Copper sulfate: WHITE → BLUE with water
⚠️ These tests only prove "water is present" — not "pure water"
Cobalt chloride turning pink tells you a sample contains water. It says nothing about salts, microbes or metals dissolved in it. To prove a sample is pure water you need its physical constants, next.

Testing purity — boiling and melting points

Pure water has fixed constants: it melts at exactly 0 °C and boils at exactly 100 °C (at normal atmospheric pressure). Dissolved impurities shift both:

The purity check — a sample is pure water only if it melts at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C. Impurities raise the boiling point (above 100 °C) and lower the melting point (below 0 °C).
✅ Why chemists use distilled, not tap, water
Distilled water is used in practical chemistry rather than tap water because it contains fewer chemical impurities. Tap water carries dissolved salts (and chlorine) that could take part in or interfere with a reaction — distilling removes them, so your results are down to the chemicals you added, not the water.

Water from natural sources is never just H₂O

Rain, river and ground water pick up substances on the way. Some are helpful, some are harmful — and a few, like metal compounds, can be either depending on which one and how much:

Dissolved oxygen
Metal compounds
Plastics
Sewage
Harmful microbes
Nitrates (from fertilisers)
Phosphates (fertilisers & detergents)
Beneficial substances
Dissolved oxygen — keeps fish and other aquatic life alive.
Some metal compounds — provide essential minerals for life (e.g. calcium, iron in trace amounts).
Potentially harmful substances
Some metal compounds are toxic. Some plastics harm aquatic life. Sewage carries harmful microbes that cause disease. Nitrates & phosphates lead to deoxygenation of the water and damage aquatic life.
🐟 Why "extra fertiliser in the river" backfires
A farmer's nitrate and phosphate fertiliser is brilliant for crops — but when rain washes it into a pond, it over-feeds algae and water plants. They bloom, then die and rot, and the rotting uses up the dissolved oxygen in the water. With the oxygen gone, fish suffocate. The very nutrient that grows plants on land ends up killing life in the water.
⚠️ How much detail on nitrates/phosphates?
For 0620 you must know that nitrates and phosphates cause deoxygenation of the water and damage to aquatic life — that phrase scores the mark. The full step-by-step eutrophication mechanism is not required, so don't burn revision time memorising it.
⚠️ Where the pollutants come from
Nitrates → from fertilisers Phosphates → from fertilisers AND detergents Sewage → harmful microbes → disease
04 / 10 · 10.1 Treating the domestic water supply

From murky river to safe tap — three jobs

🚰 Three problems, three fixes, in order
Raw water has three faults: solid bits, bad taste and smell, and living microbes. Water treatment is just three stages that each tackle one fault — first get the solids out, then fix the taste, then kill the germs. Do them in that order and murky river water becomes safe tap water.
  1. Sedimentation and filtration — let heavy solids settle out (sedimentation), then pass the water through filter beds (e.g. sand) to remove the remaining solids.
  2. Carbon — pass the water through carbon (charcoal), which removes tastes and odours by adsorbing the substances that cause them.
  3. Chlorination — add a small, controlled amount of chlorine to kill harmful microbes, making the water safe to drink.
☕ Like making a clean cup of filter coffee
You strain out the coffee grounds (sedimentation & filtration = removing solids), you'd never drink water that smelt off so you run it through a charcoal filter (taste and odour), and finally you'd boil or treat it to be sure nothing living is left (chlorination). The municipal plant does exactly this, at the scale of a whole city.
✅ Match each stage to its job
Examiners love "state what each treatment stage does". Answer: filtration/sedimentation → removes solids; carbon → removes tastes and odours; chlorination → kills microbes. One purpose per stage, in that order.
⚠️ Filtration does NOT kill germs
Filtering only takes out solids — microbes are far too small to trap and pass straight through. That's why chlorination is a separate, essential final step. "The water was filtered so it's germ-free" loses the mark.
05 / 10 · 10.2 Fertilisers

Fertilisers — feeding crops the elements they run short of

🌱 Plants are hungry for three elements
Crops pull nutrients out of the soil, and season after season the soil runs low on three in particular: nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P) and potassium (K). Fertilisers just put those three back so plants grow strong. Nitrogen especially reaches the plant as nitrate or ammonium ions dissolved in soil water.
Nitrogen fertilisers — ammonium salts (e.g. ammonium nitrate NH₄NO₃, ammonium sulfate (NH₄)₂SO₄) and nitrates are used as fertilisers because they supply nitrogen in a form plant roots can take up.
NPK fertiliser — a fertiliser that supplies the three key elements together: Nitrogen, Phosphorus and K (potassium) — used to give improved plant growth.
💊 A multivitamin for the field
An NPK bag is the crop's multivitamin: N for leafy green growth, P for roots, K for overall health and flowering. Just as you'd take a supplement when your diet is short of something, farmers add NPK when the soil is short of these elements — the numbers on the bag (like 10-26-26) are the N:P:K ratio.
✅ The two things to be able to state
(1) Ammonium salts and nitrates are used as fertilisers. (2) NPK fertilisers provide nitrogen, phosphorus and potassium for improved plant growth. Those two sentences are the whole of 10.2 — know them word-perfect.
⚠️ Fertilisers link straight back to water
N, P, K = the three fertiliser elements Run-off → nitrates & phosphates in rivers → deoxygenation
06 / 10 · 10.3 Clean air & the pollutants

What clean air is — and the five things we add to it

Start from the baseline. Clean, dry air is roughly:

78% N₂ 21% O₂ ·

≈ 78% nitrogen (N₂) · 21% oxygen (O₂) · the remaining ~1% = a mixture of noble gases and carbon dioxide (CO₂).

💨 A pollutant is just "the wrong thing, or too much of it"
The air's job is done by N₂ and O₂. A pollutant is anything we add that harms health or the environment — mostly the leftovers of burning fuels. Four of the five come from combustion; the fifth (methane) comes from rotting matter and animals. Nail down each pollutant's source and its effect and this section is done.

⚠️ Complete vs incomplete combustion
Complete combustion → CO₂ (plenty of O₂) Incomplete combustion → CO + particulates (too little O₂) SO₂ ← sulfur in the fuel · NOₓ ← made in the hot engine
⚠️ CO₂ is not a "poison" — CO is
Carbon dioxide is a natural part of clean air; its problem is the extra we add warming the planet. Carbon monoxide is the toxic one — it's odourless and stops your blood carrying oxygen. Don't swap their effects: CO₂ → global warming; CO → toxic.
07 / 10 · 10.3 The greenhouse effect & climate change

Why more CO₂ and methane warm the planet (Supplement)

🔥 A one-way heat trap
Sunlight passes through the atmosphere and warms the ground. The warm ground then radiates that energy back out as thermal (infrared) energy. Greenhouse gases like CO₂ and methane soak up that outgoing thermal energy and re-emit some of it back down — so less heat escapes to space and the surface stays warmer. Add more of these gases and the trap tightens: global warming.
The greenhouse mechanism — some incoming sunlight is reflected, but the greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄) absorb the thermal energy radiated by the Earth's surface and emit some of it back towards the ground (rather than letting it all pass out to space). This reduces thermal energy loss to space, so the surface warms — leading to climate change.
🚗 The parked-car / blanket effect
Leave a car in the sun with the windows up: light gets in and warms the seats, but the glass traps the heat the seats give off, so it's like an oven inside. Greenhouse gases are the "glass" of the planet — or a blanket that lets you warm up but won't let your body heat escape. A thin blanket is fine; keep piling on layers (more CO₂) and you overheat.

Strategies to reduce climate change

Take CO₂ out / add less
Plant trees (they absorb CO₂ by photosynthesis). Decrease use of fossil fuels. Increase use of hydrogen and renewable energy — e.g. wind, solar.
Cut methane
Reduction in livestock farming — fewer cattle means less methane from animal digestion.
✅ Answer with "absorb → emit → less loss to space"
A full-mark greenhouse answer names the chain: greenhouse gases absorb thermal energy from the Earth and emit some back down, reducing thermal energy lost to space, so the Earth warms. Naming the gases (CO₂ and methane) and the energy as thermal/infrared secures the marks.
08 / 10 · 10.3 Acid rain, engines & catalytic converters

Acid rain — and cleaning up what causes it

🌧️ Acidic oxides + rain = acid rain
Two pollutants are acidic oxides: sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and oxides of nitrogen (NOₓ). When they dissolve in rain water they form acids, and the rain falls as acid rain — which corrodes buildings and statues, kills trees, and acidifies lakes. Stop acid rain by stopping SO₂ and NOₓ getting out.
⚠️ Which pollutants cause acid rain
Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) → acid rain Oxides of nitrogen (NOₓ) → acid rain + photochemical smog + respiratory problems

How oxides of nitrogen form in a car engine (Supplement)

Air is 78% N₂ and 21% O₂ — normally they ignore each other. But inside a running engine it is so hot that the nitrogen and oxygen from the air are forced to react:

N₂ + O₂ → 2NO

So the NOₓ isn't from the fuel at all — the engine makes it from the air itself because of the high temperature.

Catalytic converters remove it again (Supplement)

Catalytic converter — a device in a vehicle's exhaust whose catalyst lets the harmful gases react together and cancel out. It removes carbon monoxide and oxides of nitrogen in one reaction:
2CO + 2NO → 2CO₂ + N₂

Elegant: a toxic gas (CO) and an acid-rain gas (NO) destroy each other, leaving harmless nitrogen and (less harmful) carbon dioxide.

Cutting sulfur dioxide

Low-sulfur fuels
Remove sulfur compounds from the fuel before burning, so little SO₂ can form in the first place.
Flue gas desulfurisation
Scrub SO₂ out of power-station waste gases using calcium oxide (CaO) — a base that neutralises the acidic SO₂: CaO + SO₂ → CaSO₃.
✅ Two acid-rain strategies, named
For acid rain, quote: catalytic converters in vehicles (remove NOₓ and CO) and reducing SO₂ emissions by using low-sulfur fuels and flue gas desulfurisation with calcium oxide. Pair each strategy to the gas it targets.
⚠️ Don't confuse acid rain with global warming
Acid rain is caused by SO₂ and NOₓ (acidic oxides dissolving in rain). Global warming is caused by CO₂ and CH₄ (greenhouse gases trapping heat). They're different problems with different gases, effects and fixes — a very common mix-up in the exam.
09 / 10 · 10.3 Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis — nature's own CO₂ remover

🌿 The reaction that runs the greenhouse effect backwards
Green plants do the opposite of a car exhaust: they take CO₂ out of the air and lock it into sugar, releasing oxygen. Using light energy captured by the green pigment chlorophyll, carbon dioxide and water are combined into glucose and oxygen. This is exactly why "planting trees" is a climate-change strategy — more plants means more CO₂ pulled back out.
Photosynthesis — the reaction between carbon dioxide and water to produce glucose and oxygen, in the presence of chlorophyll and using energy from light.

The equations

carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen
6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂   (Supplement)
🏭 A solar-powered sugar factory
Think of a leaf as a tiny factory running on sunlight. Raw materials — CO₂ from the air and water from the roots — go in; the chlorophyll acts like the solar panel that powers the machinery; and out comes glucose (the plant's food/fuel) plus oxygen as the by-product. The oxygen you're breathing right now was released by exactly this reaction.
✅ Word equation (Core) & symbol equation (Supplement)
Core needs the word equation: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen, with the conditions chlorophyll and light. Supplement needs the balanced symbol equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Check it balances: 6 C, 12 H, 18 O each side.
⚠️ Glucose is C₆H₁₂O₆ — and the equation must balance
A common slip is unbalanced coefficients. You need 6 CO₂ and 6 H₂O to make one glucose and 6 O₂. Miss the sixes and the carbons/oxygens won't tally.
