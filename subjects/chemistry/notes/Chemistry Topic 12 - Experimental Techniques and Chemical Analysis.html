01 / 11 · The thread

Every analysis is: separate → purify → identify

🔬 The one mental model
A chemist handed an unknown sample runs the same three-step pipeline every time. Separate the mixture into its parts (filtration, distillation, chromatography). Purify a part and check it really is pure (sharp melting/boiling point). Then identify what it is with a specific test that gives a specific observation. Almost every mark in Topic 12 is one rung of that ladder.
🕵️ A detective's evidence lab
Think of a crime-scene sample. First you separate the muddy mixture into individual substances, then you clean each one up so it's not contaminated, then you run a test that fingerprints it — "white precipitate with silver nitrate → it's chloride". Chemical analysis is forensic work: the right tool, then the tell-tale sign.

02 / 11 · 12.1 Experimental design

Pick the right apparatus for what you're measuring

📏 Four quantities, one tool each
Almost every school experiment measures one of four things — time, temperature, mass and volume. Learn which instrument owns each quantity, and for volume, know that a burette and pipette are the precise pair, while a measuring cylinder is the quick-and-rough one.
QuantityApparatusWhat it's for
TimeStop-watchTiming a reaction (e.g. rate experiments).
TemperatureThermometerReading temperature and temperature changes (energetics).
MassBalanceWeighing solids and finding mass changes.
Volume of liquidBurette · volumetric pipette · measuring cylinderBurette & pipette = accurate volumes (titrations); measuring cylinder = quick approximate volumes.
Volume of gasGas syringeCollecting and measuring a volume of gas made in a reaction.
⚖️ Advantages & disadvantages — the examiner's favourite compare
For any method you should be able to weigh up its pros and cons. A gas syringe measures gas volume accurately but can stick or leak; collecting gas over water is cheaper but no good for gases that are very soluble in water (like NH₃, which would just dissolve). A burette reads to 0.05 cm³ but is slow; a measuring cylinder is fast but far less precise. Match the apparatus to how much accuracy the experiment actually needs.
Precise volume: burette & pipette
A volumetric pipette delivers one fixed, accurate volume (e.g. 25.0 cm³). A burette delivers any variable volume and is read to 0.05 cm³. These are the titration pair.
Quick volume: measuring cylinder
Fast and simple, but only accurate to about ±0.5–1 cm³. Fine for "roughly 50 cm³ of water", wrong for a titration.
⚠️ "Accurate" vs "quick" is the trade-off they test
Don't call a measuring cylinder "wrong" — it's just less precise. The skill is choosing: if the number feeds a calculation (a titre, reacting mass), use the burette/pipette; if you only need an approximate amount, the measuring cylinder saves time. Naming the trade-off scores the mark.
03 / 11 · 12.1 Key definitions

The vocabulary of dissolving, filtering and evaporating

🧂 Six words examiners want back exactly
These are cheap definition marks — but only if you use the precise word. When salt dissolves in water: the salt is the solute, the water is the solvent, and together they make a solution. What passes through a filter is the filtrate; what's left behind is the residue.
Solute — a substance that is dissolved in a solvent (e.g. the salt).
Solvent — a substance that dissolves a solute (e.g. the water).
Solution — a mixture of one or more solutes dissolved in a solvent.
Saturated solution — a solution containing the maximum concentration of a solute dissolved in the solvent at a specified temperature.
Residue — the substance that remains after evaporation, distillation, filtration or a similar process.
Filtrate — the liquid or solution that has passed through a filter.
⚠️ "At a specified temperature" is part of the saturated definition
A solution's maximum concentration depends on temperature — hot water usually dissolves more solute than cold. So a saturated solution is only saturated at that temperature; drop those four words and the definition loses the mark.
⚠️ Filtrate vs residue — don't swap them
Filtrate = liquid that goes THROUGH the filter Residue = solid left ON the filter paper
04 / 11 · 12.4 Separation & purification

Five ways to pull a mixture apart

🧪 Choose the method from the difference you can exploit
Every separation technique works by exploiting one property difference between the substances. Solubility, particle size, or boiling point. Get the difference and the method picks itself: solids that won't dissolve → filter; a dissolved solid you want back → crystallise; a liquid from a solution → distil; two mixed liquids → fractional distillation.

🍚 Crystallisation vs evaporation — the sugar-syrup test
To get sugar crystals back from syrup you don't boil it dry — you heat it to a hot saturated solution, then let it cool slowly so clean crystals grow. Boiling to dryness would scorch the sugar. That's why crystallisation is used for solids that would decompose if heated strongly, and evaporation-to-dryness is only for heat-stable solids.
⚠️ Simple vs fractional distillation
Simple distillation → solvent FROM a solution (e.g. pure water from seawater) Fractional distillation → two+ LIQUIDS with different boiling points (e.g. ethanol/water, crude oil)
✅ "Suggest a method" questions
Given an unknown mixture, state the property difference then the method: "salt and sand — sand is insoluble, so add water, filter (sand = residue), then evaporate/crystallise the salt solution". Always justify with the property, don't just name the technique.
05 / 11 · 12.4 Assessing purity

A pure substance melts and boils sharply

🌡️ Purity has a temperature signature
A pure substance melts at one sharp temperature and boils at one sharp temperature — its fixed physical constants. An impurity smears those out: it lowers and broadens the melting point (melts over a range, below the true value) and raises the boiling point. So the melting/boiling behaviour is itself a purity test.
Identifying & checking purity — you can identify a substance by comparing its measured melting point / boiling point with known (data-book) values, and assess its purity: a pure substance melts/boils at a sharp, fixed temperature; impurities give a lower, broader melting range and a raised boiling point.
Pure substance
Melts at a single sharp temperature; boils at a single sharp temperature. Example: pure ice melts at exactly 0 °C, pure water boils at exactly 100 °C.
Impure substance
Melts over a range and at a lower temperature; boils at a higher temperature. The wider the range, the more impurity present.
✅ Two jobs one measurement does
Melting/boiling point data lets you do both: identify (match the value to a known substance) and check purity (sharp = pure, range = impure). Chromatography does the same double duty for coloured mixtures — a pure substance gives one spot.
06 / 11 · 12.2 Acid–base titrations

Titration — measuring exactly how much neutralises what

🎯 Add drop-by-drop until the indicator flips
A titration answers "how much acid exactly neutralises this alkali?". You put a measured volume of one solution in a flask with an indicator, then run the other solution in from a burette, swirling, until the indicator just changes colour. That colour flip — the end-point — marks the instant the reaction is exactly complete.
  1. Use a volumetric pipette to add a fixed, accurate volume of one solution (say the alkali) to a conical flask.
  2. Add a few drops of a suitable indicator (e.g. phenolphthalein or methyl orange).
  3. Fill a burette with the second solution (the acid) and record the starting reading.
  4. Run the acid in slowly, swirling, until the indicator just changes colour — the end-point.
  5. Record the final burette reading; the titre = final − initial reading.
  6. Repeat until you get concordant titres (within 0.10 cm³) and take the mean.
End-point — the point in a titration, shown by the indicator changing colour, at which one solution has exactly neutralised the other (the reaction is just complete).
🎨 Like tinting paint one drop at a time
Adding acid to the flask is like adding tint to a tin of white paint drop by drop — nothing looks different for ages, then one more drop suddenly shifts the whole colour. In a titration that one-drop flip of the indicator is the end-point: overshoot it and you've gone too far.
✅ Why an indicator, not litmus paper
A titration needs a sharp, single-drop colour change, so you use a suitable indicator solution — phenolphthalein (pink in alkali, colourless in acid) or methyl orange (yellow in alkali, red in acid); the colour flips whichever way you run the titration. Universal indicator or litmus change too gradually to spot the exact end-point. The volumes → moles/concentration calculation itself lives in Topic 3 (stoichiometry).
⚠️ Pipette the flask, burette the drip
A classic slip is swapping the apparatus. The pipette puts the fixed known volume into the flask; the burette delivers the variable measured volume you're finding. Read the burette at the bottom of the meniscus, at eye level.
07 / 11 · 12.3 Chromatography & Rf

Chromatography — separating a mixture by how fast it travels

🖊️ A race up the paper
Spot a mixture near the bottom of paper and stand it in solvent. As the solvent creeps up, it carries each substance along — but more-soluble substances travel further and less-soluble ones lag. They spread into separate spots, like runners finishing a race at different distances. One spot = a pure substance; several spots = a mixture.
Paper chromatography — separates a mixture of soluble coloured substances using a suitable solvent. Each substance travels a different distance up the paper, so the mixture spreads into separate spots.

Reading a chromatogram

Identify unknowns
Run the unknown alongside known substances. If an unknown's spot travels the same distance (same Rf) as a known, they're the same substance.
Pure or impure?
A pure substance gives a single spot. An impure substance (a mixture) separates into two or more spots.
⚠️ Colourless substances need a locating agent (Supplement)
If the substances are colourless, their spots are invisible. After the run you spray the paper with a locating agent, which reacts to make the spots show up. (You don't need to name specific locating agents.) The pencil baseline is drawn in pencil so it doesn't dissolve and run.

The Rf value (Supplement)

Rf value — a ratio that fingerprints how far a substance travels:
Rf = distance travelled by substance ÷ distance travelled by solvent
It has no units and is always between 0 and 1 (the substance can't outrun the solvent that carries it).

Drag the spot to see how its Rf comes out. The solvent front is fixed at 8.0 cm from the pencil baseline:

0.63
5.0 cm ÷ 8.0 cm
✅ Rf is a ratio of distances — measure from the baseline
Measure both distances from the pencil baseline: the substance to the centre of its spot, the solvent to the solvent front. Divide. A worked example: spot at 3.6 cm, solvent front at 4.8 cm → Rf = 3.6 ÷ 4.8 = 0.75. Same solvent + same paper → same Rf, so it identifies the substance.
08 / 11 · 12.5 Identifying the negative ions

Tests for anions — the reagent-and-observation table

🧫 A specific reagent for each negative ion
Each anion has a signature test — add the right reagent, watch for the specific gas, precipitate colour or colour change. The three you'll meet most are the carbonate fizz, the silver-nitrate halide colours, and the barium-sulfate white precipitate. Learn the exact reagents and the exact observation.
AnionTestPositive result
Carbonate, CO₃²⁻Add dilute acidEffervescence; the gas turns limewater milky (CO₂).
Chloride, Cl⁻Acidify with dilute nitric acid, then add aqueous silver nitrateWhite precipitate (AgCl).
Bromide, Br⁻Acidify with dilute nitric acid, then add aqueous silver nitrateCream precipitate (AgBr).
Iodide, I⁻Acidify with dilute nitric acid, then add aqueous silver nitrateYellow precipitate (AgI).
Nitrate, NO₃⁻Add aqueous sodium hydroxide, then aluminium foil; warmAmmonia gas given off (turns damp red litmus blue).
Sulfate, SO₄²⁻Acidify with dilute nitric acid, then add aqueous barium nitrateWhite precipitate (BaSO₄).
Sulfite, SO₃²⁻Add acidified aqueous potassium manganate(VII)The purple manganate(VII) is decolourised.
⚠️ The silver-nitrate halide colours (white → cream → yellow)
Cl⁻ → WHITE Br⁻ → CREAM I⁻ → YELLOW Down the group, the precipitate deepens in colour.
⚠️ Acidify FIRST — with the right acid
For chloride/bromide/iodide you acidify with dilute nitric acid before the silver nitrate (and for sulfate before the barium nitrate). The acid removes carbonate ions that would otherwise give a false white precipitate. Use the acid named in the syllabus — nitric — not hydrochloric (which would add its own chloride!).
09 / 11 · 12.5 Identifying the positive ions

Tests for cations — sodium hydroxide, ammonia & flame colours

🎨 Precipitate colour, then "does it redissolve?"
Add aqueous sodium hydroxide (or aqueous ammonia) drop by drop: most metal ions form a coloured hydroxide precipitate. The colour narrows it down — and whether the precipitate dissolves in excess reagent splits the look-alikes apart (that's how you tell zinc from calcium, or copper's two reagents apart). Tap each cation:
With aqueous sodium hydroxide (NaOH)
With aqueous ammonia (NH₃)
⚠️ The "soluble in excess" shortlist
Dissolve in EXCESS NaOH: Al³⁺, Zn²⁺, Cr³⁺ Dissolve in EXCESS NH₃: Cu²⁺ (deep blue), Zn²⁺ (colourless) White ppts: Al³⁺, Ca²⁺, Zn²⁺ — tell apart by "excess" behaviour

Flame tests

Some metal cations give a characteristic flame colour. Dip a clean wire in the sample and hold it in a hot blue Bunsen flame:

Li⁺ → red
Na⁺ → yellow
K⁺ → lilac
Ca²⁺ → orange-red
Ba²⁺ → light green
Cu²⁺ → blue-green
✅ Two independent lines of evidence
Cations get tested two ways: the hydroxide precipitate (colour + excess behaviour) and, for some, a flame colour. Ca²⁺, for instance, gives a white precipitate with NaOH (insoluble in excess) and an orange-red flame — quote both for a confident identification.
⚠️ Iron(II) green vs iron(III) red-brown
Don't merge the two irons. Fe²⁺ → green precipitate; Fe³⁺ → red-brown precipitate (both insoluble in excess). And copper(II) is the tell-tale: a light blue precipitate that dissolves in excess ammonia to a deep blue solution.
10 / 11 · 12.5 Identifying gases

Tests for gases — the six one-line checks

💨 A quick bench test for each gas
Each gas has a fast, memorable test — a splint, a piece of damp litmus, or a colour change. These are guaranteed marks and easy to mix up, so drill the exact wording: which splint (lighted vs glowing), which litmus (damp red vs damp), and what happens.
GasTestPositive result
Ammonia, NH₃Damp red litmus paperTurns litmus blue (it's alkaline).
Carbon dioxide, CO₂Bubble through limewaterLimewater turns milky (white precipitate).
Chlorine, Cl₂Damp litmus paperBleaches the litmus (turns it white).
Hydrogen, H₂Lighted splintBurns with a "squeaky pop".
Oxygen, O₂Glowing splintRelights the glowing splint.
Sulfur dioxide, SO₂Acidified aqueous potassium manganate(VII)Changes from purple to colourless.
⚠️ Lighted vs glowing splint — the classic swap
Hydrogen → LIGHTED splint → squeaky pop Oxygen → GLOWING splint → relights Chlorine BLEACHES litmus · Ammonia turns red litmus BLUE
⚠️ Chlorine "bleaches" — it doesn't just turn one colour
Chlorine is acidic and a bleach: damp litmus may briefly turn red, but the tell-tale sign is that it then goes white (bleached). Write "bleaches the litmus", not merely "turns it red", or you'll be describing any acid gas.
