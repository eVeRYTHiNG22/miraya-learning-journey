01 / 06 · 1.1 Kinetic particle theory

The one idea behind the whole topic

All matter is made of incredibly small particles (atoms, molecules or ions) that are always moving. How much energy those particles have — and so how fast they move and how tightly they're held — is the only thing that changes between a solid, a liquid and a gas. This is the kinetic particle theory ("kinetic" just means moving).

💡 The core picture
Heat a substance and you are really just feeding energy to its particles — they move faster and push each other further apart. Cool it and they slow down and pack closer. Solid → liquid → gas is the same particles, getting more and more energetic. Nothing new appears; the particles just rearrange.
🎟️ The concert analogy
Picture people in a hall. Solid = seated in neat rows, only able to fidget in their seats. Liquid = standing crowd, shuffling and sliding past each other but still shoulder-to-shoulder. Gas = everyone sprinting around an empty stadium, far apart. Same people — only their energy and spacing changed.
⚠️ Must say it precisely
Particles in a substance are in constant, random motion. The hotter the substance, the more (kinetic) energy its particles have and the faster they move. This single sentence explains state changes, gas pressure and diffusion — examiners reward it everywhere.

Properties & particle picture, side by side

Solid
Fixed shape, fixed volume; cannot be compressed. Particles packed closely in a regular pattern, touching, held by strong forces. They only vibrate about fixed positions — lowest energy.
Liquid
Fixed volume but no fixed shape (takes the container's shape); almost incompressible. Particles still close together but randomly arranged, and they can slide past one another — more energy than a solid.
Gas
No fixed shape or volume (fills its container); easily compressed. Particles far apart, random, with negligible forces between them, moving quickly in all directions — highest energy.
⚠️ The wording trap
Don't write "particles in a gas have no forces" — say negligible (very weak) forces. And a gas is "easily compressed" because the particles are far apart (lots of empty space), not because the particles themselves shrink — particles never change size.
✅ Score the full description
For every state, answer the three things examiners look for: arrangement (regular / random), separation (close / far apart) and motion (vibrate / slide / move freely). Hitting all three is how you earn full marks, not just "particles move".
03 / 06 · 1.1 Changes of state

Changing state — adding or removing energy

Heat a substance and its particles gain energy; cool it and they lose energy. At certain temperatures this tips it from one state into another. The five changes you must name:

Meltingsolid → liquidtaken inthe melting point

🌫️ Evaporation vs boiling — the classic mix-up
Boiling is loud and bulk: at the boiling point, bubbles of gas form all through the liquid. Evaporation is quiet and slow: only the fastest particles at the surface escape, and it happens at any temperature — which is how a puddle dries up on a cool day and why sweat cools you down.

💡 Why the temperature pauses
During melting or boiling, the energy you supply is not raising the temperature — it is being used to overcome the forces of attraction holding the particles together (pulling them apart into the next state). Temperature only climbs again once the change of state is complete. That's why a pure substance has a sharp, constant melting and boiling point.
✅ Particle changes during a state change (Extended)
Be ready to describe what happens to arrangement, separation and motion. Melting: regular → random, particles slightly further apart, vibration becomes sliding. Boiling: particles move far apart, forces become negligible, motion becomes fast and free. State both directions if asked.
04 / 06 · 1.1 Gases — temperature & pressure

How temperature & pressure change a gas's volume

A gas pushes outwards because its particles are constantly hitting the walls of the container — each collision is a tiny push. Add up billions of collisions per second over an area and you get gas pressure. Change the temperature or squeeze the container and the volume responds. Drag the sliders:

Reading the particles
Higher temperature → particles gain energy and move faster → they hit the piston harder and more often → at constant pressure the piston rises and the volume increases (the gas expands). Higher pressure (pushing the piston down) forces the particles into less space → the volume decreases.
Effect of temperature (at constant pressure) — heating a gas makes its particles move faster, so they strike the walls harder and more frequently. To keep the pressure the same the gas must expand: volume increases with temperature.
Effect of pressure (at constant temperature) — increasing the pressure squeezes the same particles into a smaller space, so the volume decreases. Pushed into a smaller space, the particles hit each unit of wall more often — which is also why squeezing a gas raises its pressure.
⚠️ Explain pressure correctly
Gas pressure is caused by particles colliding with the container walls — say "more frequent collisions and harder collisions", not just "particles move faster". A common lost mark is forgetting that both the force per collision and the number of collisions per second matter.
✅ Extended must-know
You must be able to explain these effects using the kinetic particle model, not just state them. Frame every answer around speed of particles → frequency & force of wall collisions → resulting change in volume (or pressure).
05 / 06 · 1.2 Diffusion

Diffusion — particles spreading on their own

Diffusion — the net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, caused by the random motion of the particles. No stirring needed; it happens because particles are always moving and bumping around. It is fastest in gases (particles fast and far apart) and slower in liquids.
💡 Why it only goes one way (at first)
Particles move randomly in all directions. But where they're crowded, more of them happen to wander outwards than inwards — so the crowd spreads until it's evenly mixed. Once spread out, movement still happens but there is no longer a net flow. Think of perfume filling a room from one corner.

The ammonia & hydrogen chloride experiment (Extended)

Two gases are released at opposite ends of a long glass tube. Where they meet, they react to form a white ring of solid ammonium chloride. Press play and watch where the ring forms:

What you're seeing
Both gases diffuse along the tube. Ammonia particles (Mr 17) are lighter, so at the same temperature they travel faster and cover more of the tube. The white ring of NH₄Cl therefore forms nearer the hydrochloric acid end.
Rate of diffusion vs molecular mass (Extended) — at the same temperature, particles with a smaller relative molecular mass (Mr) move faster, so they diffuse faster. NH₃ (Mr 17) out-runs HCl (Mr 36.5), so they meet closer to the HCl end. Reaction: NH₃ + HCl → NH₄Cl.
🏃 Light particles are faster runners
Give every particle the same energy (same temperature) and the lighter ones end up moving faster — like throwing a tennis ball and a cricket ball with the same effort; the lighter ball flies off quicker. Lighter gas → faster diffusion → travels further before they meet.
✅ Two factors that speed up diffusion
Diffusion is faster when (1) the particles have a lower Mr (lighter) and (2) the temperature is higher (more kinetic energy). Quote both if a question asks how to make a gas diffuse more quickly.
