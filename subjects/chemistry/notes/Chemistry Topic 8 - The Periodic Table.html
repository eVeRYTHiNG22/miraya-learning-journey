The Periodic Table

One idea runs through the whole topic: where an element sits tells you how it behaves. Elements are lined up by proton number and stacked so that families with the same number of outer electrons fall into the same column — so a column shares chemistry, and a row drifts from metal to non-metal. Explore the live table, then let the halogen-displacement selector rank the family by reactivity.

01 / 09 · 8.1 Arrangement of elements

The table is a seating plan for the elements

🗺️ The one mental model
The Periodic Table is just every element lined up in order of increasing proton number, then folded into rows so that elements which behave alike stack into the same column. Nothing is random: the position of a box is its chemistry. Learn to read the address — which group (column) and which period (row) — and you can predict how an element reacts before you've ever seen it.
Periodic Table — an arrangement of the elements in order of increasing proton number (atomic number), set out in horizontal rows called periods and vertical columns called groups.
Period = a row
A horizontal row. The period number tells you how many electron shells the atoms have (Period 3 → 3 shells).
Group = a column
A vertical column. The group number tells you how many electrons are in the outer shell (Group VII → 7 outer electrons).

🎬 A cinema hall seating plan
Think of a Mumbai multiplex. Everyone has a row (period) and a seat number (group). Once you know a person's row and seat, you know exactly where they are and who their neighbours are — without seeing them. The Periodic Table is that seating plan for atoms: the address predicts the behaviour.
02 / 09 · 8.1 Reading an element's position

What the address tells you — charge, family, character

🔑 It all comes back to outer electrons
Chemistry is a game of getting a full outer shell. An element's group number = its number of outer-shell electrons, and that single fact decides the ion it makes, the family it belongs to, and how metallic it is. Same column → same outer electrons → same chemistry. That's the deep reason the table works.

1 · Group number → the charge of the ion

Metals on the left lose their few outer electrons; non-metals on the right gain electrons to fill their shell. The group number predicts the charge every time:

✅ The shortcut examiners reward
For a metal, ion charge = group number (Group II → 2+). For a non-metal, ion charge = group number − 8 (Group VI → 6−8 = 2−). Noble gases (Group VIII) already have full shells, so they form no ions.

2 · Same group → similar chemical properties

Why a group is a family — elements in the same group have the same number of outer-shell electrons (the same electronic configuration in the outer shell), so they react in the same way and form ions of the same charge. This is the explanation to give for family behaviour.

3 · Across a period → metal turns into non-metal

➡️ The left-to-right drift
Walk along any period from left to right and the character changes smoothly: metals on the left → metalloids in the middle → non-metals on the right. In Period 3, sodium and magnesium are shiny reactive metals; silicon in the middle is a metalloid; phosphorus, sulfur and chlorine are non-metals; argon is an unreactive gas.

⚠️ "Group number = period number" — no
They're different addresses. Group = column = outer electrons; period = row = number of shells. Sodium is in Group I, Period 3 — one outer electron, three shells. Don't mix the two axes.
03 / 09 · 8.2 Group I properties

Group I — the alkali metals

Group I — the alkali metals — lithium, sodium and potassium are soft, silvery metals with one outer electron. They are so reactive they are stored under oil and react with water to give an alkali (a metal hydroxide) plus hydrogen — hence the family name.
🪨 Not the "metal" you expect
Forget hard, heavy iron. Alkali metals are soft enough to cut with a knife, so light that lithium, sodium and potassium float on water, and shiny only for a second before the air tarnishes them. One lonely outer electron makes them desperate to react — they'd rather be an ion than an atom.

(The density climb is the overall trend to Rb and Cs; potassium dips very slightly below sodium — but the direction examiners want is "density increases down the group".)

Reaction with water — the classic demo

Every alkali metal reacts with cold water to give a hydroxide (an alkali) and hydrogen gas. The reaction gets more violent down the group:

Lithium
Floats and fizzes steadily, moving gently across the surface.
Sodium
Melts into a shiny ball from the heat, whizzes about fizzing rapidly.
Potassium
Ignites instantly with a lilac flame, moving fast — sometimes a small pop.
2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂
2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂
✅ Why they get MORE reactive down the group
Reacting means losing the single outer electron. Going down, that outer electron sits in a shell further from the nucleus (and is shielded by more inner shells), so it is held less tightly and lost more easily — reactivity rises. Naming this "outer electron lost more easily" scores the explanation mark.
🛢️ Why the school lab keeps sodium under oil
Your teacher fishes a lump of sodium out of a jar of kerosene / oil and cuts it to show the shiny inside before it dulls. It lives under oil because it would otherwise react with the moisture and oxygen in the air of a humid Indian classroom. Reactivity this high means the metal can never be left out in the open.
⚠️ "Soft, light metals that float and burn" is the whole point
Students lose marks assuming all metals are hard, dense and slow to react. Group I metals are the opposite: soft, low-density, low melting point, and violently reactive. That contrast (especially against the transition metals later) is exam gold.
04 / 09 · 8.3 Group VII properties

Group VII — the halogens

Group VII — the halogens — chlorine, bromine and iodine are diatomic non-metals (they exist as Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ molecules) with seven outer electrons. They are one electron short of a full shell, so they gain one electron to form 1− ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻).
🎨 Read the family by its colour and state
Going down Group VII the elements get darker and denser and their state marches gas → liquid → solid. And — the crucial twist — unlike Group I, reactivity DECREASES down the group. A halogen reacts by grabbing an electron, and the further the outer shell is from the nucleus, the weaker the pull, so the harder it is to grab one.

🧴 Halogens you've actually met
Chlorine is what makes swimming-pool and tap water safe to drink — it kills microbes. Iodine is the brown tincture of iodine antiseptic dabbed on a cut, and the "iodised" part of iodised salt that prevents goitre. Same family, very different everyday jobs — but all reactive non-metals.
⚠️ Group VII reactivity runs the OPPOSITE way to Group I
Group I: reactivity increases down (easier to lose an electron). Group VII: reactivity decreases down (harder to gain an electron). So the most reactive halogen is at the top (fluorine, then chlorine); the most reactive alkali metal is at the bottom. Mixing these up is the classic slip.
05 / 09 · 8.3 Halogen displacement

Displacement — the reactivity contest you can see

🥇 A more reactive halogen bullies out a less reactive one
Add a halogen to a solution of another halogen's salt (a halide). If the added halogen is more reactive, it steals the salt's place and kicks out the less reactive halogen, which appears in solution as a colour. If it's less reactive, nothing happens. That's a direct ranking of the family — visible as a colour change.

Pick which halogen you add. The three tubes show what happens with chloride, bromide and iodide solutions — colour, verdict and equation:

✅ Reading a displacement result
"A reaction happened" (a colour appears) means the added halogen is more reactive than the one in the salt. So chlorine displaces both bromine and iodine; bromine displaces only iodine; iodine displaces neither. This proves the order Cl > Br > I — reactivity falling down the group.
Cl₂ + 2KBr → 2KCl + Br₂ (turns orange)
Cl₂ + 2KI → 2KCl + I₂ (turns brown)
Br₂ + 2KI → 2KBr + I₂ (turns brown)
⚠️ The colour comes from the halogen SET FREE, not the one you added
When chlorine displaces bromine, the orange colour is the bromine (Br₂) released — the chlorine has become colourless chloride ions. Describe the change as "the solution turns orange/brown as bromine/iodine is displaced", not "the chlorine turns orange".
06 / 09 · 8.4 Transition elements

Transition elements — the "proper metals"

Transition elements — the block of metals in the centre of the table (iron, copper, zinc, chromium, manganese, nickel…). They are hard, dense metals with high melting points that form coloured compounds and often act as catalysts.
🏗️ Everything Group I is not
If alkali metals are soft, light and frantic, transition metals are the opposite — the strong, heavy, dependable metals you actually build with. Iron in railways and utensils, copper in wiring, gold in jewellery. They're much less reactive, so they last. Four signatures give them away: high density, high melting point, coloured compounds, catalytic.
High density & melting point
Iron melts at 1538 °C (density 7.9) and copper at 1085 °C (density 8.9) — compare sodium: melts at just 98 °C, density 0.97.
Coloured compounds
Copper(II) salts are blue, iron(II) pale green, iron(III) orange-brown, potassium manganate(VII) purple. (Group I compounds are white/colourless.)
Catalysts
Speed up reactions without being used up: iron in the Haber process, vanadium(V) oxide in the Contact process, nickel in hardening vegetable oils.
Variable oxidation number (Supplement)
The same metal can form ions of different charges, shown by Roman numerals: iron(II) Fe²⁺ and iron(III) Fe³⁺; copper(I) Cu⁺ and copper(II) Cu²⁺.
🍳 A transition metal in your kitchen
That tin of vanaspati (hydrogenated vegetable oil) is made by bubbling hydrogen through liquid oil over a nickel catalyst — a transition metal doing its classic job. Meanwhile the steel of your pressure cooker (iron), the copper bottom that spreads the heat, and the brass handle are all transition-metal chemistry on the stove.
✅ The four properties to list
Asked to "describe the transition elements", give: (a) high density, (b) high melting points, (c) form coloured compounds, (d) often act as catalysts. For Supplement, add variable oxidation numbers (iron(II) and iron(III)). These are the exact marking points.
⚠️ "Colourless compound" ≠ transition metal
Coloured compounds are a flag for a transition metal, but the reverse test can trip you: a white/colourless salt is more likely Group I or Group II. Zinc is often grouped with the transition block yet its common compounds are white — a reminder to lead with the whole set of properties, not colour alone.
07 / 09 · 8.5 Noble gases

Group VIII — the noble gases

Noble gases (Group VIII / 0) — helium, neon, argon… are unreactive, monatomic gases. They are unreactive because they have a full outer shell of electrons — the stable arrangement every other element is chasing.
😌 The elements that already have what everyone wants
All of chemistry is atoms trying to reach a full outer shell. The noble gases start with one (helium 2; neon 2,8; argon 2,8,8), so they have no reason to react — nothing to gain, lose or share. That's why they're inert, and why they drift around as single atoms (monatomic) instead of pairing up into molecules.
Unreactive (inert)
Full outer shell → no tendency to gain, lose or share electrons → essentially no reactions.
Monatomic
Exist as single atoms (He, Ne, Ar), not molecules — because they don't need to bond to anything, even themselves.
Full outer shell
He: 2 · Ne: 2,8 · Ar: 2,8,8. This stable configuration is the reason for everything above.
💡 Noble gases lighting up the bazaar
The glowing neon shop signs of a market, the argon filling an old tube-light (and shielding a welder's arc), and the helium in a birthday balloon all use noble gases precisely because they don't react — they glow or float or protect without catching fire or corroding.
✅ The mark-scoring sentence
"Noble gases are unreactive because they have a full (complete) outer shell of electrons, so they have no tendency to lose, gain or share electrons." The reason — full outer shell — is what earns the mark, not just "they are inert".
08 / 09 · 8.1 Predicting properties from position

Using the table to predict the unknown

🔮 Trends let you fill in the gaps
Once you know a family's trends, you can predict an element you've never studied just from where it sits. Read the pattern above and below, then continue it. This is exactly the "given information about the elements, predict…" question — worth easy marks if you state the trend and extend it.
Predict: rubidium (Group I, below K)
Continue the Group I trends: even softer, lower melting point than potassium, higher density, and even more reactive with water (would ignite / explode). Still forms a 1+ ion, Rb⁺.
Predict: astatine (Group VII, below I)
Continue the Group VII trends: a dark (black) solid, even denser and higher melting than iodine, and the least reactive halogen. Still forms a 1− ion, At⁻, and would be displaced by all the halogens above it.
Predict: fluorine (Group VII, above Cl)
Above chlorine, so even more reactive — the most reactive halogen of all. A pale yellow gas, lower density than chlorine, and it would displace chlorine, bromine and iodine from their salts.
Predict any element's ion
Use the group number: a Group II element forms a 2+ ion; a Group VI element forms a 2− ion (6 − 8). Position → outer electrons → charge, every time.
✅ How to phrase a prediction (Supplement 8.1.6)
Don't just guess a value — name the trend and continue it: "Group I reactivity increases down the group, so caesium is more reactive than potassium." Quoting the trend as your reason is what turns a lucky guess into a full-mark answer.
⚠️ Two trends that run OPPOSITE ways
Group I (metals): reactivity INCREASES down ↓ Group VII (non-metals): reactivity DECREASES down ↓ Metals lose electrons easier low down; non-metals gain harder low down
