01 / 10 · 2.1 Pure substances & mixtures

Element, compound, mixture — the three things matter can be

🧱 The one mental model
Picture atoms as coloured LEGO bricks. An element is a box of one colour only. A compound is bricks of different colours clicked together in a fixed pattern — to separate them you must break the clicks (a chemical reaction). A mixture is different bricks just tipped into the same box, not clicked — you can pick them apart physically.
Element
One type of atom only. Cannot be broken into anything simpler by chemical means. There are ~118. e.g. O₂, Fe, He, C.
Compound
Two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio. New properties; only split by chemical reaction. e.g. H₂O, CO₂, NaCl.
Mixture
Two or more substances not chemically combined; any ratio. Keep their own properties; split by physical means. e.g. air, sea water, alloys.

🍳 Iron + sulfur, the classic demo
Mix grey iron filings with yellow sulfur powder — a mixture: a magnet still pulls the iron out, and the ratio doesn't matter. Now heat it. It glows and forms iron(II) sulfide (FeS), a compound: a single new black solid, no longer magnetic, fixed 1:1 ratio. The heating is the chemical "click".
✅ Pure vs. impure (Extended link)
A pure substance melts/boils at one sharp temperature. An impurity lowers and spreads the melting point over a range — a quick lab test for purity you can quote in Topic 2 or Topic 12.
02 / 10 · 2.2 Atomic structure

Inside the atom — three particles, two regions

⚛️ Mostly empty space
An atom is a tiny dense nucleus (protons + neutrons) with electrons whizzing around it in shells far out. If the nucleus were a marble at the centre of a cricket stadium, the electrons would be up in the furthest stands — the rest is empty. Almost all the mass sits in that marble; almost all the volume is electron space.

⚠️ Must-memorise charges & masses
Proton +1, mass 1 · Neutron 0, mass 1 · Electron −1, mass ≈ 0. A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons, so the +1s and −1s cancel out.
⚠️ The slip examiners hunt for
The electron's mass is not zero — it's about 1/1840 of a proton, i.e. negligible. Write "negligible", never "no mass". And the nucleus is positive (protons), never the whole atom.
03 / 10 · 2.2 Proton & nucleon number

Two numbers that pin down any atom

Proton number, Z (atomic number) — the number of protons in the nucleus. It is the atom's identity card: change Z and you change the element. The Periodic Table is simply every element lined up in order of Z.
Nucleon number, A (mass number) — the total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus. So neutrons = A − Z, and (in a neutral atom) electrons = Z.

We write these around the symbol as ᴬ𝓏X — for example ²³₁₁Na means 11 protons, 11 electrons, and 23 − 11 = 12 neutrons.

04 / 10 · 2.2 Electronic configuration

Electron shells — and how they predict the Periodic Table

Electrons don't sit just anywhere. They fill shells (energy levels) from the inside out. The rule for the first 20 elements:

⚠️ Shell capacities & filling order
1st shell holds 2, then 2nd holds 8, then 3rd holds 8 (for elements up to calcium). Always fill the inner shell before starting the next. Write configurations comma-separated: oxygen = 2,6 · sodium = 2,8,1 · calcium = 2,8,8,2.
🎯 Why the outer shell is everything
Chemistry is a fight over the outer shell. Atoms react to reach a full outer shell (the super-stable noble-gas arrangement). The number of outer electrons therefore decides how — and how vigorously — an element reacts. Two elements with the same outer count behave alike: that's why the table works.
Group number = outer electrons
An element in Group VII has 7 electrons in its outer shell (e.g. chlorine 2,8,7). Group I → 1 outer electron; Group 0 (noble gases) → a full outer shell.
Period number = number of shells
An element in Period 3 uses 3 shells (e.g. sodium 2,8,1). Going across a period adds electrons to the same outer shell.
✅ Mark-scoring move
Asked to "explain why sodium and lithium have similar properties"? Answer: both have one electron in their outer shell (Group I) — so they form +1 ions the same way. Outer-shell electrons, every time.
⚠️ Noble gases & hydrogen
Helium is 2 — its outer shell is full with just 2, so it sits in Group 0 with the other noble gases, not Group II. And hydrogen (just 1 electron) is a special case usually floated above the table.
05 / 10 · 2.3 Isotopes

Isotopes — same element, different weight

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same proton number) with different numbers of neutrons — so they share Z but differ in nucleon number A.
⚖️ Same identity, different luggage
Add or remove neutrons and you change the atom's mass, but not who it is — protons are the identity, and they're untouched. Chlorine-35 and chlorine-37 are both chlorine: 17 protons each, but 18 vs 20 neutrons.
⚠️ Why isotopes are chemically identical
Same proton number → same number of electrons → same outer-shell arrangement → same chemistry. Isotopes react in exactly the same way. They differ only in physical properties that depend on mass (e.g. density, rate of diffusion).

Relative atomic mass, Aᵣ (Extended)

A natural sample is a mixture of isotopes, so its average mass isn't a whole number. The relative atomic mass is the weighted average of the isotope masses, by abundance:

Relative atomic mass Aᵣ = Σ(isotope mass × % abundance) ÷ 100 — the average mass of an atom relative to 1/₁₂ of a carbon-12 atom.

Drag the abundance of chlorine's two isotopes and watch Aᵣ shift. Real chlorine is ≈ 75% Cl-35 / 25% Cl-37 — which is exactly why its Aᵣ is the oddly non-whole 35.5:

06 / 10 · 2.4 Ions & ionic bonds

Ionic bonding — atoms that give and take electrons

⚡ The whole story in one line
A metal has a few spare outer electrons; a non-metal is short a few. So the metal donates, the non-metal accepts. Both end up with full outer shells, but now one is positive and one is negative — and opposite charges pull together. That pull is the ionic bond.
Ion — a charged particle formed when an atom loses or gains electrons. Metals lose electrons → positive ions (cations). Non-metals gain electrons → negative ions (anions).
Ionic bond — the strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, holding them in a giant lattice.

Why ionic compounds behave as they do

The ions stack into a giant ionic lattice — a regular 3-D grid of alternating + and − ions, held by strong attractions in every direction. That structure explains every property:

⚠️ "Why doesn't solid salt conduct?"
Not because it has no charges — it's full of ions. They simply can't move in the solid lattice. Melt it or dissolve it and the same ions are freed → it conducts. Say "ions are free to move", not "electrons".
07 / 10 · 2.5 Simple molecules & covalent bonds

Covalent bonding — atoms that share

🤝 When neither will give
Two non-metals both want to gain electrons — neither will donate. So they compromise and share a pair: each shared pair counts toward both atoms' full shells. A shared pair is one covalent bond.
Covalent bond — a shared pair of electrons between two non-metal atoms. Share two pairs = a double bond (e.g. O₂, CO₂); three pairs = a triple bond (N₂).

Dot-and-cross: water, H₂O

Oxygen (2,6) needs 2 more electrons; each hydrogen (1) needs 1. Oxygen shares one pair with each H — two bonds — and keeps two lone pairs:

Single bonds
H₂, Cl₂, HCl, H₂O, NH₃, CH₄. One shared pair per bond. Methane (CH₄) shares all 4 of carbon's outer electrons, one with each H.
Double & triple
O₂ = O=O (two pairs). CO₂ = O=C=O. N₂ = N≡N (three pairs) — that strong triple bond is why nitrogen is so unreactive.

Why simple molecules are soft, low-melting non-conductors

⚠️ Strong bonds, weak forces — keep them separate
The covalent bonds inside a molecule are strong. But the intermolecular forces between separate molecules are weak. Melting/boiling only has to overcome those weak forces — so simple molecular substances have low melting and boiling points and many are liquids or gases at room temperature.
⚠️ The trap on "low boiling point"
You are not breaking covalent bonds when water boils — you're only separating whole molecules by overcoming weak intermolecular forces. Writing "the covalent bonds break" loses the mark. Also: simple molecules have no free electrons or ions, so they don't conduct electricity.
08 / 10 · 2.6 Giant covalent structures

Giant covalent — when the bonds never stop

🕸️ One molecule, billions of atoms
Instead of small separate molecules, here every atom is covalently bonded into one endless 3-D network. To melt it you must break strong covalent bonds themselves — so these have very high melting points. Same carbon atoms, two famous arrangements with opposite uses:
Diamond
Each carbon bonds to 4 others in a rigid tetrahedral lattice. Hardest natural substance, very high melting point. No free electrons → does not conduct. Used in cutting/drilling tools.
Graphite
Each carbon bonds to 3 others in flat layers. Spare electrons are delocalised → conducts electricity. Weak forces between layers let them slide → soft, slippery. Used as electrodes & a lubricant.

✅ Silicon(IV) oxide, SiO₂
Sand/quartz is a third giant covalent solid — its structure is like diamond's (each Si bonded to O in a rigid network). So it shares diamond's traits: hard, very high melting point, doesn't conduct.
⚠️ "Graphite is a non-metal that conducts"
It's the classic exception. The reason isn't ions — it's delocalised electrons (one spare per carbon) free to move along the layers. Same physics as a metal, in a non-metal. Diamond has none spare, so diamond does not conduct.
09 / 10 · 2.7 Metallic bonding

Metallic bonding & the three structures side by side

Metallic bond (Extended) — a giant lattice of positive metal ions sitting in a "sea" of delocalised (free) electrons. The bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between the positive ions and that shared electron sea.
🌊 Ions in a shared electron ocean
Metal atoms let their outer electrons roam free across the whole structure. The result explains metals perfectly: the mobile electrons carry current and heat, and because the ion layers can slide over one another without breaking the bond, metals are malleable and ductile (bend & draw into wire).

Flip between the three bonding types — see what's transferred, shared, or pooled:

Covalent

Ionic — transfer
Metal + non-metal. Electrons are transferred, making + and − ions that lock into a giant lattice. High melting point; conducts only when molten or dissolved.
Covalent — share
Non-metal + non-metal. A pair of electrons is shared between the atoms. Simple molecules → low melting points, don't conduct. Giant covalent (diamond/graphite) → very high melting points.
Metallic — pool
Metal atoms only. Outer electrons are delocalised into a shared sea. Free electrons → conducts heat & electricity; sliding ion layers → malleable & ductile.
✅ Quick decision rule for any substance
Metal + non-metal → ionic. Non-metal + non-metal → covalent. Metal only → metallic. Then read the properties straight off the structure.
