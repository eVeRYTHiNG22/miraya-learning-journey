Electrochemistry

One idea runs through it all: electricity pushes electrons onto positive ions and pulls them off negative ions — so it tears compounds apart. Tap the panels — predict the products at each electrode, and flip a real cell through every electrolyte the exam can ask for.

01 / 10 · 4.1 Electrolysis

What electrolysis actually is

⚡ The one mental model
An ionic compound is a crowd of charged ions — positive metal ions and negative non-metal ions — locked in a lattice. Dip in two electrodes hooked to a battery and you create a charged tug-of-war: the negative electrode drags positive ions in and force-feeds them electrons; the positive electrode rips electrons off the negative ions. Electricity literally un-builds the compound back into its elements.
Electrolysis — the decomposition of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current.
⚠️ Why it must be molten or dissolved
The ions have to be free to move to carry the current. In a solid ionic compound the ions are locked in the lattice — it does not conduct, so no electrolysis. Melt it or dissolve it in water and the ions break free → now it conducts and can be split.
🧲 A reverse battery
A battery turns a chemical reaction into electricity. Electrolysis is that film run backwards: you spend electricity to force a reaction that wouldn't happen on its own. That's why it always needs a power supply — the energy has to come from somewhere.
⚠️ Only ionic compounds, only d.c.
Covalent substances (like sugar or ethanol solution) have no ions, so they don't undergo electrolysis. And you must use direct current (d.c.) — a.c. would keep swapping the electrodes, so products would never settle at one side.
02 / 10 · 4.1 The electrolytic cell

The cell: anode, cathode, electrolyte — and how charge flows

Every electrolysis question is built from three parts. Learn these three names cold:

Anode — the positive (+) electrode
Cathode — the negative (−) electrode
Electrolyte — the molten / aqueous substance being split
Cathode (−) — reduction
Positive ions (cations) are attracted here. They gain electrons → they are reduced. Metals or hydrogen form at the cathode.
Anode (+) — oxidation
Negative ions (anions) are attracted here. They lose electrons → they are oxidised. Non-metals (other than hydrogen) form at the anode.
⚠️ Two mnemonics that never fail
OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). And "An Ox & Red Cat" — Anode = Oxidation, Reduction = Cathode. Also: CATions go to the CAThode.

How the charge actually moves (Extended)

Charge does a full loop, but it is carried by two different things in the two halves of the circuit — this distinction earns marks:

WhereWhat carries the chargeWhat happens
External circuit (wires)ElectronsFlow from the anode, through the power supply, to the cathode.
At the electrodesElectrons gained / lostCathode: ions gain e⁻ (reduction). Anode: ions lose e⁻ (oxidation).
In the electrolyteMoving ionsCations move to the cathode; anions move to the anode.
⚠️ The classic trap
Electrons never flow through the electrolyte. Inside the liquid, charge is carried only by ions drifting. Electrons travel only through the metal wires of the external circuit. Mixing these up is the most common lost mark in this topic.
✅ "Inert electrode" — define it
An inert electrode (platinum or carbon/graphite) just carries the current and does not react or take part in the reaction. We use inert electrodes whenever we want to see the electrolyte's own products.
03 / 10 · 4.1 Electrolysis of a molten compound

Molten lead(II) bromide — the cleanest case

With a molten binary compound (one metal + one non-metal, no water) there are only two ions to worry about, so prediction is automatic: metal at the cathode, non-metal at the anode.

Cathode (−)
Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb
A silvery bead of molten lead collects at the bottom electrode.
Anode (+)
2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻
Red-brown bromine vapour bubbles off at the anode.
⚠️ It has to be heated until molten first
Solid PbBr₂ won't conduct — the ions are locked. You heat it until it melts; only then can Pb²⁺ and Br⁻ move and the current flow. "Molten" is the whole reason the demo works.
✅ Predicting any molten binary compound
For a molten binary ionic compound: the metal ion → metal at the cathode, the non-metal ion → non-metal at the anode. e.g. molten NaCl → sodium at (−), chlorine at (+); molten Al₂O₃ → aluminium at (−), oxygen at (+).
⚠️ Build half-equations by balancing electrons
Cathode = gain e⁻ (electrons on the left): Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb. Anode = lose e⁻ (electrons on the right): 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻. The number of electrons must match the total charge on the ions.
04 / 10 · 4.1 Electrolysis in water (Extended)

Aqueous solutions: water joins the competition

💧 Water sneaks extra ions in
Dissolve a salt and you no longer have just two ions. Water itself supplies a few H⁺ and OH⁻ ions. So at each electrode there's now a competition: which positive ion grabs the electrons at the cathode, and which negative ion gives them up at the anode? Two simple rules decide the winner.
Cathode rule — the less reactive cation is discharged. If the metal is below hydrogen in reactivity (Cu, Ag, Au…) the metal forms. If the metal is more reactive than hydrogen (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe…) then hydrogen is given off instead.
Anode rule — a concentrated halide (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) is discharged as the halogen (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂). Otherwise — a dilute halide, or ions like SO₄²⁻ / NO₃⁻ — the OH⁻ wins and oxygen is given off.

Use the predictor to drill it: choose what's dissolved and read off the products and half-equations.

Why these win
At cathode (−)
Hydrogen
At anode (+)
Chlorine
✅ The decision order
Cathode: is the metal below hydrogen? Yes → metal. No → hydrogen. Anode: is a concentrated halide present? Yes → halogen. No → oxygen. Two yes/no checks answer every aqueous question.
05 / 10 · 4.1 Concentrated NaCl(aq)

Concentrated sodium chloride (brine)

Ions present: Na⁺, Cl⁻ from the salt, plus H⁺ and OH⁻ from the water. Apply the two rules:

Cathode (−) → Hydrogen
2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂
Na is far more reactive than H, so H₂ wins. Observation: bubbles of a colourless gas.
Anode (+) → Chlorine
2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
Concentrated chloride, so Cl₂ wins. Observation: bubbles of a pale green-yellow gas that bleaches damp litmus.
⚠️ What's left behind in the beaker
The ions not discharged — Na⁺ and OH⁻ — stay in solution. So the liquid that remains is sodium hydroxide, NaOH(aq). This is the industrial chlor-alkali process: it makes three useful products at once — hydrogen, chlorine and sodium hydroxide.
⚠️ Concentration changes the answer
This is for concentrated brine → chlorine. For very dilute sodium chloride, OH⁻ beats the scarce Cl⁻ and you get oxygen at the anode instead. Always read whether the solution is concentrated or dilute before you answer the anode.
06 / 10 · 4.1 Dilute H₂SO₄(aq)

Dilute sulfuric acid — really, electrolysis of water

Ions present: H⁺ and OH⁻ from water, plus SO₄²⁻ from the acid.

Cathode (−) → Hydrogen
2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂
Colourless gas bubbles — and twice the volume of the anode gas.
Anode (+) → Oxygen
4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂ + 4e⁻
SO₄²⁻ stays put, OH⁻ is discharged → colourless oxygen bubbles.
💧 You are just splitting water
H⁺ and OH⁻ both come from water, so the net change is 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂. You make hydrogen and oxygen in a 2 : 1 volume ratio — exactly water's formula. As water is used up, the acid that's left gets more concentrated.
✅ The 2 : 1 give-away
Twice as much gas at the cathode as the anode is the signature of electrolysing water (dilute acid). Hydrogen at (−), oxygen at (+), volumes 2 : 1. Test the gases: H₂ gives a squeaky pop; O₂ relights a glowing splint.
07 / 10 · 4.1 CuSO₄ & the master cell

Copper(II) sulfate — and why the electrode material matters

🔁 Same solution, two completely different anodes
With inert carbon electrodes you see the electrolyte's own reaction. Swap the anode for copper and the anode itself becomes the reaction — it dissolves. One solution, two outcomes, decided entirely by what the electrodes are made of.
Carbon (inert) electrodes
Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (pink-brown copper coats it).
Anode: 4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂ + 4e⁻ (oxygen bubbles).
Blue colour fades; solution turns acidic.
Copper (active) electrodes
Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (cathode gains mass).
Anode: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ (anode dissolves, loses mass).
Blue colour stays the same.
⚠️ Why the blue stays constant with copper electrodes
Every Cu²⁺ removed at the cathode is replaced by a Cu²⁺ dissolving off the anode. The concentration of Cu²⁺ — and so the blue colour — doesn't change. This is exactly how copper is purified on an industrial scale: impure copper anode → pure copper cathode.

08 / 10 · 4.1 Electroplating

Electroplating — a thin metal coat by electrolysis

✨ Why plate at all
Metal objects are electroplated to improve their appearance and to resist corrosion. Think of silver-plated trophies and cutlery, or gold-plated bangles and jewellery — a cheap base metal wearing a thin, shiny, protective layer of an expensive one.
How to set it up — three rules: the object to be plated is the cathode (−); the plating metal is the anode (+); the electrolyte is a solution of a salt of the plating metal.

To electroplate a steel spoon with silver:

Cathode (−)
The steel spoon.
Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag — silver deposits onto it.
Anode (+)
A bar of pure silver.
Ag → Ag⁺ + e⁻ — it dissolves to top up the solution.
Electrolyte
A silver salt solution (e.g. silver nitrate) — it supplies the Ag⁺ ions.
✅ The same logic as copper electrodes
Electroplating is just the "active electrode" cell from section 7: the anode dissolves at the same rate the cathode is coated, so the solution's concentration stays constant. Object always goes on the cathode — that's where positive metal ions are reduced and stick.
⚠️ Don't flip the electrodes
Put the object on the cathode, the coating metal on the anode — never the other way round. On the anode the object would dissolve, not get plated.
09 / 10 · 4.2 Hydrogen–oxygen fuel cells

Hydrogen–oxygen fuel cells

🔋 Electrolysis run in reverse
Electrolysis spends electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. A fuel cell does the opposite: combine hydrogen and oxygen and you get electricity back — with water as the only chemical product.
Hydrogen–oxygen fuel cell — a cell that uses hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity, with water as the only chemical product. Overall: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O.

Fuel cell vs petrol engine (Extended)

Advantages
• Only product is water — no CO₂, CO or NOx; no air pollution at the vehicle.
• More efficient — chemical energy → electrical energy fairly directly, fewer energy-wasting steps.
• Few moving parts, so quieter and less maintenance than an engine.
Disadvantages
• Hydrogen is hard to store and transport — very flammable, needs high pressure or very low temperature.
• Making the hydrogen often uses fossil-fuel energy (so pollution moves elsewhere).
• Fuel cells are expensive (costly catalysts) and refuelling stations are scarce.
🚌 On Indian roads
Hydrogen fuel-cell buses have been trialled in cities like Delhi as a clean alternative to diesel. The tailpipe emits only water vapour — the appeal — but the hydrogen still has to be produced, compressed and stored, which is the hard part.
✅ The one Core fact to nail
For a Core mark, state plainly: a hydrogen–oxygen fuel cell uses hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity, and water is the only chemical product. Then add the advantages/disadvantages for the Extended marks.
