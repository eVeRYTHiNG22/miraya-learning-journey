The Chemistry of Carbon: Fuels, Alcohols & Polymers

One element — carbon — bonds to itself in endless chains, and every organic compound you'll meet is just a carbon skeleton with a small reactive group bolted on. Learn the families (the homologous series) and their functional groups, and the whole topic collapses into a handful of patterns. Drag the series explorer to build molecules atom-by-atom, and tap through the oil-refinery column.

01 / 10 · The thread

Carbon builds families — learn the family, not every member

🧬 The one mental model
Carbon is unique: each atom forms four strong covalent bonds, including to other carbons, so it builds long chains and branches. That gives millions of compounds — but they aren't random. They sort into families (called homologous series), and within a family every member behaves almost the same because they all carry the same functional group — the small cluster of atoms that does the chemistry. Learn one member of a family well and you know them all.
🚂 A family is a train; –CH₂– is one more carriage
Picture each molecule as a train. The engine at the front is the functional group — it decides where the train goes (how it reacts). Behind it you add identical carriages, one –CH₂– unit at a time, to make the next, longer member. Same engine, one more carriage each step — that's why the whole family reacts alike but melts and boils at steadily changing temperatures.
✅ How Topic 11 fits together
Meet the toolkit first (11.1–11.2: formulae, functional groups, naming). Then where the carbon comes from (11.3 fuels & petroleum). Then the four key families in order of reactivity: alkanes (11.4, unreactive), alkenes (11.5, one double bond, reactive), alcohols (11.6) and carboxylic acids (11.7). Finally, joining small molecules into giant ones: polymers (11.8).
02 / 10 · 11.1 Formulae, functional groups & terminology

Four families, four general formulae, four functional groups

🔑 A functional group is the "business end"
A functional group is the atom or group of atoms that determines the chemical properties of a homologous series. The long carbon chain is fairly inert scaffolding; the functional group is where the reactions happen. Change the group and you change the family (and the name ending); lengthen the chain and you stay in the same family.

The four series you must know, each with its general formula and its functional group (here n = the number of carbons in the CₙHₓ chain):

SeriesGeneral formulaFunctional groupName ending
AlkanesCₙH₂ₙ₊₂none — all single C–C bonds-ane
AlkenesCₙH₂ₙC=C double bond-ene
AlcoholsCₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH–OH (hydroxyl)-ol
Carboxylic acidsCₙH₂ₙ₊₁COOH–COOH (carboxyl)-oic acid

For alkanes, alkenes and alcohols n is the total number of carbon atoms. For a carboxylic acid, the –COOH already holds one carbon, so n counts only the carbons in the CₙH₂ₙ₊₁ part — one fewer than the total (ethanoic acid CH₃COOH has 2 carbons but n = 1).

Structural formula

Saturated vs unsaturated

Saturated — a compound whose molecules have only single carbon–carbon bonds (e.g. all alkanes). "Full up" with hydrogen.
Unsaturated — a compound whose molecules contain one or more C–C bonds that are not single (e.g. the C=C in alkenes).

Structural isomers — compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural formulae (the atoms are joined up in a different order).

03 / 10 · 11.2 Naming organic compounds

Read the ending, know the family

🔤 The name is a code with two parts
An organic name is a stem (how many carbons) plus an ending (which family). The stem tells you chain length — meth- 1, eth- 2, prop- 3, but- 4. The ending tells you the functional group — -ane, -ene, -ol, -oic acid. So "propan-1-ol" instantly reads as: 3 carbons, an alcohol.

Read the two halves as independent lists — pick any stem and bolt on any ending. "prop-" + "-ene" = propene; "but-" + "-ol" = butanol.

✅ "State the type of compound"
Given a name, formula or displayed formula, name the family from the clue: ends in -ane → alkane, -ene → alkene, -ol → alcohol, -oic acid → carboxylic acid. Spot C=C → alkene, –OH → alcohol, –COOH → carboxylic acid in a drawn structure.
⚠️ Naming scope is unbranched, up to 4 carbons
For 0620 you name and draw unbranched alkanes, alkenes, alcohols and carboxylic acids up to four carbons (plus the simple esters). You are not expected to name long branched chains — don't panic over IUPAC rules beyond this.
04 / 10 · 11.3 Fuels

Petroleum — one mixture, split into useful fractions

⛽ Crude oil is a mixture, so we separate it
The fossil fuels are coal, natural gas and petroleum (crude oil). Natural gas is mostly methane. Petroleum is a mixture of hydrocarbons — compounds of hydrogen and carbon only — with chains of many different lengths. Because a mixture of chain lengths is useless as-is, we split it by boiling point using fractional distillation: short chains boil off easily and rise high; long chains barely evaporate and stay low.

The fractionating column — tap a fraction

The column is hot at the bottom, cool at the top. Vapours rise until they cool enough to condense, so each fraction (a group of similar chain lengths) collects at its own level. Tap a fraction to see its use and where it sits on every trend:

🏙️ Like sorting a crowd by height as they climb stairs
Imagine a mixed crowd climbing a very tall, warm staircase where it gets colder higher up. The light, energetic ones (short chains) sprint to the top floors; the heavy, sluggish ones (long chains) tire quickly and stop near the bottom. 05 / 10 · 11.4 Alkanes

Alkanes — the unreactive workhorses

🪵 Strong, saturated, and mostly bored
Alkanes have only single covalent C–C and C–H bonds and are saturated. With no weak spot for a reactant to attack, they are generally unreactive — with just two exceptions worth knowing: they burn (combustion), and in UV light they undergo substitution with chlorine.

Combustion

Complete combustion (plenty of oxygen) gives carbon dioxide and water — this is why alkanes are our main fuels:

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

methane burning completely

Substitution with chlorine (Supplement)

Substitution reaction — one atom (or group of atoms) is replaced by another. In an alkane, a hydrogen is swapped for a chlorine.

It's a photochemical reaction: ultraviolet light provides the activation energy, Ea. Limited to monosubstitution (one H replaced):

CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl

UV light · methane → chloromethane + hydrogen chloride

⚠️ No UV, no reaction — and it's substitution, not addition
Methane and chlorine can sit together in the dark forever. The reaction only starts with UV light (that's the "photochemical" part). And because a hydrogen is swapped out for a chlorine (releasing HCl), it is substitution — don't confuse it with the addition reactions of alkenes.
06 / 10 · 11.5 Alkenes

Alkenes — the reactive ones, thanks to C=C

⚡ The double bond is a spare bond ready to react
Alkenes contain a C=C double covalent bond and are unsaturated. That double bond is a reactive site: it can open up and grab new atoms onto the two carbons — an addition reaction. This one feature makes alkenes far more useful (and reactive) than alkanes, and it's the basis of both the bromine test and polymerisation.

Making alkenes — cracking

Cracking — breaking larger alkane molecules into smaller, more useful ones using a high temperature and a catalyst. It produces smaller alkanes, alkenes and sometimes hydrogen.
C₁₀H₂₂ → C₈H₁₈ + C₂H₄

a long alkane → a shorter alkane (petrol-sized) + ethene

⚠️ Why we crack
to turn less-useful long chains into short chains in demand (e.g. petrol) to make alkenes for polymers to make hydrogen

The test for unsaturation

✅ Bromine water tells saturated from unsaturated
Shake the hydrocarbon with aqueous bromine (bromine water). An alkene (unsaturated) decolourises it: orange → colourless. An alkane (saturated) leaves it orange (no reaction). This is a guaranteed exam favourite.

Addition reactions (Supplement)

In an addition reaction the C=C opens and only one product is formed:

+ Bromine
C₂H₄ + Br₂ → C₂H₄Br₂
gives 1,2-dibromoethane (the decolourising test).
+ Hydrogen (Ni)
C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆
a nickel catalyst; makes an alkane (hydrogenation).
+ Steam (acid cat.)
C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH
an acid catalyst; makes ethanol (hydration).
⚠️ Addition = ONE product; substitution = TWO
Because the double bond simply opens and takes the whole reactant on board, addition makes a single product with nothing thrown off. Contrast the alkane's substitution, which always spits out a second molecule (like HCl). "One product ⇒ addition ⇒ alkene."
07 / 10 · 11.6 Alcohols

Ethanol — two ways to make it, two ways to use it

🍶 One alcohol dominates the syllabus: ethanol
The alcohols carry the –OH functional group, and the star is ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH). You must know its two industrial routes, that it burns, and its two uses — as a solvent and a fuel.

Manufacturing ethanol — two routes

1 · Fermentation
Aqueous glucose + yeast, 25–35 °C, in the absence of oxygen.
C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂
2 · Catalytic addition of steam to ethene
Ethene + steam, 300 °C, 6000 kPa (60 atm), acid catalyst.
C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH

Combustion of ethanol

C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

clean, energy-rich — why ethanol is blended into fuels

Fermentation vs hydration — pros & cons (Supplement)

✅ The two uses of ethanol
State both plainly: ethanol is used as a solvent (it dissolves substances water can't) and as a fuel (it burns cleanly and releases energy).
08 / 10 · 11.7 Carboxylic acids

Ethanoic acid — a real acid that makes salts called -oates

🧴 It behaves like the acids you already know
Carboxylic acids carry –COOH. Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH) — the acid in vinegar — is a genuine acid, so it does the three classic acid reactions from Topic 7: with metals, bases and carbonates. The only new twist is the name of the salt: the -oate (an ethanoate).

The salt of ethanoic acid is a sodium/magnesium ethanoate — the acid's name ending -oic acid becomes -oate.

Making ethanoic acid by oxidising ethanol (Supplement)

Acidified KMnO₄
Warm ethanol with acidified aqueous potassium manganate(VII). It oxidises ethanol to ethanoic acid; the purple colour fades to colourless.
Bacterial oxidation
Bacteria oxidise ethanol during vinegar production — this is why an open bottle of wine slowly turns sour.

Esters — acid + alcohol (Supplement)

Esterification — a carboxylic acid + an alcohol, with an acid catalyst, form an ester + water.
CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH → CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O

ethanoic acid + ethanol → ethyl ethanoate + water

⚠️ Name the ester: alcohol part first, acid part second
An ester from ethanol + ethanoic acid is ethyl ethanoate — the "ethyl" comes from the alcohol, the "ethanoate" from the acid. The alkyl group from the alcohol is written first.
09 / 10 · 11.8 Polymers

Polymers — small molecules joined into giants

🔗 A polymer is a long chain of repeating links
A polymer is a large molecule built from many small molecules called monomers. Plastics are made from polymers. There are two ways to link the monomers: addition (alkenes zip together, losing nothing) and condensation (two different monomers join, kicking out a small molecule like water each time).

Addition polymerisation

Many ethene monomers open their C=C bonds and add together to make poly(ethene) — no other product is formed:

n CH₂=CH₂ → –(CH₂–CH₂)–ₙ

the bit in brackets is the repeat unit

✅ Deduce the repeat unit (Supplement)
From an alkene monomer, draw the repeat unit by opening the double bond into two single bonds sticking out of the brackets, and add the n. Reverse it to get the monomer back: a repeat unit –(CH₂–CHCl)– comes from the monomer CH₂=CHCl.

Condensation polymerisation (Supplement)

Two monomers, each with two functional groups, join and lose a small molecule (water) at every link:

Polyesters (e.g. PET)
A dicarboxylic acid + a diol → an ester linkage –CO–O–, losing water. PET is a polyester (its monomers can be recovered and re-polymerised — recycling).
Polyamides (e.g. nylon)
A dicarboxylic acid + a diamine → an amide linkage –CO–NH–, losing water. Nylon is a polyamide.
⚠️ Addition vs condensation
Addition: one monomer (an alkene), C=C opens, NO small molecule lost Condensation: two monomers, a small molecule (water) IS lost per link Ester linkage –CO–O– · Amide linkage –CO–NH–
Proteins are natural polyamides — condensation polymers built from amino acid monomers, which have the general structure H₂N–CH(R)–COOH (an amine group and an acid group on the same carbon).

Plastics & the environment

🌊 The trouble with a bond that never breaks
The very strength that makes plastics useful — unreactive, durable chains — means microbes can't digest them, so they don't rot away. Being non-biodegradable, discarded plastics pile up in landfill, drift into and accumulate in the oceans (harming marine life), and if burned they can release toxic gases. Their usefulness and their disposal problem come from the same property.
✅ The three environmental challenges of plastics
Quote all three: (1) disposal in landfill sites; (2) accumulation in oceans; (3) formation of toxic gases when burned.
