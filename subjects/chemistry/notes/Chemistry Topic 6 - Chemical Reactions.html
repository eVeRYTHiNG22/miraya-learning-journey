Chemical Reactions

Four questions about every reaction: does a new substance form (physical vs chemical), how fast is it (rate), can it go back (reversible & equilibrium), and which way do electrons move (redox). Tap the panels — steepen a rate curve, shift an equilibrium, and track oxidation numbers.

01 / 12 · 6.1 Physical & chemical changes

Physical or chemical? Did a new substance appear?

🔑 The one test that settles it
Ask a single question: is a new substance made? If the stuff is still chemically the same and you've only changed its state or form, that's a physical change. If new substances with new properties have formed, it's a chemical change. Everything else — reversibility, energy, colour — is just a clue pointing back to that one question.
🧊 Melting ghee vs frying it
Warm a spoon of ghee and it melts — cool it and it sets again. Same ghee throughout: physical. But leave that ghee on a hot tawa until it browns and smokes — new smelly compounds form that you can't turn back into fresh ghee. That browning is chemical. Melting is reversible and easy; the chemical change is not.
Physical change
No new substance. Only the state or form changes. Usually easy to reverse and involves small energy changes. Examples: melting ice, boiling water, dissolving sugar, magnetising iron, evaporating salt solution.
Chemical change
New substance(s) formed. Usually hard to reverse and involves larger energy changes. Common signs: a gas given off, a colour change, a precipitate, or heat taken in/given out. Examples: burning, rusting, cooking, neutralisation.
✅ Signs that suggest a chemical change
A new substance is the real definition, but examiners want the observable clues: a colour change, a gas produced (bubbles/fizzing), a precipitate forming, an energy change (temperature rises or falls), and it is usually not easily reversible.
⚠️ Dissolving is physical, not chemical
Stirring salt into water looks dramatic, but no new substance forms — evaporate the water and the salt comes straight back. Dissolving is a physical change. Likewise, boiling water makes steam, still H₂O — physical. Don't be fooled by a big visible change.
02 / 12 · 6.2 Rate of reaction — measuring it

Rate = how much changes, per second

Rate of reaction — how fast reactants are used up (or products formed). Practically: the amount of product formed, or reactant used up, divided by the time taken.
⏱️ Fast vs slow, made concrete
An explosion is a reaction finished in a fraction of a second; an iron gate rusting takes years. "Rate" just puts a number on that: measure something that changes as the reaction goes — the mass, the gas volume — and see how much it changes each second. A big change per second = a fast reaction.

Two practical ways to follow a reaction

You need a quantity that changes as the reaction proceeds. The syllabus names two standard methods:

1 · Change in mass
Stand the flask on a balance. If the reaction gives off a gas that escapes, the mass falls over time. Good for reactions making CO₂ (e.g. a carbonate + acid). Plot mass lost against time.
2 · Volume of gas produced
Collect the gas in a gas syringe (or an inverted measuring cylinder over water) and read its volume at set times. Plot volume of gas against time.
🧪 Marble chips and acid — the classic setup
Drop calcium carbonate (marble chips) into hydrochloric acid: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. The CO₂ bubbles away, so you can either watch the mass drop on a balance or catch the gas in a syringe. Both graphs tell the same story from opposite ends.
✅ Evaluating a method (Supplement)
Gas syringe — accurate volumes, but a very fast burst of gas can blow the plunger out or leak past it. Balance (mass loss) — simple and continuous, but a light gas like hydrogen has too little mass to weigh reliably, so mass-loss suits heavier gases like CO₂. Choose the method to fit the gas.
03 / 12 · 6.2 What speeds a reaction up

Five dials that change the rate

You can make almost any reaction go faster by turning one of these dials. Learn the effect first (Core); the why comes from collision theory in the next section (Supplement).

DialTurn it up →Effect on rate
Concentration of a solutionMore concentratedFaster
Pressure of gasesHigher pressureFaster
Surface area of a solidSmaller pieces / powderFaster
TemperatureHotterFaster
Catalyst (incl. enzymes)Add oneFaster
Catalyst — a substance that increases the rate of a reaction and is chemically unchanged at the end of the reaction. It is not used up, so a tiny amount keeps working. Enzymes are biological catalysts.
🌶️ Everyday examples of each dial
Concentration: strong acid attacks a metal faster than dilute. Surface area: a spoon of chilli powder flavours dal far faster than a whole dried chilli. Temperature: milk turns in a day on the counter but keeps for a week in the fridge. Catalyst: the enzymes in curd (yoghurt) culture set milk overnight.
⚠️ Surface area vs amount
Breaking a solid into smaller pieces increases surface area, not the amount of substance. 10 g of powder and 10 g of one lump react to give the same total product — the powder just gets there faster. More surface = faster rate, not more product.
04 / 12 · 6.2 Collision theory (Supplement)

Why the dials work — particles must collide, hard enough

💥 The whole theory in one line
Particles only react when they collide — and only if they hit with enough energy to react (at least the activation energy, Eₐ). So the rate depends on two things: how often particles collide (frequency), and what fraction of those collisions are energetic enough to work.
Collision theory — reaction rate depends on: the number of particles per unit volume, the frequency of collisions between particles, the kinetic energy of the particles, and the activation energy, Eₐ (the minimum energy a collision needs to lead to a reaction).

Every factor, explained the same way

Factor ↑Collision-theory reason
ConcentrationMore particles per unit volume → collisions are more frequent → faster.
Pressure (gases)Squeezing the gas puts more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → faster.
Surface areaMore particles exposed on the surface of the solid → more frequent collisions → faster.
TemperatureParticles move faster, so they collide more frequently and a greater proportion have energy ≥ Eₐ. Both effects speed it up.
CatalystProvides an alternative pathway with a lower Eₐ, so a greater proportion of collisions are successful → faster.
⚠️ A catalyst lowers the activation energy
Catalyst → decreases Eₐ → more collisions succeed It does not give particles more energy — it lowers the bar they must clear, so more of the collisions they already have are energetic enough to react.
⚠️ Temperature isn't just "more collisions"
For full marks on temperature you must mention the energy effect, not only frequency: at higher temperature a greater proportion of particles have energy ≥ Eₐ. This energy effect is the main reason a small temperature rise speeds reactions up so much — "they collide more often" alone won't get all the marks.
05 / 12 · 6.2 Reading rate graphs

The shape of a rate graph tells the story

📈 Steep then flat
Plot gas volume against time. The line starts steepest (most reactant, fastest rate), gradually bends over as reactants run low, then goes flat when the reaction has finished (a reactant is used up). Steepness of the line = rate. The height where it levels off = total amount of product.

How to read it
✅ The steepest point is always the start
The reaction is fastest at the beginning, where the concentration of reactants is highest — that's why the gradient is greatest at t = 0. As reactants are used up the rate falls, so the graph curves and flattens. A flat line means the reaction has stopped (a reactant has run out).
⚠️ Same amount of reactant → same final height
Concentration, surface area, pressure, temperature and a catalyst change how fast you get there, not how much product forms. If you keep the amount of limiting reactant the same, every curve plateaus at the same height — just sooner. (Using more reactant is the only way to raise the plateau.)
06 / 12 · 6.3 Reversible reactions

Some reactions run both ways — the ⇌ sign

Reversible reaction — a reaction that can go forwards and backwards. We show it with a special double arrow: ⇌ instead of →. Changing the conditions can switch which direction dominates.
💧 Add water one way, take it out the other
The two Core examples are both about water joining or leaving a salt. Heat the hydrated (water-containing) salt and you drive the water off; add water back to the dry (anhydrous) salt and you put it back. Same reaction, run in reverse — and each direction has a tell-tale colour change.

Copper(II) sulfate — blue ⇌ white

CuSO₄·5H₂O (blue) ⇌ CuSO₄ (white) + 5H₂O
Heat the blue hydrated crystals → water driven off → white anhydrous powder. Add water to the white powder → turns blue again (and gets warm — this direction is exothermic).

Cobalt(II) chloride — pink ⇌ blue

CoCl₂·6H₂O (pink) ⇌ CoCl₂ (blue) + 6H₂O
Heat the pink hydrated form → blue anhydrous form. Add water → back to pink.
✅ These are the standard tests for water
Anhydrous copper(II) sulfate turns from white to blue in the presence of water. Cobalt(II) chloride paper turns from blue to pink when water is present. Both only show that a liquid is water — to prove it's pure water you also check it boils at 100 °C / freezes at 0 °C.
⚠️ Which colour is which
Copper sulfate: hydrated = BLUE · anhydrous = white Cobalt chloride: hydrated = PINK · anhydrous = blue
07 / 12 · 6.3 Equilibrium (Supplement)

Closed system: a busy standstill

Equilibrium — a reversible reaction in a closed system is at equilibrium when (a) the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction, and (b) the concentrations of reactants and products are no longer changing.
🔁 Nothing stops — it just balances
Equilibrium is not the reaction "stopping". Both directions are still going full tilt — the forward reaction is making products exactly as fast as the reverse reaction turns them back. Because they cancel, the amounts stay constant. It is a dynamic balance, a busy standstill.
🚶 The up escalator you walk down
Imagine walking down an up-escalator at exactly the escalator's speed. You're working hard, the steps are moving, but your position never changes. That's equilibrium: lots of activity, no net change. A closed system is essential — if products could escape (an open flask), the reverse reaction couldn't keep up and there'd be no balance.
⚠️ Equal rates, not equal amounts
At equilibrium the two rates are equal — this does not mean there are equal amounts of reactants and products. The mixture might be 90% product and 10% reactant; what matters is that those amounts have stopped changing.
08 / 12 · 6.3 Shifting the position (Supplement)

Change a condition, and equilibrium fights back

⚖️ The system opposes the change
Disturb a system at equilibrium and it shifts to partly undo what you did. Heat it, and it shifts in the direction that absorbs heat. Squeeze it, and it shifts toward fewer gas molecules. Add more of something, and it shifts to use that up. Predicting the shift is the whole game.

⚠️ A catalyst never changes the yield
A catalyst speeds up the forward and reverse reactions equally, so it gets you to equilibrium sooner but does not move its position — the final amount of product is unchanged. It saves time, not yield.
09 / 12 · 6.3 Haber & Contact (Supplement)

Two industrial equilibria — the art of compromise

🏭 Yield vs speed vs cost
Both processes hit the same dilemma. The conditions that give the highest yield (low temperature) make the reaction too slow; and very high pressure gives more product but costs a fortune in strong equipment and is dangerous. Industry picks a compromise — a "good enough" yield produced fast enough, cheaply enough and safely enough.

The Haber process — making ammonia

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)  (forward reaction exothermic)
Sources: hydrogen from methane (natural gas); nitrogen from the air.
Conditions: 450 °C, 20 000 kPa / 200 atm, and an iron catalyst.

The Contact process — making sulfur trioxide (for sulfuric acid)

2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g)  (forward reaction exothermic)
Sources: sulfur dioxide from burning sulfur or roasting sulfide ores; oxygen from the air.
Conditions: 450 °C, 200 kPa / 2 atm, and a vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) catalyst.
✅ Why those conditions (the compromise argument)
Temperature (450 °C): the forward reaction is exothermic, so a low temperature would give a higher yield — but the reaction would be far too slow. 450 °C is a compromise: a reasonable yield at a workable rate.
Pressure: the forward reaction makes fewer gas molecules, so high pressure raises the yield. Haber uses a high 200 atm (yield matters, worth the cost). Contact already gives ~98% yield near normal pressure, so only 2 atm is used — high pressure isn't worth the expense.
Catalyst (iron / V₂O₅): speeds the reaction so equilibrium is reached faster; it does not change the yield but makes the process economic.
⚠️ Nail the numbers
Haber: 450 °C · 200 atm · iron Contact: 450 °C · 2 atm · vanadium(V) oxide Same temperature; the big difference is the pressure and the catalyst.
10 / 12 · 6.4 Redox — oxygen & oxidation number

Redox: oxidation and reduction, always together

Redox reaction — a reaction involving simultaneous oxidation and reduction. You can never have one without the other: if something is oxidised, something else must be reduced.
🅾️ Start with oxygen — the original meaning
The words come from oxygen. Oxidation = gain of oxygen; reduction = loss of oxygen. When carbon reduces copper(II) oxide, the copper loses its oxygen (reduced) while the carbon gains it (oxidised) — one reaction, both happening at once.
Oxidation
Gain of oxygen. Example: magnesium burning, 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO — the magnesium gains oxygen, so it is oxidised.
Reduction
Loss of oxygen. Example: 2CuO + C → 2Cu + CO₂ — the copper oxide loses oxygen, so it is reduced.

Oxidation number — the Roman numeral

Oxidation number — a number showing how oxidised an element is in a compound, written as a Roman numeral in the name. iron(II) = +2, iron(III) = +3, copper(II) = +2. It tells you which of an element's forms you mean.
✅ Identifying oxidation & reduction (Core)
Track the oxygen. In Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂: iron oxide loses oxygen → iron is reduced; carbon monoxide gains oxygen → it is oxidised. Both in one equation = a redox reaction.
11 / 12 · 6.4 Electrons, ox. numbers & agents (Supplement)

The deeper definition: it's about electrons

⚠️ OIL RIG — the two definitions in four letters
OIL — Oxidation Is Loss of electrons RIG — Reduction Is Gain of electrons And in oxidation numbers: oxidation = increase, reduction = decrease.
Oxidation
Loss of electrons, and an increase in oxidation number. e.g. Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ (0 → +2).
Reduction
Gain of electrons, and a decrease in oxidation number. e.g. Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (+2 → 0).

Oxidising & reducing agents

Oxidising and reducing agents

Oxidising agent — a substance that oxidises another substance and is itself reduced (it takes the electrons).   Reducing agent — a substance that reduces another substance and is itself oxidised (it gives the electrons away).
✅ The two colour-change tests
Acidified potassium manganate(VII), KMnO₄ is purple. A reducing agent turns it colourless (Mn goes from +7 to +2) — so decolourising KMnO₄ shows a reducing agent is present.
Potassium iodide, KI is colourless. An oxidising agent turns it brown as iodide is oxidised to iodine (I⁻ → I₂) — a brown colour shows an oxidising agent is present.
⚠️ The agent is named after what it does to the OTHER substance
An oxidising agent oxidises the other thing — so it is itself reduced. It feels backwards, but that's the rule: the agent's own change is the opposite of its name. Track the electrons and you'll never mix them up.
