Topic 7 — Acids, Bases & Salts

One-page revision reference. Everything in 7.1 – 7.3 (Core + Supplement): reactions, indicators, the pH scale, oxides, salt preparation, solubility rules and hydration. Green/violet tags mark Supplement-only content.

🧪 Acid reactions

7.1 · characteristic properties
acid + metal → salt + hydrogen

Mg + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂↑  (pop test)

acid + base → salt + water

2HCl + CuO → CuCl₂ + H₂O  (neutralisation)

acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂

2HCl + CaCO₃ → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑  (limewater test)

In water, acids release H⁺(aq) ions — that is what makes them acidic.

🔵 Bases & alkalis

7.1 · characteristic properties

Base = an oxide or hydroxide of a metal. Alkali = a soluble base. Alkalis release OH⁻(aq) in water.

base + acid → salt + water alkali + ammonium salt → salt + water + ammonia

NaOH + NH₄Cl → NaCl + H₂O + NH₃↑

That last reaction is the test for NH₄⁺: warm with NaOH → NH₃ gas turns damp red litmus blue.

📏 The pH scale

7.1 · universal indicator
0 2 4 6 7 9 11 14
← more acidicneutral 7more alkaline →
  • pH < 7 acidic — high H⁺
  • pH = 7 neutral (pure water)
  • pH > 7 alkaline — high OH⁻

⚖️ Neutralisation

7.1 · H⁺ + OH⁻

Aqueous acids contain H⁺; aqueous alkalis contain OH⁻. Neutralisation is the ionic reaction between them:

H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)

The salt's spectator ions stay in solution — only water is formed by this step.

💥 Strong vs weak acids Suppl.

7.1 · dissociation

Brønsted: acid = proton (H⁺) donor; base = proton acceptor.

Strong = fully dissociated

HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

Weak = partially dissociated

CH₃COOH(aq) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + CH₃COO⁻(aq)

Same concentration: a strong acid gives a lower pH (more H⁺) than a weak acid. "Strong" ≠ "concentrated".

🔥 Oxides

7.2 · classifying oxides

Acidic — non-metal oxides

SO₂ CO₂ — react with bases/alkalis → salt + water.

Basic — metal oxides

CuO CaO — react with acids → salt + water.

Amphoteric Suppl.

Al₂O₃ ZnO — react with both acids and bases to give a salt + water.

Trend: metallic character → basic; non-metallic → acidic; the borderline metals are amphoteric.

⚗️ Preparing soluble salts

7.3 · acid + …

A · Acid + alkali (titration)

Both soluble → titrate with an indicator to find the exact volume, repeat with no indicator, then crystallise. Used for Na / K / NH₄ salts.

B · Acid + excess metal

Add excess metal (e.g. Zn, Mg, Fe) to warm acid until fizzing stops → filter off excess.

C · Acid + excess insoluble base

Warm acid, add insoluble metal oxide/hydroxide until no more dissolves → filter off excess.

D · Acid + excess insoluble carbonate

As C, with effervescence (CO₂) until it stops → filter off excess.

Then (B–D): evaporate to the point of crystallisation → cool → filter crystals → dry between filter paper.

💧 Solubility rules

7.3 · general rules
  • All Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺ salts — soluble
  • All nitrates — soluble
  • Chlorides soluble except Ag, Pb
  • Sulfates soluble except Ba, Ca, Pb
  • Carbonates insoluble except Na, K, NH₄
  • Hydroxides insoluble except Na, K, NH₄ (Ca partially)

🌧️ Insoluble salts Suppl.

7.3 · precipitation

Mix two soluble solutions supplying the wanted ions → the insoluble salt precipitates → filter, wash, dry.

AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃ BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl

🔷 Hydrated & anhydrous

7.3 · water of crystallisation

Hydrated = chemically combined with water. Anhydrous = contains no water. Water of crystallisation = the water molecules held inside hydrated crystals. Suppl.

HydratedAnhydrous
CuSO₄·5H₂O blueCuSO₄ white
CoCl₂·6H₂O pinkCoCl₂ blue

Heating drives water off (reversible on adding water) — the basis of the test for water.

📌 Must-memorise

high-value marks
  • Neutralisation ionic eqn: H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O
  • Acid + metal → salt + H₂; + carbonate → + CO₂
  • Strong = fully dissociated (→); weak = partly (⇌)
  • Amphoteric = Al₂O₃, ZnO (react both ways)
  • Insoluble chlorides = Ag, Pb; insoluble sulfates = Ba, Ca, Pb
  • Titration → soluble-base salts; filtration methods → insoluble-solid excess

🎯 Common exam traps

avoid these
  • "Strong" acid ≠ "concentrated" acid — dissociation vs amount.
  • Choose the right prep: titration only when the base is soluble (no solid to filter); excess-solid + filter when it's insoluble.
  • Don't use very reactive metals (Na, K) with acid to make salts — too violent.
  • State excess reactant + filter step, or lose the method marks.
  • Give state symbols in ionic equations.
